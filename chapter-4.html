📖 THE EXAM MOMENT

You have submitted your letter. It is no longer in your hands. Somewhere, in an evaluation center, a person — not a machine, not an algorithm, a human being — picks up your letter and begins to read.

This person is trained. They have a scoring grid (Ch. 2). They know the register expectations (Ch. 3). They have evaluated dozens, perhaps hundreds, of letters before yours. They are professional, fair, and calibrated through standardization sessions with other examiners.

But they are still human.

And humans are subject to cognitive patterns — predictable ways that the brain processes information, forms impressions, and makes judgments. These patterns are not biases in the negative sense. They are the natural architecture of human cognition. Every examiner, no matter how experienced, is influenced by them.

This chapter teaches you what those patterns are and how to write a letter that works WITH them rather than against them. This is not manipulation. It is strategic communication — the same skill that makes a lawyer's closing argument persuasive, a diplomat's proposal compelling, or a doctor's explanation reassuring.

You are not tricking the examiner. You are making their job easy. And when you make the examiner's job easy, they reward you.

1️⃣ THE SEVEN COGNITIVE PATTERNS THAT INFLUENCE SCORING

Research in psycholinguistics, assessment theory, and cognitive psychology has identified consistent patterns in how human evaluators process written texts. Seven of these are directly relevant to TEF letter evaluation.

#PatternPlain Language DefinitionImpact on Your Score
1Primacy EffectFirst impressions disproportionately influence overall judgmentYour opening (header, salutation, first sentence) sets the tone for how everything after it is read
2Recency EffectLast impressions disproportionately influence final judgmentYour closing (final paragraph, closing formula) is what the examiner remembers when they assign scores
3Halo EffectA strong impression in one area creates a positive bias in other areasIf your structure and register are impeccable, the examiner is more forgiving of minor grammar errors
4Horn EffectA weak impression in one area creates a negative bias in other areasIf your opening is structurally broken (no salutation, no Objet), the examiner reads everything after it with suspicion
5Cognitive FluencyTexts that are easy to process are judged more favorablyClear paragraphing, logical flow, and familiar letter structure make the examiner's reading effortless — which correlates with higher scores
6AnchoringThe first strong signal the examiner encounters becomes the reference point for all subsequent evaluationIf the first thing the examiner notices is a perfect Objet line and salutation, their mental "anchor" is "competent writer." If the first thing they notice is Bonjour as a salutation, the anchor is "weak writer."
7Confirmation BiasOnce the examiner forms an initial impression, they unconsciously look for evidence that confirms itIf the anchor is "competent," they notice your connectors, your subjunctive, your vocabulary range. If the anchor is "weak," they notice your errors, your repetitions, your register breaks.

💡 The strategic implication is clear: Your first 30 words and your last 30 words carry disproportionate weight. A letter that opens and closes strongly can survive imperfections in the middle. A letter that opens or closes weakly must be exceptional in the middle to recover — and even then, recovery is incomplete.

2️⃣ THE PRIMACY EFFECT — HOW THE FIRST 10 SECONDS DETERMINE YOUR TRAJECTORY

What Happens in the First 10 Seconds

As described in Ch. 2 (the two-pass system), the examiner's first action is a visual scan. In approximately 10 seconds, they register:

  • The physical layout of the letter
  • The presence or absence of structural elements (header, Objet, salutation)
  • The approximate length
  • The visual cleanliness (paragraphing, handwriting quality or typing clarity)

This scan produces an initial classification in the examiner's mind. This classification is not a score — it is a category:

Initial ClassificationWhat Triggered ItConsequence
"This looks like a competent letter"Clean layout, visible header, Objet line, salutation, 3-4 paragraphs, closing formula visible at bottom, adequate lengthThe examiner enters the detailed read expecting to find competence. They are primed to notice strengths.
"This might have problems"Missing header elements, no Objet line, text appears as one block, very short, or messy presentationThe examiner enters the detailed read expecting to find weaknesses. They are primed to notice errors.
"This is not a letter"No letter structure at all — looks like an essay or a paragraph. No salutation, no closing formula visible.The examiner has already identified a fundamental RC and SOC failure before reading a single word.

The "10-Second Checklist" — What You Must Have Visible

Before the examiner reads your first word, these elements must be visually present:

ElementVisual PositionWhat the Examiner Sees
Sender blockTop-left area"The candidate knows letter format" ✅
Recipient blockBelow sender, right-aligned or left-aligned"Correct formatting" ✅
Date lineNear recipient block"Attention to convention" ✅
Objet lineBelow date, before salutation"The candidate has identified the purpose" ✅
SalutationStandalone line, followed by paragraph"Register awareness" ✅
Visible paragraph breaksWhite space between paragraphs"Organization is present" ✅
Closing formulaNear bottom of letter, as a distinct element"The letter is complete" ✅
SignatureBelow closing formula"Full letter structure" ✅

If all 8 elements are visible in the first scan, the examiner's initial classification is "competent." The primacy effect then works in your favor for the entire detailed read.

If 2-3 elements are missing, the classification shifts to "might have problems." The examiner is now looking for errors — and because confirmation bias ensures they will find what they look for, this initial shift can cost you points across ALL criteria, not just RC and SOC.

How to Exploit the Primacy Effect

Strategy 1: Write the frame before the content.

Before writing a single content word, set up your letter's visual structure:

Step 1: Write the sender block (top-left)
Step 2: Write the recipient block
Step 3: Write the date line
Step 4: Write the Objet line
Step 5: Write the salutation
Step 6: Leave space for 3 body paragraphs
Step 7: Write the closing formula (yes, BEFORE the body)
Step 8: Write the signature
Step 9: NOW go back and fill in the body paragraphs

🔴 This is counterintuitive but powerful. Most candidates write from top to bottom, running out of time or space before reaching the closing formula. By writing the frame first, you guarantee that the visual structure is complete even if your body paragraphs are slightly shorter than ideal. A structurally complete letter with slightly thin paragraphs scores HIGHER than a letter with rich paragraphs but no closing formula.

Strategy 2: Make your first content sentence exceptionally strong.

After the salutation, the examiner reads your first sentence. This sentence must:

  • Identify your purpose clearly
  • Use formal register unmistakably
  • Contain no errors

This first sentence is your verbal handshake with the examiner. A strong handshake signals confidence. A weak one signals uncertainty.

Weak First SentenceWhy It's WeakStrong First SentenceWhy It's Strong
"Je vous écris pour un problème."Vague, basic vocabulary, no specifics"Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de la commande n° 4521, dont la livraison a fait l'objet d'un retard considérable."Specific, formal register (je me permets de), detailed reference, formal vocabulary (a fait l'objet de)
"Bonjour, je suis Sophie et j'ai un problème avec mon appartement."Bonjour is not a salutation, self-introduction is informal, problème is vague"Je me permets de porter à votre attention les dégradations constatées dans l'appartement que j'occupe au 15, rue des Lilas, en tant que locataire depuis le mois de septembre 2023."Formal register, specific details, proper context-setting, no errors
"J'ai vu votre annonce et je veux postuler."J'ai vu is basic, je veux is direct/informal"Ayant pris connaissance de l'offre d'emploi parue sur votre site le 12 avril dernier, je souhaiterais vous présenter ma candidature au poste de chargé(e) de communication."Participial opening (Ayant pris connaissance), conditional politeness (souhaiterais), precise reference

💡 The first sentence is covered in full depth in Ch. 11. For now, understand its psychological function: it either confirms the examiner's "competent" classification (if the visual scan was positive) or begins to repair it (if the visual scan was neutral).

3️⃣ THE RECENCY EFFECT — HOW THE LAST 30 WORDS DETERMINE YOUR FINAL SCORE

What Happens in the Last 30 Seconds

After reading your entire letter, the examiner turns to the scoring grid. They must assign a number (0, 1, 2, or 3) for each of the five criteria. At this moment, their most recent memory is your closing — the final content sentence, the closing formula, and the signature.

If this closing is:

Closing QualityExaminer's Final ImpressionScoring Tendency
Perfect — complete formula, matching salutation, correct structure, no errors"This candidate knows what they're doing. The letter ended as strongly as it began."Examiner is inclined to round UP on borderline criteria. A CC that could be 2 or 3 gets pushed to 3.
Adequate — formula is present and mostly correct, minor imperfection"Acceptable ending. The candidate has the basics."Examiner scores according to the evidence — no positive or negative push.
Weak — formula is incomplete, mismatched with salutation, or informal"The letter fell apart at the end. The candidate may not really control formal register."Examiner is inclined to round DOWN on borderline criteria. A SOC that could be 2 or 3 gets pushed to 2. A VOC that seemed like a 2 gets re-examined.
Missing — no closing formula at all"The letter is structurally incomplete. This is a fundamental failure."RC drops (structural incompleteness). SOC drops (convention not respected). The negative impression colors ALL criteria.

The Closing Formula as a "Competence Certificate"

Think of the closing formula as the final stamp on your letter. In French administrative culture, the closing formula is not a nicety — it is a required structural element that signals the writer's mastery of formal conventions. Omitting it is equivalent to submitting an unsigned legal document. It invalidates the seriousness of everything that came before.

The examiner knows this. When they see a complete, correct, well-matched closing formula, they register: "This candidate understands French formal communication at a cultural level, not just a linguistic level." This is the highest signal you can send for SOC.

When they see an incomplete, mismatched, or missing closing formula, they register the opposite — and the recency effect ensures that this negative impression is the freshest one in their mind when they score.

How to Exploit the Recency Effect

Strategy 1: Pre-write your closing formula.

As mentioned in the primacy effect section, write your closing formula before filling in the body. This guarantees it exists, it is complete, and it is not rushed.

Strategy 2: Memorize 3 closing formulas — one for each scenario.

You do not need 20 closing formulas. You need 3 reliable ones that you can deploy without hesitation:

ScenarioReliable Closing Formula
Formal letter to unknown recipient (Madame, Monsieur,)"Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées."
Formal letter to titled recipient (Monsieur le Directeur,)"Je vous prie d'agréer, Monsieur le Directeur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées."
Semi-formal letter to named recipient (Madame Dupont,)"Veuillez agréer, Madame Dupont, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées."

These three formulas are universally correct, universally safe, and universally appropriate for TEF contexts. Ch. 34 will provide the complete bank with 20+ options, but these three alone will carry you through any exam scenario.

Strategy 3: The pre-closing sentence.

The sentence BEFORE the closing formula is also part of the recency zone. It should:

  • Summarize your position or expectation in one sentence
  • Use forward-looking language (dans l'attente de...)
  • Maintain perfect register
ScenarioEffective Pre-Closing Sentence
Complaint"Dans l'attente d'une réponse favorable de votre part, et restant à votre disposition pour tout renseignement complémentaire..."
Request"En vous remerciant par avance de l'attention que vous voudrez bien porter à ma demande..."
Suggestion"Espérant que cette proposition retiendra votre attention, je me tiens à votre entière disposition pour en discuter plus amplement."
Cover letter"Dans l'espoir que ma candidature retiendra votre attention, je reste à votre disposition pour un entretien à votre convenance."
Opinion letter"En espérant que ces réflexions contribueront utilement au débat..."

💡 The pre-closing sentence + closing formula together form the "exit sequence" of your letter. Memorize one pre-closing sentence for each letter type and one closing formula for each recipient type. Your exit sequence is then assembled from these two memorized components. This is covered in full depth in Ch. 32 and Ch. 34.

4️⃣ THE HALO EFFECT AND THE HORN EFFECT — HOW ONE STRENGTH (OR WEAKNESS) COLORS EVERYTHING

The Halo Effect: When Excellence Spreads

The halo effect occurs when a strong performance in one visible area creates a positive glow that extends to other areas. In TEF letter evaluation, this works as follows:

Visible StrengthHalo Extends ToHow It Helps
Perfect letter structure (header, Objet, salutation, paragraphs, closing)VOC and GRAM scoringThe examiner, already impressed by the structure, is more likely to interpret borderline vocabulary as "adequate" rather than "limited" and to view grammar errors as "occasional" rather than "frequent"
Flawless register (consistent formal language throughout)CC and GRAM scoringThe examiner, noting the candidate's sociolinguistic competence, assumes organizational competence and grammatical awareness
Sophisticated vocabulary (precise, varied, formal)GRAM and CC scoringRich vocabulary suggests an advanced learner; the examiner expects (and tends to find) correspondingly strong grammar and organization
Excellent connectors (varied, accurate, well-placed)VOC and SOC scoringStrong connectors signal a candidate who understands both linguistic and structural conventions

The strategic lesson: If you have one area of genuine strength, make it visible early. If your vocabulary is your best asset, front-load your most impressive vocabulary in the first paragraph. If your grammar is strongest, use a complex structure (participial opening, subjunctive, conditional) in the first sentence. The halo effect will extend your strength into areas where you might be merely adequate.

The Horn Effect: When Weakness Spreads

The horn effect is the opposite — a visible weakness in one area creates suspicion about all other areas.

Visible WeaknessHorn Extends ToHow It Hurts
Missing letter structure (no header, no Objet, no closing formula)ALL criteriaThe examiner assumes that a candidate who doesn't know letter conventions probably also has limited vocabulary, weak grammar, and poor organization
Register break (du coup, missing ne, ça)GRAM and CC scoringThe examiner, noting the register failure, reads subsequent grammar more critically and judges organization more harshly
Major grammar error in first sentenceVOC and SOC scoringAn early grammar error suggests the candidate is struggling; the examiner reads everything after it with heightened scrutiny
Off-topic contentCC, VOC, GRAM scoringIf the candidate has misunderstood the task, the examiner questions whether the organization, vocabulary, and grammar are truly purposeful

The strategic lesson: If you have a known weakness, bury it in the middle. The middle of paragraph 2 receives the least attention. The opening and closing receive the most. If your grammar is shaky, keep your first and last sentences simple and correct, saving complex structures for the middle where a minor error is less visible.

⚠️ This does NOT mean you should avoid complex structures. It means you should deploy them strategically. Use structures you are confident about in high-visibility positions (first sentence, last content sentence). Use structures you are less sure about in lower-visibility positions (middle of paragraph 2).

5️⃣ COGNITIVE FLUENCY — WHY EASY-TO-READ LETTERS SCORE HIGHER

The Science

Cognitive fluency is the ease with which the brain processes information. Research consistently shows that texts which are easier to process are judged more favorably — not because evaluators are lazy, but because fluent processing creates a feeling of clarity, competence, and coherence.

In the context of TEF evaluation, cognitive fluency means: when the examiner can read your letter smoothly, without stopping, re-reading, or puzzling over meaning, they perceive the letter as better organized, better written, and more competent.

What Creates Fluency in a Formal Letter

Fluency FactorWhat It MeansHow to Achieve It
Visual structureThe letter looks organized before it is readClear paragraphing, consistent spacing, visible structural elements (header, Objet, salutation, closing)
Predictable organizationIdeas appear where the examiner expects themContext in P1, argument in P2, solution/request in P3 — the standard structure that examiners are trained to find
Logical flowEach sentence leads naturally to the nextConnectors signal relationships; no jumps or gaps in logic
Sentence clarityEach sentence has one main idea, expressed clearlyAvoid overly long sentences (more than 30 words) that force the reader to hold multiple ideas in memory
Lexical accessibilityWords are precise but not obscureUse formal vocabulary that is natural, not archaic or hyper-specialized
Grammatical transparencyStructures are complex enough to show competence but clear enough to be parsed easilyThe subjunctive after bien que is complex but transparent. A triple-embedded relative clause is complex but opaque.

What Destroys Fluency

Fluency KillerWhat HappensExample
Run-on sentencesThe examiner must re-read to find the main clause"Ayant commandé un produit le 15 mars sur votre site Internet et ne l'ayant pas reçu dans les délais prévus malgré le fait que j'avais payé les frais de livraison express ce qui aurait dû garantir une livraison sous 48 heures, je me permets de vous écrire." — This is ONE sentence. It is technically grammatical. But the examiner must read it twice.
Missing connectorsThe reader must guess the relationship between sentences"J'ai commandé un produit. Il est arrivé en retard. Il était endommagé. J'ai appelé le service client. Personne n'a répondu." — Each sentence is correct, but the relationships (cause? consequence? addition? contrast?) are invisible.
Incorrect connectorsWorse than missing connectors — they actively mislead"J'ai commandé un produit. En effet, il est arrivé en retard." — En effet means "indeed/in fact" (confirmation). But the late arrival is a PROBLEM, not a confirmation. The examiner's processing is disrupted.
Vocabulary above the candidate's controlA misused complex word forces the examiner to guess the intended meaning"J'ai subi un préjudice conséquent vis-à-vis de votre manquement." — The candidate may mean "I suffered significant harm due to your failure," but conséquent is misused (it means "logical/consistent," not "significant" — the correct word is considérable). The examiner stops to process the error.
Ambiguous pronoun referencesThe examiner must figure out what il, elle, ce, or le refers to"J'ai contacté le service client au sujet du produit. Il n'a pas pu résoudre le problème." — Does il refer to the service client or the product? The examiner pauses.

How to Maximize Fluency

The "One Idea Per Sentence" Rule:

Each sentence should express one main idea. If you find yourself writing a sentence with more than two clauses, split it.

Low Fluency (One Long Sentence)High Fluency (Two Clear Sentences)
"Ayant constaté que le produit livré ne correspondait pas à la description figurant sur votre site, et ayant par ailleurs remarqué que l'emballage présentait des traces d'endommagement, je me suis immédiatement rapproché de votre service après-vente.""À la réception du colis, j'ai constaté que le produit livré ne correspondait pas à la description figurant sur votre site. De surcroît, l'emballage présentait des traces d'endommagement visibles. J'ai donc immédiatement contacté votre service après-vente."

The second version has the same information but is easier to process because each sentence has one main idea, and the relationships are made explicit through connectors (de surcroît, donc).

The "Paragraph Function" Rule:

Each paragraph should have one clear function. The examiner should be able to label each paragraph in one word:

  • P1 = Context
  • P2 = Argument
  • P3 = Request/Solution

If a paragraph contains both context AND argument, or both argument AND request, the examiner's processing is disrupted. They cannot classify it easily. Fluency drops. CC score drops.

The "Connector Bridge" Rule:

Every paragraph should begin with a connector or transitional phrase that signals its relationship to the previous paragraph. This creates a "bridge" that the examiner walks across smoothly.

Without Bridge (Low Fluency)With Bridge (High Fluency)
P1 ends. P2 begins: "Le produit était endommagé."P1 ends. P2 begins: "De surcroît, le produit présentait plusieurs dommages considérables."
P2 ends. P3 begins: "Je voudrais un remboursement."P2 ends. P3 begins: "En conséquence, je vous saurais gré de bien vouloir procéder au remboursement intégral de ma commande."

💡 Fluency is not about simplicity. It is about clarity. A complex sentence with clear structure is fluent. A simple sentence without logical connection to its neighbors is not fluent. The goal is organized complexity — not simplicity, not chaos.

6️⃣ ANCHORING AND CONFIRMATION BIAS — THE FIRST SIGNAL WINS

How Anchoring Works

The examiner's brain, like all human brains, uses the first strong piece of information as a reference point — an "anchor" — for all subsequent judgments. In TEF evaluation, the anchor is set by the first significant element the examiner processes.

First Element ProcessedAnchor SetConsequence
A clean, correctly formatted header with Objet line"Competent writer"Every subsequent element is evaluated against this positive anchor. Errors are seen as "exceptions." Strengths are seen as "confirmation."
A correct, register-appropriate salutation (Monsieur le Directeur,)"Register-aware writer"The examiner expects formal register throughout and is pleasantly confirmed when they find it.
A strong, specific first sentence with conditional politeness and formal vocabulary"B2+ candidate"The examiner's internal scale is now calibrated to B2. They evaluate everything that follows against a B2 standard — which means B2-level performance confirms the anchor, and minor errors below B2 are seen as anomalies.
"Bonjour" as a salutation"Weak register awareness"The examiner's internal scale drops. They now expect register problems throughout — and confirmation bias ensures they find them.
A grammar error in the first sentence"Grammar is a weakness"The examiner is now primed to notice grammar errors. They may notice errors they would have overlooked if the first sentence had been clean.

The Practical Strategy: Control Your Anchor

You control the anchor by controlling the first three elements the examiner encounters:

PriorityElementYour Strategy
1stVisual layoutComplete letter structure visible (primacy effect, covered above)
2ndObjet linePrecise, concise, correctly formatted (Ch. 9)
3rdSalutation + First sentenceCorrect salutation for the recipient type + strong first sentence with formal register markers

If these three elements are flawless, the anchor is set to "competent B2 writer." Everything that follows benefits from this anchor.

Confirmation Bias in Action

Once the anchor is set, the examiner unconsciously seeks evidence that confirms it. This is not unfairness — it is human cognition. Here is how it plays out:

Scenario A: Positive Anchor ("Competent Writer")

What the Examiner EncountersHow They Process It
A well-used connector (en conséquence)"Confirms my impression — this candidate knows how to organize ideas." (CC +)
A minor agreement error (les informations envoyé instead of envoyées)"A slip, but the overall grammar is solid. Probably an oversight." (GRAM: impact minimized)
A sophisticated vocabulary choice (dysfonctionnement instead of problème)"Confirms the candidate's lexical range." (VOC +)
A subjunctive used correctly (bien que je comprenne)"Expected from this level of candidate." (GRAM +)

Scenario B: Negative Anchor ("Weak Writer")

What the Examiner EncountersHow They Process It
A well-used connector (en conséquence)"Possibly memorized rather than genuinely mastered. Let me check if other connectors are used correctly." (CC: benefit of doubt reduced)
A minor agreement error (les informations envoyé)"Another grammar problem. This confirms my impression." (GRAM: impact amplified)
A sophisticated vocabulary choice (dysfonctionnement)"Possibly learned this word specifically for the exam. Let me check if the rest of the vocabulary matches." (VOC: benefit of doubt reduced)
A subjunctive used correctly (bien que je comprenne)"Surprising for this level. But let me see if it's consistent." (GRAM: credit given but cautiously)

🔴 The same letter, with the same content, can score differently depending on the anchor. This is not theory — it is a well-documented phenomenon in assessment research. Your job is to ensure that the anchor is always positive. The investment is small (a clean layout, a correct Objet, a strong first sentence), but the return is enormous.

9️⃣ THE EXAMINER'S INTERNAL MONOLOGUE — A SIMULATION

To make all of these psychological patterns concrete, here is a simulated examiner's internal monologue while reading two different letters responding to the same prompt.

The Prompt

You are a resident of the city of Bordeaux. A new construction project near your home is causing excessive noise from 6:00 AM to 10:00 PM. You write to the mayor to express your concern and suggest a solution.

Letter A — The Examiner's Experience

Visual scan (10 seconds):

"Let me see... No header. No recipient block. No Objet line. The text starts with 'Bonjour Monsieur le Maire.' One large block of text — no paragraph breaks. It's about... 150 words? Short. No closing formula visible at the bottom. Hmm. This might be challenging."

Anchor set: ⚠️ "Weak structure, possibly weak register."

Detailed read begins:

Bonjour Monsieur le Maire,

"'Bonjour' — not a formal salutation. This confirms my concern about register."

Je vous écris parce que y'a un chantier à côté de chez moi et ça fait beaucoup de bruit.

"'Y'a' — contracted informal. 'Ça' — informal demonstrative. 'Parce que' — basic connector. The register is courant at best, familier in places. SOC is looking like a 1."

Le bruit commence à 6h du matin et ça dure jusqu'à 10h du soir. C'est vraiment pas normal et je peux plus dormir. Du coup j'ai décidé de vous écrire pour que ça change.

"'C'est vraiment pas normal' — missing 'ne', informal expression. 'Je peux plus' — missing 'ne' again. 'Du coup' — informal connector. Three register breaks in two sentences. SOC confirmed at 1. GRAM affected by missing 'ne'. CC is weak — no connectors except 'du coup'."

🔟 THE "WHAT THEY REMEMBER" PRINCIPLE — THE THREE-ELEMENT MEMORY

The Science of Limited Recall

After evaluating dozens of letters in a session, the examiner does not remember your entire letter. They remember three things:

  1. The first impression (visual structure + first sentence)
  2. The most notable element (either the best thing or the worst thing in the letter)
  3. The last impression (closing formula + final sentence)

This is the serial position effect from memory research — people remember the beginning, the end, and the peak (or trough) of a sequence.

Strategic Implication: The Three Investment Points

Investment PointWhat to DoTime Investment
First impressionPerfect header, perfect Objet, perfect salutation, strong first sentence5 minutes at the start (planning + header writing)
Most notable elementEnsure your "peak" is positive — one impressive structure, one sophisticated vocabulary choice, one elegant connector usageDeliberately place your best material where it will be noticed (opening of P2, or the concession-refutation structure)
Last impressionPerfect pre-closing sentence + perfect closing formula + clean signature3 minutes at the end (or pre-written at the start)

💡 Total investment for maximum psychological impact: 8 minutes out of 60. These 8 minutes — spent on the opening, one impressive element, and the closing — have more impact on your score than 20 additional minutes spent perfecting the middle of paragraph 2.

The "Make the Examiner's Job Easy" Principle

This is the overarching principle that encompasses all cognitive patterns discussed in this chapter:

When you make the examiner's job easy, they reward you. When you make their job difficult, they penalize you — not intentionally, but inevitably, through the cognitive mechanisms described above.

Making their job easy means:

  • They can classify your letter instantly (clear structure)
  • They can identify your purpose instantly (clear Objet + first sentence)
  • They can follow your logic without re-reading (clear connectors)
  • They can evaluate your register without ambiguity (consistent formal language)
  • They can assign scores without agonizing (clear evidence for each criterion)
