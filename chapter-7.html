CHAPTER 7: THE RECIPIENT BLOCK & REFERENCE LINES — WHO YOU'RE WRITING TO

🎯 TEF Scoring Criteria Addressed in This Chapter

CriterionRelevance
SOC (Adaptation sociolinguistique)🔴 PRIMARY — Correct recipient formatting is a core letter convention
RC (Respect de la consigne)🟡 SECONDARY — Identifying and addressing the correct recipient is task compliance

📖 THE EXAM MOMENT

Your sender block is complete at the top-left of the page (Ch. 6). You have left 2-3 blank lines below it. Your pen moves to the next element — the recipient block (les coordonnées du destinataire).

This is where many candidates make their first significant formatting decision: Who exactly am I writing to, and how do I represent that on paper?

The sender block was about you. The recipient block is about them — and in French formal correspondence, how you represent the recipient reveals how well you understand the social relationship between writer and reader. A letter addressed to "Monsieur le Directeur, Société Électro-Plus, Service Client" communicates something fundamentally different from a letter addressed to "Électro-Plus." The first signals a writer who understands organizational hierarchy and addressing conventions. The second signals a writer who knows only the company name.

The recipient block is also the element that connects most directly to your salutation (Ch. 10) and your closing formula (Ch. 34). The name and title that appear in the recipient block must be consistent with the salutation and closing. Getting the recipient block right therefore sets up two subsequent elements correctly. Getting it wrong creates inconsistencies that ripple through the entire letter.

1️⃣ WHAT THE RECIPIENT BLOCK IS AND WHERE IT SITS

The Function

The recipient block answers the question: To whom is this letter addressed, and where should it be delivered?

In real correspondence, this information appears on the envelope and at the top of the letter. In TEF letters, it appears on the letter itself, positioned below and to the right of the sender block.

Physical Position

ConventionRule
Horizontal positionRight side of the page — aligned with the right margin or slightly indented from it
Vertical positionBelow the sender block, separated by 2-3 blank lines
AlignmentLeft-aligned within its own block (all lines of the recipient block start at the same position)

⚠️ Important note on positioning: In traditional French letter formatting, the recipient block is positioned on the right side of the page. However, in some modern French business correspondence (and in Quebec conventions), the recipient block may be positioned on the left side, below the sender block. Both conventions are acceptable on the TEF. The examiner is checking that the recipient block EXISTS and is CORRECTLY FORMATTED — not that it is on one specific side. When in doubt, use the right-side placement, as it is the most widely recognized French convention and creates the clearest visual distinction from the sender block.

2️⃣ WHAT GOES IN THE RECIPIENT BLOCK — THE FIVE POSSIBLE LINES

The recipient block can contain up to five lines, depending on the situation. Not all lines are always needed.

LineContentWhen to IncludeExample
Line 1Title + Name of the personWhen the prompt identifies a specific personMonsieur Jean DUPONT
Line 2Professional title / FunctionWhen the person holds a specific roleDirecteur du service client
Line 3Organization / Company / Institution nameWhen writing to an organizationSociété Électro-Plus
Line 4Street addressIn formal correspondence120, avenue des Ternes
Line 5Postal code + CityIn formal correspondence75017 Paris

The Hierarchy: Person → Function → Organization → Address

The lines follow a hierarchy from specific to general:

Most specific:    The individual person
                  ↓
                  Their role/title
                  ↓
                  The organization they belong to
                  ↓
Most general:     The physical address

This hierarchy ensures that the letter reaches the right person, not just the right building.

💡 For the TEF, the recipient's address is often unknown to you (the prompt rarely gives it). In this case, you have two options:

  1. Invent a plausible address — just as you did for the sender block (Ch. 6)
  2. Omit the address lines and include only the name/title/organization — this is acceptable and common in exam contexts

Option 2 is recommended when you are unsure. A recipient block with just the person's name, title, and organization is fully adequate for TEF purposes. The examiner will not penalize a missing recipient address if the person and organization are clearly identified. However, they WILL penalize a completely missing recipient block.

5️⃣ THE "À L'ATTENTION DE" LINE — WHEN AND HOW TO USE IT

What It Is

"À l'attention de" means "For the attention of." It is used when you are sending a letter to an organization but want it directed to a specific person or department within that organization.

When to Use It

SituationUse "À l'attention de"?Why
Writing directly to a named person❌ No — put the person's name on Line 1The letter is addressed to them directly; no routing needed
Writing to an organization, directed to a specific department✅ YesThe letter goes to the organization; the "À l'attention de" line routes it internally
Writing to an organization, directed to a named person within it✅ Yes (optional — you can also put the name on Line 1)Either format is correct
Writing to a government office🟡 OptionalCan be useful for large institutions with multiple departments

Format

[Organization name]
À l'attention de [title/name]
[Address]

Examples:

Société Électro-Plus
À l'attention du Directeur du service client
120, avenue des Ternes
75017 Paris
Mairie de Bordeaux
À l'attention du Service de l'urbanisme
Place Pey-Berland
33000 Bordeaux

Formatting Rules

RuleCorrectIncorrect
"À l'attention de" — capital À, lowercase remainderÀ l'attention de Monsieur DupontA L'ATTENTION DE MONSIEUR DUPONT
Followed by title or nameÀ l'attention du Directeur / À l'attention de Madame MartinÀ l'attention de: le directeur (no colon needed, article contracts with de)
Article contraction: de + le = du, de + les = desÀ l'attention du DirecteurÀ l'attention de le Directeur
Placed AFTER the organization name, BEFORE the addressSee examples abovePlacing it before the organization name or after the address

Common Confusion: "À l'attention de" vs. "À l'intention de"

ExpressionMeaningContext
À l'attention de"For the attention of" — routing instructionUsed in letter headers to direct the letter to a specific person
À l'intention de"For the benefit of / Intended for" — purpose statementUsed in sentences: "Un guide à l'intention des nouveaux employés" (A guide intended for new employees)

🔴 These are NOT interchangeable. Using "À l'intention de" in a letter header is a common error that signals confusion between two similar-looking expressions. Always use "À l'attention de" in letter headers.

💡 Memory trick: "Attention" = the letter calls for this person's attention (they need to read it). "Intention" = something was created with the intention of helping someone (purpose/goal).

6️⃣ REFERENCE LINES — "RÉF." AND "V/RÉF."

What Reference Lines Are

In formal French correspondence, reference lines appear below the recipient block (or between the recipient block and the date) to identify previous correspondence or administrative file numbers.

Reference LineMeaningWhen Used
V/Réf. :Votre référence — "Your reference"When the recipient previously sent you a letter with a reference number
N/Réf. :Notre référence — "Our reference"When you have your own file number or reference
Réf. :Référence — general referenceWhen referencing an order number, invoice number, contract number, etc.

When TEF Prompts Require Reference Lines

Most TEF prompts do NOT require reference lines. However, some prompts provide specific reference numbers that should appear in the letter:

Prompt DetailWhere It Goes
"Votre commande n° 4521"In the Objet line (Ch. 9) AND optionally as Réf. : Commande n° 4521
"En référence à votre courrier du 10 mars, réf. 2025-0342"As V/Réf. : 2025-0342
"Votre contrat n° AB-7890"In the Objet line AND optionally as Réf. : Contrat n° AB-7890

Format

                              [Recipient block]

V/Réf. : 2025-0342
N/Réf. : SM/2025/03

                              Toulouse, le 15 mars 2025

Practical Advice for the TEF

SituationRecommendation
The prompt gives a specific reference number (order #, contract #, file #)Include it as a Réf. line AND in the Objet line. This shows attention to detail.
The prompt mentions previous correspondence with a referenceInclude it as V/Réf. This shows you understand administrative correspondence conventions.
The prompt gives no reference numbersSkip reference lines entirely. They are not required when there is nothing to reference.

💡 Reference lines are a BONUS element, not a required one. Including them when appropriate demonstrates administrative sophistication. Omitting them when no reference exists is perfectly correct. Never invent a reference number — this would be confusing and pointless.

7️⃣ THE RECIPIENT BLOCK AND THE SALUTATION — THE CONSISTENCY RULE

The recipient block and the salutation (Ch. 10) must be consistent. The title or form of address used in the recipient block determines what appears in the salutation. Here is the consistency table:

Recipient Block (Line 1)Salutation Must BeWhy
Monsieur Jean DUPONT / Directeur du service clientMonsieur le Directeur, or Monsieur,You address the person by their function or by Monsieur — not by first name
Madame Isabelle MERCIER / PropriétaireMadame, or Madame Mercier, (semi-formal)Madame alone is formal; adding the last name is semi-formal
Monsieur le Maire de BordeauxMonsieur le Maire,The title from the recipient block echoes in the salutation
Société Électro-Plus / Service clientMadame, Monsieur,No specific person → use the double salutation for unknown gender
Service des ressources humaines / Entreprise DataCorpMadame, Monsieur,Same — no specific person identified
Monsieur le Rédacteur en chefMonsieur le Rédacteur en chef, or Monsieur,Title echoes in salutation

🔴 The critical rule: Whatever title or form of address you use in the recipient block, it must reappear (or be compatible with) both the salutation AND the closing formula. This is the three-point consistency chain:

Recipient Block ↔ Salutation ↔ Closing Formula

If the recipient block says Monsieur le Directeur, the salutation must say Monsieur le Directeur, and the closing must say ...agréer, Monsieur le Directeur, l'expression de...

Breaking this chain at any point is a SOC error that the examiner will notice immediately.

The Three-Point Consistency Chain — Examples

Example 1: Letter to a School Director (Male)

SalutationMonsieur le Directeur,✅ Echoes recipient block
Closing Formula...agréer, Monsieur le Directeur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.✅ Echoes salutation

Example 2: Letter to an Unknown Recipient at a Company

ElementContentConsistency Check
Recipient BlockSociété Électro-Plus / Service client✅ No specific person → double salutation needed
SalutationMadame, Monsieur,✅ Double form for unknown gender
Closing Formula...agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.✅ Echoes salutation

Example 3: Letter to a Named Female Mayor

ElementContentConsistency Check
Recipient BlockMadame la Maire de Bordeaux / Mairie de Bordeaux✅ Title: Madame la Maire
SalutationMadame la Maire,✅ Echoes recipient block
Closing Formula...agréer, Madame la Maire, l'expression de mes respectueuses salutations.✅ Echoes salutation

Broken Chain — What Goes Wrong:

ElementContentProblem
Recipient BlockMonsieur le DirecteurTitle: Directeur
SalutationMonsieur,⚠️ Dropped the title — not technically wrong, but loses the specificity
Closing Formula...agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de...🔴 WRONG — Closing says Madame, Monsieur but salutation said Monsieur only. Gender mismatch + form mismatch.

Situation 1: You Don't Know the Recipient's Gender

Prompt: "Vous écrivez au directeur du service client." (No Monsieur or Madame specified)

Options:

Situation 4: The Prompt Gives Minimal Information

You know only that the recipient is a landlord. No name, no gender, no address.

Recipient Block (minimal but acceptable):

                              Le Propriétaire de l'immeuble
                              [your building address]

Salutation: Madame, Monsieur,

💡 When the prompt gives minimal recipient information, your two strategies are: (1) keep it minimal and correct, or (2) invent plausible details for a more complete block. Both are acceptable. Strategy (1) is safer because you cannot make errors on information that does not exist. Strategy (2) is more impressive if executed correctly.

Situation 5: The Prompt Gives a Specific Person and Organization

Prompt: "Vous écrivez à Madame Claire Fontaine, directrice des ressources humaines de l'entreprise InnoTech."

This is the easiest case — everything is provided:

                              Entreprise InnoTech
                              [invented address]

Salutation: Madame la Directrice, or Madame,

⚠️ Never use the first name in the salutation. Even though the prompt tells you her name is Claire, your salutation is "Madame," or "Madame la Directrice," — NEVER "Madame Claire," or "Claire." First names in salutations are reserved for personal letters to friends and family, which are outside the TEF formal letter scope.

🔟 WHAT COULD GO WRONG — RECIPIENT BLOCK ERRORS

Error 1: Omitting the Recipient Block Entirely

How common: Common (25-35% of candidates)

Why it happens: Candidates focus on the letter body and forget the header elements. Or they were never taught that French letters include recipient identification in the letter itself (not just on the envelope).

Impact: SOC − (missing convention), RC − (incomplete format). Combined with a missing sender block, this creates a letter that looks like an essay with a salutation — not a letter.

Fix: The recipient block is part of your Phase 2 writing sequence (Ch. 5/Ch. 6). Write it immediately after the sender block, before anything else. Make it automatic.

Error 2: Addressing the Wrong Recipient

How common: Rare but catastrophic

What it looks like: The prompt says to write to the directeur du service client, but the candidate addresses the letter to the directeur général or to the service technique.

Impact: RC − (task completion failure — you wrote to the wrong person). The examiner checks whether you identified the correct recipient.

Fix: Step 1 of your planning method (Ch. 5) extracts the recipient. Copy the recipient information from the prompt EXACTLY. Do not modify, generalize, or substitute.

Error 3: Using "Mr." Instead of "Monsieur" or "M."

How common: Very common among English-speaking candidates

Why it's wrong: "Mr." is English. The French abbreviation is "M." (with a period, no "r"). Better yet, write "Monsieur" in full — it is the most formal option.

LanguageAbbreviationFull Form
EnglishMr. / Mrs. / Ms.Mister / Missus / Ms
FrenchM. / MmeMonsieur / Madame

🔴 Never write Mrs. or Ms. in a French letter. The French equivalent for all women is Mme (abbreviation) or Madame (full form). There is no French equivalent of Ms. because Madame already serves this function (it does not indicate marital status in modern usage).

Error 4: Using the First Name in the Recipient Block Without the Last Name

What it looks like:

                              Madame Claire
                              Directrice des ressources humaines

Why it's wrong: Using a first name without a last name is informal and incomplete. In formal correspondence, either use the full name (Madame Claire FONTAINE) or omit the name and use only the title (Madame la Directrice).

Fix: Full name or title only. Never first name alone.

Error 5: Inconsistency Between Recipient Block and Salutation

What it looks like:

Recipient block: Monsieur le Directeur
Salutation: Madame, Monsieur,

Why it's wrong: The recipient block identifies a male director. The salutation then uses the double form as if the gender is unknown. This inconsistency signals carelessness.

Fix: Apply the three-point consistency chain (Section 7 of this chapter). After writing both elements, verify that the title/form of address matches.

Error 6: Confusing "À l'attention de" and "À l'intention de"

How common: Occasional (10-15%)

Impact: VOC − (incorrect expression), SOC − (signals unfamiliarity with formal correspondence vocabulary)

Fix: See Section 5 of this chapter. Attention = routing. Intention = purpose. In letter headers, always attention.

1️⃣4️⃣ THE 90-SECOND RECIPIENT BLOCK ROUTINE

On exam day, writing the recipient block should take no more than 90 seconds. Here is the exact routine:

STEP A [15 seconds]: Identify the recipient from your planning sheet
  → Person named? Title given? Organization given?
  → Gender known? Unknown?

STEP B [15 seconds]: Decide on the format
  → Named person: Name + Title + Organization + Address
  → Department only: Department + Organization + Address
  → Organization only: Organization + Address
  → Unknown gender: Use double form in salutation (note for later)

STEP C [45 seconds]: Write the recipient block
  → Line 1: Title + Name (or function title)
  → Line 2: Role/Department (if not on Line 1)
  → Line 3: Organization name
  → Lines 4-5: Address (invent if necessary)

STEP D [15 seconds]: Verify consistency
  → Does this recipient block match the salutation I planned in Step 4 of Ch. 5?
  → If yes: proceed to date line
  → If no: adjust either the recipient block or the planned salutation

Total: ~90 seconds. The recipient block is complete.
