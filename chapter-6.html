🎯 TEF Scoring Criteria Addressed in This Chapter

📖 THE EXAM MOMENT

Your 5-minute plan is complete (Ch. 5). The grid is filled. Your salutation, first sentence, and closing formula are pre-decided. You are ready to write.

But you do not start with your first sentence. You do not start with the salutation. You start at the very top of the page — the sender block (les coordonnées de l'expéditeur).

This is the first physical mark you make on the page. It is the first thing the examiner sees during their visual scan (Ch. 4: primacy effect). And for many candidates, it is the first thing they get wrong — not because it is difficult, but because they have never been taught the French conventions for sender information in a formal letter.

In English, the sender block is often optional in personal letters and varies widely in business letters. In French, the sender block follows specific formatting rules that are consistent across all formal correspondence. Getting it right takes 30 seconds and signals immediate competence. Getting it wrong — or omitting it entirely — signals that the candidate does not understand French letter conventions.

This chapter teaches you exactly what goes in the sender block, how to format it, and how to handle the specific challenges that TEF prompts create.

1️⃣ WHAT THE SENDER BLOCK IS AND WHY IT EXISTS

The Function

The sender block answers one question for the recipient: Who is writing to me, and how can I reach them?

In real-world French correspondence, this is practical — the recipient needs to know where to send their reply. In the TEF exam, this is conventional — the examiner needs to see that you know a formal letter includes sender identification at the top.

The Cultural Convention

In French formal letters, the sender block sits at the top-left of the page. This is not arbitrary — it reflects the physical reality of French postal correspondence. When a letter is folded and placed in an envelope with a window (enveloppe à fenêtre), the recipient's address is visible through the window. The sender's address, being at the top-left, appears on the back of the folded letter or on the envelope itself.

Even though TEF letters are not mailed, the convention persists because it is the expected visual format. The examiner, upon scanning the top of your letter, expects to see sender information at the top-left. Its presence signals: "This candidate knows how French letters are structured."

What the Examiner Registers

Present, minor formatting errors"The candidate attempted the convention. Acceptable."SOC neutral — no penalty, no bonus

4️⃣ WHEN THE PROMPT GIVES YOU AN IDENTITY vs. WHEN IT DOES NOT

This is a source of confusion for many candidates. TEF prompts vary in how much identity information they provide.

Scenario A: Full Identity Provided

"Vous êtes Marie Dupont, cliente de la société TéléPlus. Vous habitez au 15, rue de la République, 75003 Paris."

What you do: Use this information exactly.

Marie DUPONT
15, rue de la République
75003 Paris

Do NOT change the name, address, or city. The prompt has established your character. Changing these details contradicts the scenario.

Scenario B: Partial Identity Provided

"Vous êtes parent d'élève au collège Jean-Moulin de Toulouse."

What you do: Use the city from the prompt (Toulouse). Invent a name and address.

Laurent MOREAU
7, rue des Écoles
31000 Toulouse

Key rule: The city MUST match the prompt. If the prompt says Toulouse, your address must be in Toulouse. Inventing a Paris address for a Toulouse scenario is a task completion inconsistency.

Scenario C: Minimal Identity Provided

"Vous avez commandé un produit sur le site Internet de la société Électro-Plus."

What you do: The prompt gives you no name, no city, no address. You invent everything.

Claire DUBOIS
22, avenue des Champs
69003 Lyon

The only constraint: Make it plausible and French. Do not spend more than 10 seconds deciding.

Scenario D: Professional Identity Provided

"Vous êtes employé(e) dans une entreprise de marketing à Montréal."

What you do: You may include your professional title in the sender block if relevant:

Yasmine KHALIL
Chargée de communication
Société MarketPro
500, rue Sherbrooke Ouest
Montréal (Québec) H3A 3C6

💡 Including a professional title in the sender block is appropriate ONLY for cover letters and professional correspondence where your role is relevant. For a consumer complaint, you are writing as an individual, not as a professional. Your sender block should show your personal identity, not your job title.

5️⃣ FORMATTING CONVENTIONS — ALIGNMENT, SPACING, AND PUNCTUATION

Alignment

ConventionRule
PositionTop-left corner of the page
AlignmentLeft-aligned (all lines start at the same left margin)
MarginStart at the normal left margin of the page — no indentation

Spacing

ConventionRule
Between linesSingle-spaced (no extra space between lines of the sender block)
After sender blockLeave 2-3 blank lines before the recipient block

Punctuation

ConventionRuleExample
No period at the end of address linesAddress lines are not sentences — they do not end with periods45, rue des Lilas ✅ / 45, rue des Lilas. ❌
Comma after street numberSeparates number from street name45*,** rue des Lilas*
No comma between postal code and cityFrench convention31000 Toulouse ✅ / 31000, Toulouse ❌
Accents on capital lettersFrench requires accents on capitalsÉmilie ✅ / Emilie ❌ (missing accent on É)

🔴 The accent on capital letters rule is important and often violated. In French, accents are REQUIRED on capital letters: À, É, È, Ê, Ô, Î, Ù, Ç. Writing EMILIE LEFEVRE instead of ÉMILIE LEFÈVRE is technically a spelling error. On paper, make the effort to add accents to capitals. On computer, use the appropriate key combinations.

7️⃣ SPECIAL CASES AND COMPLICATIONS

Case 1: The Prompt Gives You a Role but No Personal Details

"Vous êtes le/la président(e) d'une association de quartier."

Solution: Invent a name AND include the role:

Fatima BENALI
Présidente de l'Association
  des Résidents du Quartier Saint-Michel
12, rue de la Liberté
33000 Bordeaux

Note: The association name can flow onto a second line with indentation.

Case 2: You Are Writing on Behalf of a Group

"Vous écrivez au nom des résidents de votre immeuble."

Solution: The sender block shows YOUR identity (you are the one writing), but your first paragraph will clarify that you represent a group. The sender block does not change.

Ahmed KHELIFI
18, avenue Jean-Jaurès
13001 Marseille

Then in P1: "Au nom des résidents de l'immeuble situé au 18, avenue Jean-Jaurès, je me permets de vous écrire..."

Case 3: Cover Letter with Current Employer

"Vous travaillez actuellement chez InfoTech et vous souhaitez postuler chez DataCorp."

Solution: Your sender block shows your PERSONAL information, not your current employer's. You are applying as an individual, not as a representative of your current company.

Julie MOREAU
30, rue du Commerce
44000 Nantes
Tél. : 06 98 76 54 32
Courriel : j.moreau@courriel.fr

Your current employment is mentioned in the BODY of the letter, not in the header.

Case 4: You Cannot Remember a Plausible Postal Code

Solution: Invent one. The examiner will not verify it. A 5-digit number starting with the correct regional prefix is ideal, but any 5-digit number is acceptable.

CityReal PrefixSafe Invention
Paris7575001, 75008, 75012
Lyon6969001, 69003
Marseille1313001, 13008
Toulouse3131000, 31200
Bordeaux3333000, 33100
MontréalH__ ___H2X 1Y4 (a common format)

💡 If you truly cannot remember any postal code, write just the city name without a code. This is a minor formatting imperfection, not a scoring disaster. The examiner will note the absence but will not significantly penalize it. A missing postal code is far less damaging than a missing sender block entirely.

Case 5: Computer-Based Exam — No Precise Positioning

On a computer, you cannot position text precisely in the top-left. Solution: Simply type the sender block as the first lines of your document, left-aligned. The examiner understands the constraints of computer-based formatting.

Sophie MARTIN
45, rue des Lilas
31000 Toulouse

[blank line]

[Recipient block follows]

The blank line between sender and recipient blocks is sufficient to signal the separation.

8️⃣ WHAT COULD GO WRONG — SENDER BLOCK ERRORS

Error 1: Omitting the Sender Block Entirely

How common: Very common (30-40% of candidates)

Why it happens: Candidates focus on the content and forget that a letter has a header. Or they were never taught that French formal letters include sender information.

Impact: SOC − (missing structural convention), RC − (incomplete letter format). The examiner's first visual scan fails to find sender information → negative anchor.

Fix: Always write the sender block FIRST, before anything else. It is Step 1 of your writing phase. Make it automatic.

Error 2: Putting the Sender Block on the Right

How common: Occasional (10-15%)

Why it happens: In some English letter formats, the sender's address goes on the right. Candidates transfer this convention to French.

Impact: Minor SOC concern — the French convention is top-left. However, this is a relatively minor formatting error. The examiner will note it but is unlikely to penalize heavily if all other elements are correct.

Fix: Sender = left. Recipient = right (or left, below sender, depending on convention — Ch. 7). Memorize: Sender = top-left. Always.

Error 3: Including Irrelevant Information

How common: Rare

What it looks like:

Sophie MARTIN
Née le 15 avril 1985
Nationalité française
45, rue des Lilas
31000 Toulouse

Why it's wrong: Date of birth and nationality do not belong in a letter sender block. This is not a CV header. Include only: name, address, and optionally phone/email.

Fix: The sender block contains contact information — how to identify and reach you. Personal data (date of birth, nationality, marital status) never appears here.

Error 4: Using Informal Email Addresses

How common: Occasional

What it looks like:

Sophie MARTIN
45, rue des Lilas
31000 Toulouse
Courriel : sophiecool92@gmail.com

Why it's problematic: In a formal letter context, the email address should appear professional. "sophiecool92" breaks the formal register.

Fix: Either use a professional-looking email (s.martin@courriel.fr) or omit the email line entirely. Since TEF letters are not actually sent, the email line is optional. If you include one, make it plausible and neutral.

Error 5: Inconsistency Between Sender Block and Letter Content

How common: Occasional

What it looks like: The sender block says "Sophie MARTIN, 45, rue des Lilas, Toulouse" but the letter body says "En tant que résidente de Bordeaux depuis cinq ans..."

Why it's wrong: The city in your address must match the city referenced in your letter content. If the prompt places you in Bordeaux, your sender address must be in Bordeaux.

Fix: During planning (Ch. 5, Step 1), note the city from the prompt. Use this city consistently in your sender block AND your letter body.

9️⃣ THE 60-SECOND SENDER BLOCK ROUTINE

On exam day, writing the sender block should take no more than 60 seconds. Here is the exact routine:

STEP A [10 seconds]: Check the prompt for name and location
  → Name given? Use it. Not given? Invent one.
  → City given? Use it. Not given? Choose a major French city.

STEP B [10 seconds]: Write Line 1 (Name)
  → First name + LAST NAME in capitals
  → Example: Sophie MARTIN

STEP C [20 seconds]: Write Lines 2-3 (Address)
  → Invent a street number + street name
  → Write postal code + city
  → Example: 45, rue des Lilas
              31000 Toulouse

STEP D [10 seconds]: Decide on Line 4 (Contact — optional)
  → If relevant to the scenario: add phone or email
  → If not: skip. Move on.

STEP E [10 seconds]: Leave 2-3 blank lines below
  → This space separates the sender block from the recipient block

Total: ~60 seconds. The sender block is complete.

Sophie Martin

  • No street number
  • No postal code
  • City and street on the same line

  • Street number added with comma

  • Clean, scannable format

The difference is small in terms of effort — perhaps 15 additional seconds of writing. But the difference in examiner impression is significant: the "before" version looks uncertain; the "after" version looks competent.

1️⃣2️⃣ THE SENDER BLOCK AND THE PLANNING METHOD — WHERE IT FITS

In Ch. 5, you learned the 5-step planning method. The sender block is written AFTER planning but BEFORE any content. Here is where it sits in the complete writing sequence:

PHASE 1: PLANNING [5 minutes]
  → Steps 1-5 from Ch. 5
  → Result: completed planning grid on scrap paper

PHASE 2: HEADER WRITING [3-4 minutes]
  → Write sender block ← YOU ARE HERE (Ch. 6)
  → Write recipient block (Ch. 7)
  → Write date line (Ch. 8)
  → Write Objet line (Ch. 9)

PHASE 3: BODY WRITING [35-40 minutes]
  → Write salutation (Ch. 10)
  → Write first sentence + P1 (Ch. 11, 13-18)
  → Write P2 (Ch. 19-24)
  → Write P3 (Ch. 26-30)
  → Write pre-closing (Ch. 32)

PHASE 4: CLOSING [2-3 minutes]
  → Write closing formula (Ch. 33-34) — or copy from plan
  → Write signature (Ch. 35)

PHASE 5: EDITING [5-8 minutes]
  → Edit and verify (Ch. 42)

The sender block is the first element of Phase 2. It takes 60 seconds. After it, you move directly to the recipient block (Ch. 7).

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY — WHAT THIS CHAPTER TEACHES THAT NO OTHER CHAPTER COVERS

Chapter 6 is the only chapter that teaches you how to present your identity in a French formal letter.

This may seem like a small, mechanical element — and it is. But small mechanical elements, when executed correctly, create the foundation of the examiner's positive first impression (Ch. 4). The sender block is the very first thing written and the very first thing seen. Its presence and correct formatting contribute to the "competent letter" initial classification that triggers the halo effect.

What you should be able to do after this chapter:

  • Write a correctly formatted sender block in under 60 seconds
  • Handle all four prompt scenarios (full identity, partial identity, minimal identity, professional identity)
  • Format a French address correctly (number + comma + street, postal code + city, no final periods)
  • Know when to include and when to omit Line 4 (contact information)
  • Use the correct French terms (Tél., Courriel) instead of anglicisms
  • Capitalize last names and accented capital letters correctly
  • Position the sender block correctly on the page (top-left)

The single sentence summary: The sender block is 60 seconds of writing that establishes the first visual signal of competence — a small investment that activates the primacy effect and sets a positive anchor for everything that follows.
