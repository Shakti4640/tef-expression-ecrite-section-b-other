CHAPTER 35: THE SIGNATURE BLOCK AND FINAL FORMATTING

📖 THE EXAM MOMENT

You have written your closing formula. It is correct. The salutation echo matches your opening. The verb, the offering word, the object, the adjective agreement — everything is in place (Ch. 33–34). You feel a wave of relief.

And then you do something that approximately 15% of candidates do under exam pressure:

You stop.

You put down your pen. Or you move your cursor away from the text field. You think the letter is done.

It is not done.

The closing formula is the last sentence of your letter. But it is not the last element. Below the closing formula sits the signature block — the element that identifies you as the author, that gives the letter its legal and communicative authority, and that completes the visual architecture you began in Chapter 6 with the sender block.

A letter without a signature is like a contract without a name at the bottom. It exists. It communicates. But it lacks the final mark of authenticity that transforms a text into a personal document from a specific person.

This chapter covers everything that happens after the closing formula: the signature itself, the optional elements that may follow it, and the final formatting check that ensures your letter looks, from top to bottom, like a properly constructed French formal letter.

1️⃣ WHAT GOES BELOW THE CLOSING FORMULA

The Complete Post-Closing Architecture

After the closing formula, the following elements appear in this exact order:

[CLOSING FORMULA — last sentence of the letter]

                                         [SPACE — 2 to 4 blank lines]

                                         [Handwritten signature — paper only]
                                         [Typed name: Prénom NOM]
                                         [Title/Role — if relevant]

[P.J. : list of attached documents — if applicable]
[Copie à : list of people receiving copies — if applicable]

Let us examine each element in detail.

2️⃣ THE SIGNATURE — YOUR NAME AS THE FINAL MARK

What the Signature Is

In a formal French letter, the "signature" has two components:

ComponentWhat It IsPaper-BasedComputer-Based
The handwritten signatureYour actual handwriting of your name — the physical mark✅ Required — leave space for it❌ Not possible — skip this component
The typed/printed nameYour name written in standard text: Prénom NOM✅ Required — appears below the handwritten signature✅ Required — this IS your signature in digital format

How to Write Your Name

The Format: Prénom NOM

In French formal correspondence, the standard convention for the signature line is:

First name in normal case + LAST NAME in capital letters

Examples:

CorrectWhy
Marie DUPONTFirst name normal, last name capitalized — standard French convention
Jean-Pierre MARTINHyphenated first name normal, last name capitalized
Fatima BENALISame convention applies to all names

Why the last name is capitalized:

In French administrative and professional writing, capitalizing the last name (nom de famille) is a convention that ensures the last name is immediately distinguishable from the first name. This is particularly important in French, where some names can be either first names or last names (Laurent, Pierre, Martin). The capitalization removes ambiguity.

⚠️ Common errors in the signature name:

ErrorProblemCorrection
❌ MARIE DUPONTBoth names fully capitalized — looks like a form field, not a signature✅ Marie DUPONT
❌ marie dupontNo capitalization at all — looks informal and careless✅ Marie DUPONT
❌ Dupont MarieLast name before first name — this is database/administrative order, not signature order✅ Marie DUPONT
❌ M. DupontAbbreviated — the signature should use your full first name✅ Marie DUPONT
❌ MarieFirst name only — insufficient for a formal letter✅ Marie DUPONT

What Name to Use

When the prompt gives you an identity:

"Vous êtes Marie Dupont, habitante de Lyon."

Your signature must be: Marie DUPONT

You cannot use your real name if the prompt assigns you a different identity. You are playing a role. Sign as that role.

When the prompt does not give you a name:

"Vous écrivez une lettre au directeur du service client..."

You have two options:

OptionHow It WorksRecommendation
Invent a French nameChoose a plausible French name: Pierre MARTIN, Sophie LAURENT, Ahmed BENAISSA✅ Recommended — it completes the letter naturally
Use your real nameSign with your actual name✅ Also acceptable — the TEF does not penalize this

💡 Practical advice: Before exam day, decide on a default "character name" that you will use whenever the prompt does not assign one. Having this decided in advance saves you 30 seconds of hesitation during the exam. Choose something simple and unmistakably French: Pierre MARTIN or Marie DUPONT are classic defaults.

3️⃣ THE TITLE/ROLE LINE — WHEN TO INCLUDE IT

What It Is

Below your name, you may include a line stating your title or role — the capacity in which you are writing.

When to Include It

SituationInclude Title?Example
Cover letter✅ Yes — if you have a relevant current titleMarie DUPONT <br> Ingénieure en informatique
Complaint as a customer🟡 Optional — usually not neededPierre MARTIN <br> Client réf. 4521 (if reference number was mentioned)
Suggestion as a citizen🟡 Optional — can strengthen your standingSophie LAURENT <br> Habitante du quartier Saint-Michel
Letter as a parent🟡 OptionalAhmed BENAISSA <br> Parent d'élève
Letter as an employee✅ Yes — establishes your positionJulie MOREAU <br> Responsable du service marketing
Opinion letter to newspaper❌ Usually not — you are writing as a private citizenPierre MARTIN
Response letter🟡 Depends on contextInclude if your role is relevant to the response

Format

The title/role line appears directly below your name, without any punctuation or prefix:

Marie DUPONT
Ingénieure en informatique

Not:

❌ Marie DUPONT, Ingénieure en informatique     (not on the same line)
❌ Titre : Ingénieure en informatique            (no label needed)
❌ Marie DUPONT
   - Ingénieure en informatique                  (no bullet point)

4️⃣ THE PHYSICAL PLACEMENT OF THE SIGNATURE

Where the Signature Sits on the Page

The signature block is right-aligned — positioned on the right side of the page, mirroring the position of authority. This is the French convention.

     [LEFT SIDE]                              [RIGHT SIDE]

     [Letter content...]

     Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur,
     l'expression de mes salutations
     distinguées.


                                              Marie DUPONT

Spacing

ElementSpacing Rule
Between closing formula and signature2–4 blank lines on paper (space for handwritten signature); 1–2 blank lines on computer
Between name and title/roleNo blank line — immediately below the name
Between signature block and P.J. line1–2 blank lines

Paper-Based Exam — The Handwriting Zone

On paper, the space between the closing formula and the typed name is where your handwritten signature goes. This is the physical mark — the pen stroke that makes the letter "signed."

     Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur,
     l'expression de mes salutations
     distinguées.

                                              [handwritten: Marie Dupont]
                                              Marie DUPONT

💡 Practical tip for paper-based exams: When you reach the closing formula, count down 3–4 lines and write your printed name (Marie DUPONT). Then go back to the blank space above and add your handwritten signature. This ensures you do not accidentally write your printed name too close to the closing formula, leaving no room for the signature.

Computer-Based Exam — No Handwriting

On a computer-based TEF, there is no handwritten signature. Simply leave one blank line after the closing formula and type your name:

Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Marie DUPONT

This is sufficient. The examiner knows that computer-based letters cannot include handwritten signatures.

5️⃣ THE "P.J." LINE — PIÈCES JOINTES (ATTACHED DOCUMENTS)

What It Is

P.J. stands for Pièces jointes — "attached documents" or "enclosures." This line appears at the very bottom of the letter, below the signature block, and lists any documents that accompany the letter.

When to Include It

Include the P.J. line only when the prompt mentions or implies that documents are enclosed. Common scenarios:

ScenarioDocuments to Reference
Complaint about defective productInvoice, receipt, photos of damage
Cover letterCV (curriculum vitae), diplomas, recommendation letters
Request for refundProof of purchase, bank statement
Suggestion with supporting evidenceStatistics, petition signatures, plans
Response to an offerSigned contract, identity documents

How to Format It

Standard format:

P.J. : Curriculum vitae
       Lettre de recommandation

Or on a single line if there is only one document:

P.J. : Copie de la facture n° 4521

Formatting rules:

RuleCorrectIncorrect
AbbreviationP.J. (with periods after each letter)❌ PJ (no periods), ❌ Pj, ❌ p.j.
Followed by colon + spaceP.J. : Curriculum vitae❌ P.J. Curriculum vitae (missing colon)
Left-alignedAlways at the left margin❌ Right-aligned or centered
PositionBelow signature block, separated by 1–2 blank lines❌ Above the closing formula, ❌ within the letter body
ContentList document names — brief, noun phrases❌ Full sentences describing the documents

Complete Example with P.J.

Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes
salutations distinguées.


                                              Marie DUPONT

P.J. : Copie de la facture du 15 mars 2024
       Photographies du produit endommagé

The Relationship Between P.J. and Body Text

If you include a P.J. line, you should also reference the documents in the body of the letter (typically in the pre-closing paragraph, as covered in Ch. 32, Element C). The P.J. line is a formal inventory of what is enclosed; the body text is where you explain what the documents are and why you are including them.

Body text (pre-closing):

Vous trouverez ci-joint une copie de la facture ainsi que des photographies attestant de l'état du produit à la réception.

P.J. line (bottom of letter):

P.J. : Copie de la facture du 15 mars 2024

  • Photographies du produit endommagé*

The body text and the P.J. line should match — do not reference a document in the body that is not listed in the P.J., and do not list a document in the P.J. that is not mentioned in the body.

When NOT to Include P.J.

If the prompt does not mention any documents, do not invent them. Adding "P.J. : Curriculum vitae" to a complaint letter about a noisy neighbor is irrelevant and confusing. The P.J. line is only for documents that are logically part of the situation described in the prompt.

💡 TEF-specific guidance: Including a P.J. line when appropriate demonstrates task completion awareness — you have thought about what a real letter in this situation would include. However, forgetting the P.J. line when documents are mentioned in the prompt is a minor omission, not a catastrophic error. The examiner will primarily evaluate whether you referenced the documents in your body text (Ch. 32). The P.J. line is a bonus detail.

6️⃣ THE "COPIE À" LINE — WHEN YOU'RE SENDING COPIES

What It Is

The "Copie à" line (sometimes abbreviated "C.c." for copie conforme) indicates that a copy of the letter is being sent to other parties.

When to Include It

This is extremely rare in TEF contexts. You would include it only if the prompt specifically states that you are sending copies to other people. For example:

"Vous écrivez au directeur de l'hôtel et envoyez une copie de cette lettre à l'office du tourisme."

Format

Copie à : M. le Directeur de l'Office du tourisme de Lyon

Or:

C.c. : Office du tourisme de Lyon

Position

Below the P.J. line (if present), or below the signature block (if no P.J.):

                                              Marie DUPONT

P.J. : Copie de la facture
Copie à : Office du tourisme de Lyon

💡 For TEF purposes, you will almost never need this line. It is included here for completeness. If the prompt does not mention sending copies, do not include it. Focus your attention on the elements that matter most: the signature and, when relevant, the P.J. line.

7️⃣ THE FINAL FORMATTING CHECK — YOUR LETTER AS A VISUAL OBJECT

Why Formatting Matters

Your letter is not only read — it is seen. Before the examiner reads a single word, they see the letter as a visual object on the page (or screen). In that first glance, they form an impression:

What They SeeWhat They Think
Clear sections, visible paragraphs, consistent spacing, right-aligned signature"This candidate knows letter format. Let me read the content."
One continuous block of text, no paragraphs, no visible structure"This candidate may not understand the letter format. Let me look more carefully for structural issues."
Header at top, body in middle, closing formula + signature at bottom"Properly structured. Good."
Text that starts immediately with content, no header, no salutation"Missing structural elements. Deduction likely."

The visual impression takes two seconds to form and influences the examiner's reading of everything that follows.

The "No Formatting Tools" Reality

Most TEF computer-based platforms offer minimal or no formatting tools:

This means your letter will look like a plain text document. This is expected and acceptable. The examiner evaluates the structural elements (are they present and in the right order?), not the visual polish (are they perfectly aligned?).

9️⃣ SPECIAL CASES

Case 1: The Prompt Assigns You a Professional Identity

"Vous êtes Sophie Laurent, responsable de la communication de l'association Les Amis du Parc."

Your signature block should reflect this identity:

                                              Sophie LAURENT
                                              Responsable de la communication
                                              Association Les Amis du Parc

Why include the title and organization: The prompt gave you this identity because it is relevant to the letter. Your signature should confirm your authority to write in this capacity. A letter from "Sophie Laurent, responsable de la communication" carries more weight than a letter from "Sophie Laurent" alone — and the examiner rewards your awareness of this.

Case 2: The Prompt Gives You No Identity

"Vous écrivez au maire de votre ville pour proposer..."

Your signature block is simple:

Pierre MARTIN

Or, if you want to reinforce your standing (as discussed in Ch. 16, suggestion letters):

                                              Pierre MARTIN
                                              Habitant du quartier Nord

The title line is optional here, but it demonstrates awareness that the letter's persuasive power is connected to the writer's identity.

Case 3: Cover Letter Signature

Cover letters have a slightly different signature convention because they are professional documents in a hiring context.

Marie DUPONT

Do NOT include your current job title in a cover letter signature unless it is directly relevant to the position you are applying for. The cover letter body (Ch. 17, 23, 29) is where you present your qualifications. The signature is simply your name.

Exception: If the prompt specifies your current role and it strengthens your candidacy:

"Vous êtes actuellement assistant(e) de direction dans une entreprise de commerce international."

Then:

                                              Marie DUPONT
                                              Assistante de direction

Case 4: Anonymous Opinion Letter

Some opinion letters to newspapers may be written under a pseudonym or with the option of anonymity. However, for TEF purposes, always sign with a full name. The task requires a complete letter, and a complete letter has a signature.

🔟 WHAT COULD GO WRONG — SIGNATURE AND FORMATTING ERRORS

Error 1: No Signature at All

What happens: The letter ends with the closing formula. No name appears anywhere below it.

Why it's serious: The letter is structurally incomplete. It is an unsigned document. Under respect de la consigne, the examiner notes that a required element of the letter format is missing.

How it happens: The candidate runs out of time, or forgets, or assumes the closing formula is the end.

Prevention: As stated in Ch. 5 (planning method), write your signature on the page BEFORE you begin the letter. On paper, write your printed name at the bottom of the available space. Then write your letter from the top. This ensures the signature is always present, even if you run out of time mid-letter.

Error 2: Signing with First Name Only

❌ Marie

Why it's wrong: A formal letter requires a full name. A first name alone is appropriate for personal correspondence, not for a letter to a company director or a mayor.

Fix: Marie DUPONT — always first name + last name.

Error 3: Signing with an Inappropriate Name

❌ SuperMarie2024

This is not a joke — candidates accustomed to online usernames occasionally make this error on computer-based exams.

Fix: Use a proper French name, as discussed in Section 2.

Error 4: Placing the Signature Before the Closing Formula

❌ Marie DUPONT

   Veuillez agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Why it's wrong: The closing formula is the last sentence of the letter. The signature comes after it. Reversing them breaks the conventional order and confuses the reader.

Fix: Always: closing formula → space → signature.

Error 5: Signature on the Left

❌ Marie DUPONT [left-aligned]

Why it's problematic: French convention places the signature on the right side of the page. Left-aligning it is not technically "wrong" in a grammatical sense, but it deviates from the expected layout and may signal unfamiliarity with French letter conventions.

Fix: Right-align the signature. On computer-based exams where right-alignment is not possible, use spaces or tabs to push the name to the right, or accept that left-alignment is the best you can do — the examiner will understand the platform's limitations.

Error 6: Adding Content After the Signature

❌ Marie DUPONT

   J'oubliais de mentionner que le produit était également livré
   sans le câble d'alimentation.

Why it's wrong: No content should appear after the signature. If you forgot to mention something, you cannot add it as an afterthought below your name. This signals poor planning and breaks the letter's structure.

The P.S. question: In personal correspondence, a P.S. (post-scriptum) is acceptable. In formal letters, a P.S. is strongly discouraged. It implies that you did not organize your thoughts before writing. For TEF purposes, never use a P.S. If you realize you forgot a point, find a way to insert it into your existing paragraphs (possible on computer-based exams) or accept the omission (on paper-based exams, where insertion is difficult).

⚠️ The only exception: If the P.J. line or Copie à line appears after the signature, that is correct — these are formatting elements, not content. But no new sentences or arguments should appear after the signature.

Error 7: Inconsistency Between Sender Block and Signature

Sender block (top of letter):

Marie Dupont
15, rue des Lilas
69003 Lyon

Signature (bottom of letter):

Sophie LAURENT

Why it's wrong: The person who sent the letter (Marie Dupont) is not the person who signed it (Sophie Laurent). This inconsistency suggests the candidate lost track of their assigned identity, or copied a template without adjusting all the elements.

Prevention: Decide on your name ONCE at the beginning. Write it in the sender block. Write it in the signature. They must match.

1️⃣2️⃣ THE FINAL SECONDS — WHAT TO DO WHEN TIME IS ALMOST UP

You hear the invigilator announce: "Il vous reste cinq minutes." Or the screen timer shows 5:00. What do you do?

The Priority Hierarchy

If your letter is not yet finished, here is what to prioritize in the final minutes, in order of importance:

PriorityElementWhyTime Needed
1 🔴Closing formulaWithout it, the letter is structurally incomplete — major deduction30 seconds
2 🔴SignatureWithout it, the letter is unsigned — deduction under task completion10 seconds
3 🟠Pre-closing sentenceWithout it, the transition to the closing is abrupt — minor deduction20 seconds
4 🟡Completing Paragraph 3If unfinished, your argument is incomplete — but the letter still functions1–2 minutes
5 🟡P.J. lineNice to have if documents are mentioned, but its absence is a minor issue15 seconds
6 ⬜Editing/proofreadingValuable but not as critical as having a complete structureRemaining time

The Emergency Sequence

If you have only 2 minutes left and your letter has body paragraphs but no closing:

Step 1 (30 seconds): Write one pre-closing sentence:

Je reste à votre disposition pour tout renseignement complémentaire.

Step 2 (30 seconds): Write your emergency closing formula:

Veuillez agréer, [Salutation Echo], l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Step 3 (10 seconds): Write your signature:

Marie DUPONT

Step 4 (remaining 50 seconds): Quick proofread of the closing section only — check spelling, commas, agreement.

Total time used: ~2 minutes. Your letter now has a complete structure. Even if Paragraph 3 is shorter than ideal, the letter functions as a formal letter because it has all required structural elements.

The Worst Possible Outcome

The worst thing you can do in the final minutes is:

❌ Continue writing body content, hoping to "finish the argument," and run out of time with no closing formula and no signature.

A letter with three strong body paragraphs but no closing formula scores lower than a letter with two body paragraphs and a complete closing section. The structural completeness of the letter is a fundamental requirement. An incomplete argument can still earn partial credit. An incomplete letter structure is a visible failure.

🔴 Absolute rule: ALWAYS leave at least 2 minutes at the end for the closing formula and signature. Non-negotiable.

B1 Level:

Cordialement,
Marie

What marks this as B1:

  • Email sign-off instead of closing formula
  • First name only — no last name
  • No formatting awareness — no P.J., no title, no visual structure
  • Examiner assessment: The candidate does not know French letter conventions

B2 Level:

Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes
salutations distinguées.

Marie DUPONT

P.J. : Copie de la facture du 15 mars 2024

What marks this as B2:

  • Complete closing formula (correct grammar, echo present)
  • Full name in proper format (Prénom NOM)
  • P.J. line included because documents were referenced in the body
  • Clean visual separation between elements
  • Examiner assessment: The candidate understands and applies French letter conventions

C1 Level:

Je vous prie de bien vouloir agréer, Monsieur le Directeur,
l'assurance de mes sentiments les plus respectueux.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY — WHAT THIS CHAPTER TEACHES THAT NO OTHER CHAPTER COVERS

Chapter 35 is the only chapter that addresses what happens after the closing formula and how the letter functions as a visual object.

Every other chapter focuses on what to write. This chapter focuses on how the finished letter looks — the spatial arrangement, the signature conventions, the post-closing elements, and the visual impression that the examiner forms before reading a single word.

The key lessons:

  1. The signature is mandatory. Prénom NOM format, right-aligned, after the closing formula.
  2. The P.J. line is conditional. Include it only when the prompt involves documents. Format it correctly: P.J. : followed by document names.
  3. The visual structure matters. Paragraphs separated by blank lines, clear sections, consistent formatting — these create the first impression.
  4. When time runs out, prioritize structure over content. A complete letter with a shorter Paragraph 3 scores better than an incomplete letter with three fully developed paragraphs.
  5. Never add content after the signature. No P.S., no afterthoughts, no additional arguments.
