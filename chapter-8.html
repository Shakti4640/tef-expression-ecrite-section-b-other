🎯 TEF Scoring Criteria Addressed in This Chapter

📖 THE EXAM MOMENT

Your sender block sits at the top-left (Ch. 6). Your recipient block sits below and to the right (Ch. 7). You move to the next element — a single line that most candidates either skip entirely or write incorrectly.

The date line.

It seems trivial. It is one line. It contains a city, a day, a month, and a year. How could anyone get this wrong?

And yet, the date line is one of the most reliable indicators of whether a candidate has studied French writing conventions or is simply transferring English conventions into French. The differences are small but specific — and an examiner trained in French formal correspondence notices them instantly.

A correctly formatted date line takes 10 seconds to write. It signals: "This candidate knows French conventions down to the details." An incorrectly formatted date line — or a missing one — signals: "This candidate is guessing."

This chapter is short because the content is simple. But simple does not mean unimportant. In the architecture of examiner psychology (Ch. 4), small correct details contribute to the halo effect. Small incorrect details contribute to the horn effect. The date line is one of those small details.

1️⃣ WHAT THE DATE LINE IS AND WHERE IT SITS

The Function

The date line records when the letter was written and where the writer was when they wrote it. In real correspondence, this has legal and administrative significance — many formal letters (complaints, cancellations, contract notices) are time-sensitive, and the date establishes the official timestamp.

In the TEF exam, the date line serves a conventional function: it is a required element of French letter format. Its presence signals awareness of format. Its absence signals a gap.

💡 In some formatting conventions, the date line appears ABOVE the recipient block rather than below it. Both positions are acceptable on the TEF. The most common convention places it below the recipient block, and this is the format recommended in this guide. The examiner will accept either position — they are checking that the date EXISTS and is CORRECTLY FORMATTED, not its exact vertical position.

2️⃣ THE FRENCH DATE FORMAT — THE COMPLETE RULE

The Formula

The French date line in a formal letter follows this exact structure:

[City], le [day] [month] [year]

Each Component Explained

ComponentRuleExampleCommon Error
CityThe city where you are writing the letter — usually the same city as your sender addressToulouseOmitting the city entirely
CommaA comma follows the city nameToulouse*,***No comma, or a period instead
"le"The definite article le precedes the day number — always lowercaseToulouse, leOmitting le, or capitalizing it (Le) when it is not at the start of a line
Day (number)Written as a number (not spelled out), with NO "th" or "st" equivalentle 15Writing le 15ème or le 15e (French does not use ordinal numbers for dates except for the 1st — see below)
MonthSpelled out in full, in lowercasele 15 marsCapitalizing the month (Mars) — this is an English convention, not French
YearWritten as a four-digit numberle 15 mars 2025Writing only the last two digits (25)

The Complete Example

Toulouse, le 15 mars 2025

The Special Case: The 1st of the Month

The first day of the month is the ONLY day that uses an ordinal number in French date formatting:

DayFormatExample
1st of the monthle 1er [month]le 1er mars 2025
All other daysle [number] [month]le 2 mars 2025, le 15 mars 2025, le 31 mars 2025

"1er" is the abbreviation of premier (first). Note: the superscript "er" may be written as "1er" on paper or as "1ᵉʳ" if your computer supports superscript.

💡 For the TEF, the simplest strategy is to choose a date that is NOT the 1st of the month. Use the 10th, 15th, 20th, or any other date. This avoids the 1er special case entirely. If the prompt specifies a date that happens to be the 1st, use le 1er [month] [year].

The Capitalization Rule — Why French Months Are Lowercase

In English, months are proper nouns — they are always capitalized: January, February, March. In French, months are common nouns — they are NOT capitalized unless they begin a sentence (which they never do in a date line, since le precedes them).

This is one of the most common errors English-speaking candidates make. It is a small error, but it signals that the candidate is applying English conventions to French — exactly the kind of transfer error that the SOC criterion is designed to detect.

🔴 Capitalizing the month in a French date is a visible anglicism. The examiner notices it immediately. It is not catastrophic (it will not drop your score by 3 points), but it contributes to a negative impression under SOC and suggests that the candidate has not internalized French writing conventions. It is easily avoidable — simply remember: French months are always lowercase.

4️⃣ WHAT DATE TO USE — STRATEGIES FOR THE TEF

When the Prompt Specifies a Date

Some prompts include dates that are part of the scenario:

"Vous avez commandé un produit le 15 mars 2025."

In this case, the date of your letter should be AFTER the event date. If you ordered on March 15, your letter might be dated a few days or weeks later:

Toulouse, le 2 avril 2025

The date must be plausible within the scenario. Writing a complaint letter dated before the event occurred is a logical error.

When the Prompt Does Not Specify a Date

Most prompts do not tell you what date to put on your letter. You choose one.

The simplest strategy: Use the actual date of your exam. This is always plausible and requires no invention.

If you do not remember the exact date (exam stress can do this), use a plausible recent date:

Toulouse, le 10 mars 2025

What NOT to Use

Problematic DateWhy
A date in the distant past (le 15 mars 1998)Implausible for a current complaint or request
A date in the futureA letter cannot be dated in the future
A date that contradicts the prompt timelineIf the prompt says the problem started "last month," your letter date should be at least one month after the start
No date at allMissing structural element — SOC −

5️⃣ THE CITY IN THE DATE LINE — WHICH CITY TO USE

The Rule

The city in the date line is the city where you are writing the letter — which is normally the city of your sender address (Ch. 6).

Sender Address CityDate Line CityConsistency
31000 ToulouseToulouse, le 15 mars 2025✅ Consistent
75008 ParisParis, le 15 mars 2025✅ Consistent
31000 ToulouseParis, le 15 mars 2025❌ Inconsistent — you live in Toulouse but dated the letter from Paris?

When the Prompt Places You in a Specific City

If the prompt says "Vous habitez à Bordeaux," your sender address is in Bordeaux and your date line city is Bordeaux:

Sophie MARTIN
28, rue Sainte-Catherine
33000 Bordeaux


                              [Recipient block]

                              Bordeaux, le 15 mars 2025

Can You Omit the City?

In some informal contexts, the city is omitted and only the date appears: "Le 15 mars 2025." However, in formal letters, the city is conventionally included. For the TEF, include it. It takes one extra word and reinforces your awareness of conventions.

Numerical Date Format

🔴 The only acceptable date format in a French formal letter is the written-out format: City, le [day] [month in letters] [year]. Any numerical format is inappropriate for the letter body. Numerical dates may appear in reference lines (Réf. — Ch. 7) but never as the letter's date line.

English-Style Date Format

Some candidates write the date in English format translated into French:

English FormatIncorrect French AdaptationWhy It's Wrong
March 15, 2025Mars 15, 2025Month before day is English order; month capitalized
March 15th, 2025Mars 15ème, 2025Ordinal marker on a day other than the 1st; month capitalized; English word order
15th March 202515 Mars 2025Missing le; month capitalized; missing city

The correct French format eliminates all of these problems:

Toulouse, le 15 mars 2025

City first. Then le. Then day (cardinal number, no ordinal marker except for 1er). Then month (lowercase, spelled out). Then year (four digits). No additional punctuation after the year.

7️⃣ DAYS OF THE WEEK — DO THEY APPEAR IN THE DATE LINE?

The Short Answer: No.

In French formal letter dating, the day of the week is not included. The format is always:

[City], le [day number] [month] [year]

NOT:

[City], le lundi 15 mars 2025 ← INCORRECT for formal letters

When Days of the Week Appear in Letters

The day of the week may appear in the body of the letter, not in the date line:

"L'incident s'est produit le lundi 10 mars dernier."

In this context, including the day of the week adds specificity to a narrative. But the date line itself remains without the day of the week.

Days of the Week — Lowercase in French

Just like months, days of the week are lowercase in French:

English (Capitalized)French (Lowercase)
Mondaylundi
Tuesdaymardi
Wednesdaymercredi
Thursdayjeudi
Fridayvendredi
Saturdaysamedi
Sundaydimanche

💡 You will not use days of the week in the date line, but you may use them in the letter body to describe events. When you do, keep them lowercase.

8️⃣ THE DATE LINE ON PAPER vs. ON COMPUTER

Paper-Based Exam

AspectHow to Execute
PositionWrite the date on the right side of the page, below the recipient block, aligned with the recipient block's left edge
SpacingLeave 1 blank line between the recipient block and the date line
HandwritingWrite clearly — the date is a short line and easy to read, but sloppy handwriting can make mars look like mais or 15 look like 13

Computer-Based Exam

AspectHow to Execute
PositionIf you are right-aligning the recipient block (using tabs or spaces), place the date at the same alignment. If everything is left-aligned (due to computer limitations), place the date on its own line after the recipient block with a blank line separating them.
AccentsEnsure you type février with the accent on é, and décembre with the accent on é. Missing accents on a computer are harder to excuse than on paper, because the keyboard provides them.

9️⃣ WHAT COULD GO WRONG — DATE LINE ERRORS

Error 1: Omitting the Date Line Entirely

How common: Common (20-30% of candidates)

Why it happens: Candidates forget it or consider it unimportant.

Impact: SOC − (missing convention). The date line is a standard element of French formal letters. Its absence is noticed during the examiner's visual scan (Ch. 4). Combined with other missing elements (no Objet, no closing formula), it contributes to the "incomplete letter" impression.

Fix: The date line is part of your Phase 2 writing sequence. After writing the recipient block, write the date immediately. It takes 10 seconds.

Error 2: Capitalizing the Month

How common: Very common among English speakers (40-50%)

What it looks like: Toulouse, le 15 Mars 2025

Impact: SOC − (anglicism detected). The examiner sees an English convention applied to French. It is a minor error in isolation, but combined with other anglicisms, it builds a pattern.

Fix: French months are ALWAYS lowercase: janvier, février, mars, avril, mai, juin, juillet, août, septembre, octobre, novembre, décembre. No exceptions.

Error 3: Using Numerical Format

What it looks like: 15/03/2025

Impact: SOC − (format inappropriate for a formal letter). Numerical dates belong on forms, invoices, and databases — not on the date line of a letter.

Fix: Always write the date in the full written format: Toulouse, le 15 mars 2025.

Error 4: Missing the Article "le"

What it looks like: Toulouse, 15 mars 2025

Impact: Minor grammatical/formatting error. The article le is part of the standard format. Its absence makes the date look slightly incomplete.

Fix: Always include le before the day number. It is one two-letter word that completes the format.

Error 5: Omitting the City

What it looks like: Le 15 mars 2025

Impact: Minor formatting imperfection. The city is conventionally included in formal letters. Its absence is less noticeable than other errors but still represents an incomplete date line.

Fix: Include the city. It is the same city as your sender address — no decision needed, just copy it.

Error 6: Using English Month Names

What it looks like: Toulouse, le 15 March 2025

Impact: VOC − (English word in French text), SOC − (obvious language transfer error). This is more serious than capitalizing a French month name — it is a completely wrong language.

How common: Rare but it happens under stress, especially with months that look similar in English and French (December/décembre, November/novembre).

Fix: Memorize the French month names. Pay special attention to months that differ significantly from English: janvier (not January), février (not February), juin (not June), juillet (not July), août (not August).

Error 7: Inconsistent City Between Sender Block and Date Line

What it looks like:

Sender block: Sophie MARTIN, 45, rue des Lilas, 31000 Toulouse
Date line: Paris, le 15 mars 2025

Impact: Logical inconsistency. You live in Toulouse but are dating your letter from Paris? This creates confusion and signals carelessness.

Fix: The city in the date line = the city in your sender address. Always.

Error 8: Adding Punctuation After the Year

What it looks like: Toulouse, le 15 mars 2025*.*

Impact: Minimal — a period after the date line is not standard (the date line is not a sentence), but it is a very minor issue. Most examiners would not penalize this.

Fix: No period after the year. The date line stands alone without terminal punctuation.

1️⃣2️⃣ B1 vs. B2 vs. C1 — THE DATE LINE AT THREE LEVELS

B1 Date Line

15 Mars 2025

Problems: No city, no le, month capitalized. Three convention violations in one short line. SOC −.

B2 Date Line

Toulouse, le 15 mars 2025

Correct: All conventions respected. City present and consistent with sender address. Month lowercase. Article le present. SOC ✅.

C1 Date Line

Toulouse, le 15 mars 2025

Identical to B2. The date line does not change between B2 and C1 — it is a fixed-format element. What makes a letter C1 is not the date line but the content, grammar, and vocabulary. The date line simply needs to be correct.

💡 This is one of the few elements where B2 and C1 are identical. There is no "more sophisticated" way to write a date. It is either correct or incorrect. Make it correct and move on. Do not overthink this element — invest your creative energy in the body paragraphs.

1️⃣3️⃣ THE 10-SECOND DATE LINE ROUTINE

On exam day, the date line should take no more than 10 seconds:

STEP A [3 seconds]: Recall the city from your sender block
  → Same city. No decision needed.

STEP B [7 seconds]: Write the date line
  → [City], le [day] [month in lowercase] [year]
  → Example: Toulouse, le 15 mars 2025

Done. Move to Objet line (Ch. 9).

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY — WHAT THIS CHAPTER TEACHES THAT NO OTHER CHAPTER COVERS

Chapter 8 is the only chapter that teaches you the French date formatting convention for formal letters.

This is a small, mechanical element — but it is precisely the kind of small, mechanical element that separates candidates who have studied French writing conventions from those who have not. The examiner does not consciously think: "This candidate wrote the date correctly — I'll give them extra points." Instead, the correct date contributes to the cumulative impression of competence that builds throughout the visual scan (Ch. 4).

Think of it this way: the sender block, recipient block, and date line together form the header trio. If all three are present and correctly formatted, the examiner's first 10-second scan produces the classification "competent letter" — which triggers the positive anchor, the halo effect, and confirmation bias in your favor. If any of the three is missing or incorrectly formatted, the classification weakens.

The date line is the easiest of the three to get right. It is one line. It follows one formula. It takes 10 seconds. There is no reason to lose points on it.

What you should be able to do after this chapter:

  • Write a French date line correctly in 10 seconds
  • Remember that French months are ALWAYS lowercase
  • Include the city, the article le, and the full four-digit year
  • Use 1er for the first of the month and cardinal numbers for all other days
  • Avoid all seven common errors listed in Section 9
  • Maintain consistency between the sender block city and the date line city

The single sentence summary: The date line is 10 seconds of writing that either confirms your competence or reveals your unfamiliarity with French conventions — there is no middle ground, and the correct format is too simple to get wrong if you have studied it once.
