📖 THE EXAM MOMENT — A DIFFERENT CHALLENGE

In Chapter 37, you wrote a complaint. The emotional fuel was frustration. The structural engine was evidence. The communicative goal was to demand resolution for something that went wrong.

Now the situation is fundamentally different. You are not complaining. Nothing went wrong. Instead, you need something — and the person you are writing to has no obligation to give it to you.

This changes everything:

DimensionComplaint Letter (Ch. 37)Request Letter (This Chapter)
Power dynamicYou have leverage (you were wronged)You have no leverage (you are asking a favor)
ToneFirm, assertive, controlled frustrationPolite, justified, grateful in advance
Grammar centerPassé composé + passive (what happened)Conditional + subjunctive (what you hope will happen)
Paragraph 2 functionPresent evidence of the problemJustify why your request is legitimate
Paragraph 3 functionDemand specific resolutionFacilitate the "yes" — make it easy for the recipient to agree
Emotional trajectoryCalm → escalation → firm demand → opennessRespectful → justified → specific → grateful
RiskSounding too aggressiveSounding too demanding or too servile

The request letter is, in many ways, harder than the complaint letter — because you must persuade without any leverage. Your only tools are justification, politeness, and making the recipient want to help you.

1️⃣ THE PROMPT

Situation :
Vous êtes étudiant(e) en master de sciences de l'environnement à l'Université de Bordeaux. Dans le cadre de votre mémoire de fin d'études, vous devez réaliser un stage de recherche de trois mois dans un laboratoire spécialisé. Vous avez appris que le Centre de Recherche en Écologie Marine (CREM), situé à Brest, dispose d'un programme d'accueil de stagiaires. Cependant, la date limite de candidature officielle est dépassée depuis deux semaines.

Consigne :
Vous écrivez une lettre à la directrice du CREM pour demander à être accepté(e) en stage malgré le dépassement de la date limite. Dans votre lettre, vous devez :

  • vous présenter et expliquer votre situation universitaire ;
  • justifier votre intérêt pour le CREM et les raisons de votre candidature tardive ;
  • formuler votre demande de manière précise et proposer des solutions pour faciliter votre intégration.

Rédigez une lettre formelle d'au moins 200 mots.

SegmentAnnotationTEF Criterion
Je souhaiterais,Conditional present of "souhaiter." The core politeness structure (Ch. 31). "Je souhaiterais" (I would wish) is softer than "je souhaite" (I wish) and far softer than "je veux" (I want). In a request letter, the conditional is not optional — it is the grammatical embodiment of deference.Grammar — conditional for politeness; Sociolinguistic — deferential register
si cela vous paraît envisageable,"If this seems feasible to you." Conditional softener inserted parenthetically between commas. This phrase does something remarkable: it gives the recipient permission to say no. By explicitly acknowledging that the request might not be feasible, the candidate shows respect for the recipient's constraints. Paradoxically, this makes the recipient more likely to say yes — because the request does not feel like a demand. "Envisageable" (conceivable/feasible) is more formal than "possible."Sociolinguistic — deference; Vocabulary — envisageable (formal)
intégrer votre laboratoire"To join your laboratory." "Intégrer" means to become part of — it positions the internship as integration into a team, not just physical presence in a building.Vocabulary — intégrer (formal, implies belonging)
à compter du mois de mai prochain"Starting from next May." "À compter de" is the formal expression for "starting from" — administrative register. More elevated than "à partir de." Specifying the month makes the request precise (Required Point 3).Vocabulary — à compter de (administrative expression); Task Completion — precise request
pour une durée de trois mois"For a duration of three months." Specifies the length — again, precision as required by the prompt. "Pour une durée de" is more formal than "pendant trois mois."Vocabulary — formal temporal expression; Task Completion — duration specified

9️⃣ THE PERSUASION ARCHITECTURE

This letter follows a specific persuasion strategy that differs fundamentally from the complaint letter's evidence strategy:

The Request Letter Persuasion Sequence

STEP 1 — ESTABLISH LEGITIMACY (P1)
"I am a real student with a real need at a real university."
→ The recipient thinks: "This person is legitimate."

STEP 2 — ACKNOWLEDGE THE PROBLEM (P2, Sentence 3)
"I know I missed the deadline."
→ The recipient thinks: "This person is honest."

STEP 3 — EXPLAIN THE PROBLEM (P2, Sentence 4)
"Here is why — my professor told me about you recently."
→ The recipient thinks: "That is a reasonable explanation."

STEP 4 — DEMONSTRATE FIT (P2, Sentence 5)
"Your research matches my thesis perfectly."
→ The recipient thinks: "This could actually be useful for us."

STEP 5 — PROPOSE MUTUAL BENEFIT (P2, Sentence 6)
"This would be enriching for both of us."
→ The recipient thinks: "Maybe accepting this late application is worthwhile."

STEP 6 — MAKE THE REQUEST SPECIFIC (P3, Sentence 7)
"May, three months, your laboratory."
→ The recipient thinks: "This is concrete. I can evaluate it."

STEP 7 — REMOVE OBSTACLES (P3, Sentences 8-9)
"I will send documents immediately. I will adapt to any schedule."
→ The recipient thinks: "This person will not create extra work for me."

STEP 8 — EXPRESS GRATITUDE (Pre-closing, Sentence 12)
"Thank you for considering this."
→ The recipient thinks: "This person is gracious. I feel good about helping."

Each step builds on the previous one. The sequence cannot be rearranged — legitimacy must come before the request, and the request must come before the facilitation offers.

This is the persuasion architecture taught across Ch. 15 (context), Ch. 21 (justification), and Ch. 27 (facilitation) — now visible in a complete letter.

1️⃣3️⃣ WHAT COULD GO WRONG — REQUEST-SPECIFIC PITFALLS

Beyond the universal closing disasters (Ch. 36), request letters have their own specific pitfalls:

Pitfall 1: Sounding Entitled

❌ Je vous demande de m'accepter en stage malgré le retard de ma candidature. Votre centre correspond à mes besoins et je compte sur vous pour faire une exception.

Why it fails: "Je vous demande" (I demand) and "je compte sur vous" (I am counting on you) position the candidate as someone who expects compliance, not someone who is asking for a favor. The phrase "correspond à mes besoins" (corresponds to my needs) makes the letter entirely about the candidate — the recipient's perspective is absent.

The fix (from this chapter): "Je souhaiterais, si cela vous paraît envisageable, intégrer votre laboratoire..." — conditional mood + explicit acknowledgment that the recipient may say no.

Pitfall 2: Being Too Apologetic

❌ Je suis vraiment désolé d'avoir raté la date limite. Je m'excuse pour ce retard impardonnable. J'espère que vous ne m'en voudrez pas et que vous pourrez quand même considérer ma demande.

Why it fails: Excessive apology undermines credibility. The candidate sounds insecure, which makes the recipient question their competence. Three apologies in three sentences is self-defeating — it draws more attention to the problem than necessary.

The fix (from this chapter): One calm acknowledgment ("Je suis pleinement conscient que la date limite est dépassée"), one explanation ("En effet, c'est à la suite d'un échange avec mon directeur de mémoire..."), then move forward. Acknowledge once, explain once, then focus on the future.

Pitfall 3: No Justification for the Late Application

❌ La date limite est passée mais je voudrais quand même faire un stage chez vous.

Why it fails: No explanation for why the application is late. The recipient is left to assume carelessness, poor organization, or indifference to rules. Without justification, the request for an exception is groundless.

The fix (from this chapter): A credible, specific explanation (professor's recommendation discovered the program late) + a demonstration of genuine fit (research alignment). The justification transforms "I missed the deadline" from a character flaw into a circumstantial event.

Pitfall 4: Vague Request

❌ J'aimerais faire un stage dans votre centre si possible.

Why it fails: When? For how long? In which department? Doing what? The recipient cannot evaluate a vague request. The prompt specifically requires "une demande précise" — a precise request.

The fix (from this chapter): "Intégrer votre laboratoire à compter du mois de mai prochain, pour une durée de trois mois" — specific start date, specific duration, specific location within the institution.

Pitfall 5: No Facilitation — Asking Without Offering

❌ J'espère que vous accepterez ma candidature. Merci d'avance.

Why it fails: The candidate asks for something but offers nothing in return. No flexibility on dates, no promise to submit documents quickly, no willingness to adapt. The recipient bears the entire burden of making the exception work.

The fix (from this chapter): Two concrete facilitation offers — immediate document submission (Sentence 8) + complete flexibility on conditions and schedule (Sentence 9). The burden shifts from the recipient to the candidate.

Chapter 38 is the only chapter that demonstrates how deference, justification, and facilitation work together in a complete letter.

The complaint letter (Ch. 37) taught you how to build a case through evidence and demand resolution. This chapter teaches you something fundamentally different: how to ask for something you have no right to demand.

The key lessons unique to this chapter:

  1. The conditional is not decoration — it is the request letter's grammatical DNA. Four conditional forms in 265 words create a consistent tone of deference that the examiner recognizes and rewards.

  2. Strategic transparency is more persuasive than concealment. Acknowledging the missed deadline in the Objet line and Sentence 3, rather than hiding it, builds credibility that makes the rest of the letter more convincing.

  3. Persuasion through shared benefit transforms a request into a proposition. The phrase "mutuellement enrichissante" (Sentence 6) changes the dynamic from "please help me" to "let us help each other."

  4. Facilitation shifts the burden. Sentences 8 and 9 do not ask the recipient to accommodate the candidate — they offer the candidate's accommodation to the recipient. This reversal is the key strategic move of any request letter.

  5. The closing formula level must match the power dynamic. Level 4 (sentiments respectueux) for a titled authority asking for a favor — Level 3 (salutations distinguées) would have been acceptable but less nuanced.

  6. The emotional trajectory of a request is the inverse of a complaint. Where the complaint escalates frustration, the request escalates enthusiasm and deference. Where the complaint demands, the request offers.

These six lessons, combined with the structural lessons from Chapter 37, give you the tools to write the two most common TEF letter types with confidence and precision.
