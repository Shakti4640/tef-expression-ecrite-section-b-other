LEVEL 5: MAXIMUM FORMALITY (Diplomatic / Institutional)

Used for: heads of state, ambassadors, ministers, very senior officials. Rarely needed at TEF level, but included for completeness and recognition.

LEVEL 4: HIGH FORMALITY (Formal Letters to Officials and Authorities)

Used for: directors, mayors, school principals, senior managers, judges. This is the level above the TEF default — use it when the recipient holds a titled position of authority.

Formula 4A — The Deferential Standard

Formula 4C — The Deferential with croire

When to use croire instead of agréer: The verb croire ("believe in") carries a nuance of trust — you are asking the recipient to believe in the sincerity of your feelings. This is particularly appropriate when:

  • You are making a suggestion and want to emphasize your genuine concern
  • You are expressing an opinion and want to underscore your sincerity
  • You are writing to someone with whom you want to establish a relationship of trust

Grammar reminder from Ch. 33: Croire + à + object. Never omit the à.

Formula 4E — The Distinguished Sentiments

ComponentContentNotes

Use distingués when writing to a director as a professional peer; use respectueux when writing to an authority figure from a citizen/client position.

TEF relevance: 🟢 High — excellent for cover letters and response letters to professional recipients

Used for: letters where the prompt specifies a semi-formal context — writing to a teacher you know, a colleague in another organization, a community group leader, a neighbor in a formal capacity.

6️⃣ FORMULAS THAT APPEAR CORRECT BUT ARE NOT

These are formulas that candidates commonly produce, believing them to be correct. Each one contains an error.

Error Formula 1

❌ Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes sentiments les plus distingués.

The error: Distingués is masculine plural — but it agrees with sentiments (masculine plural), so the agreement is actually correct. Wait — this formula is technically valid. The issue is not grammatical but tonal: "les plus distingués" with sentiments sounds slightly unusual. "Les plus distingués" is more naturally paired with salutations ("mes salutations les plus distinguées"), while sentiments more naturally takes respectueux in the superlative ("mes sentiments les plus respectueux").

Verdict: Not wrong, but unidiomatic. Avoid for TEF — use the more natural pairings.

Error Formula 2

❌ Veuillez accepter, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

The error: Accepter is not a standard verb of acceptance in formal closing formulas. The standard verbs are agréer (formal) and recevoir (semi-formal). Accepter is the everyday word for "accept" and lacks the ceremonial register of agréer.

Fix: Replace accepter with agréer: Veuillez agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Error Formula 3

❌ Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, à l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

The error: The preposition à before l'expression de. As explained in Ch. 33, agréer is a direct transitive verb — it takes its object directly, without a preposition. The à belongs with croire, not agréer.

Fix: Remove à: Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Error Formula 4

❌ Je vous prie de croire, Monsieur, l'expression de mes sentiments distingués.

The error: Missing à after croire. The verb croire requires the preposition à before its object.

Fix: Add à: Je vous prie de croire, Monsieur, à l'expression de mes sentiments distingués.

Error Formula 5

❌ Veuillez agréer, Monsieur, mes sentiments les plus respectueuses.

The error: Agreement. Sentiments is masculine plural. The adjective must be respectueux (masculine plural), not respectueuses (feminine plural). The -euses ending would be correct with salutations (feminine) but not with sentiments (masculine).

Fix: Veuillez agréer, Monsieur, mes sentiments les plus respectueux.

Error Formula 6

❌ Je vous prie d'agréer l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

The error: Missing salutation echo. The formula has no Madame, Monsieur, or titled address between commas. As established in Ch. 33, the echo is structurally mandatory.

Fix: Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Error Formula 7

❌ Veuillez agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutation distinguée.

The error: Number agreement. Salutations should be plural (salutations distinguées), not singular (salutation distinguée). The standard expression uses the plural form.

Fix: Veuillez agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Error Formula 8

❌ Agréez, Monsieur le Directeur, l'expression de mes sentiments distingués.

The error: While agréez (imperative, second person plural) is grammatically possible, the bare imperative of agréer is never used in standard French closing formulas. The convention requires either Veuillez agréer (imperative of vouloir + infinitive) or Je vous prie d'agréer (request construction). The direct imperative sounds like a command rather than a polite request.

Fix: Veuillez agréer, Monsieur le Directeur, l'expression de mes sentiments distingués. OR Je vous prie d'agréer, Monsieur le Directeur, l'expression de mes sentiments distingués.

8️⃣ WHAT COULD GO WRONG — SELECTION ERRORS

Beyond the grammatical errors covered in Section 6, candidates can make selection errors — choosing a formula that is grammatically correct but wrong for the situation.

Selection Error 1: Over-Formality

Situation: Semi-formal letter to a community group leader you know.
Opening: Cher Monsieur,

❌ Je vous prie de bien vouloir agréer, cher Monsieur, l'assurance de mes sentiments les plus respectueux.

Why it's wrong: The opening "Cher Monsieur" signals semi-formal register and prior acquaintance. The closing uses Level 4–5 language (bien vouloir, l'assurance de, les plus respectueux). The mismatch between a warm opening and an ultra-formal closing creates tonal dissonance. The examiner reads this as: "The candidate memorized a very formal formula and deployed it regardless of context."

Fix: Veuillez recevoir, cher Monsieur, mes sentiments les meilleurs. (Formula 2D — matches the semi-formal opening)

Selection Error 2: Under-Formality

Situation: Formal complaint to the director of a company.
Opening: Monsieur le Directeur,

❌ Recevez, Monsieur le Directeur, mes cordiales salutations.

Why it's wrong: The opening uses a titled salutation (Monsieur le Directeur) — this establishes formal register. The closing uses Level 1 language (Recevez, cordiales), which is too casual. Writing to a titled director with a minimal formula signals insufficient register awareness.

Fix: Veuillez agréer, Monsieur le Directeur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées. (Formula 3A — or 4A for more deference)

Selection Error 3: Tone Mismatch in Complaint

Situation: You just wrote a firm complaint demanding a refund.

❌ Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes sentiments dévoués.

Why it's wrong: Sentiments dévoués means "devoted feelings." You have just complained about being wronged, and now you are expressing devotion? The tonal contradiction undermines your entire letter. Dévoué implies loyalty and service — appropriate for an employee writing to an employer, not for a dissatisfied customer.

Fix: Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées. (Formula 3A — neutral, distanced, appropriate for a complaint)

Selection Error 4: Excessive Warmth in Decline

Situation: You are declining a job offer.

❌ Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame la Directrice, l'expression de mes sentiments les plus chaleureux.

Why it's wrong: Chaleureux (warm) is not a standard adjective in closing formulas. More importantly, expressing "warmest feelings" while declining an offer sends a confusing message. If you are saying no, your closing should maintain professional distance.

Fix: Veuillez agréer, Madame la Directrice, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées. (Formula 3A — neutral, appropriate for a decline)

9️⃣ B1 vs. B2 vs. C1 — THE SELECTION SKILL AT THREE LEVELS

The closing formula is not just about correct grammar — it is about appropriate selection. Here is how the selection skill manifests at three levels:

B1 Level — No Selection (Default to Email Conventions)

The B1 candidate does not know closing formulas. They write:

Cordialement, / Merci. / Au revoir.

Examiner assessment: Sociolinguistic competence not demonstrated. The candidate cannot perform the formal letter convention.

B2 Level — Correct Selection from a Limited Repertoire

The B2 candidate knows 2–3 formulas and selects appropriately:

  • Writes to a company → uses Formula 3A (salutations distinguées)
  • Writes to a mayor → uses Formula 4A (sentiments respectueux)
  • Writes semi-formally → uses Formula 2A (recevoir + salutations distinguées)

Examiner assessment: Sociolinguistic competence demonstrated. The candidate understands the system and selects appropriately.

C1 Level — Nuanced Selection from a Full Repertoire

The C1 candidate knows the full formula bank and makes nuanced choices:

  • Writes a cover letter for a prestigious position → uses Formula 4B with l'assurance de (elevated deference)
  • Writes a suggestion to a mayor → uses Formula 4C with croire à (emphasizes sincerity)
  • Writes an acceptance → uses Formula 4E with sentiments distingués (warm professionalism)
  • Adjusts the superlative based on the relationship (les plus respectueux vs. respectueux)

Examiner assessment: Sociolinguistic mastery. The candidate not only knows the system but navigates its nuances.

💡 Your target is B2. Knowing three formulas and selecting the right one for the right situation is sufficient. The C1 nuances are valuable for those aiming higher but are not required for a strong TEF score.
