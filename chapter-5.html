📖 THE EXAM MOMENT

The booklet is open. You have read the prompt. You understand the scoring criteria (Ch. 2), the register requirements (Ch. 3), and the examiner's psychology (Ch. 4). Your pen is in your hand. Your instinct screams: start writing immediately.

Do not.

The single most consequential decision you make in the entire exam is what you do in the first 5 minutes after reading the prompt. Candidates who begin writing immediately produce letters that wander, miss required points, run out of structure in the third paragraph, and end abruptly because they never decided where they were going.

Candidates who plan for 5 minutes produce letters that are focused, complete, logically structured, and properly closed — because they knew the destination before they started the journey.

Five minutes of planning is not lost writing time. It is the most productive writing time you will spend. Every minute of planning saves two minutes of writing, because you never stop mid-sentence to think "What should I say next?" You already know.

This chapter gives you the exact planning method — a repeatable, step-by-step system that works for every prompt, every letter type, every exam session.

1️⃣ WHY PLANNING MATTERS — THE EVIDENCE

What Happens Without Planning

2️⃣ THE PLANNING METHOD — OVERVIEW

The method has five steps, each taking approximately one minute. You perform these steps on scrap paper (if paper-based) or in a mental framework (if computer-based, though you can type brief notes at the top of your response and delete them later).

StepTimeWhat You DoWhat You Produce
Step 1: READ60 secondsRead the prompt twice — extract the four elementsIdentity, recipient, trigger, required points
Step 2: CLASSIFY30 secondsIdentify the letter type and register levelLetter type label + register decision
Step 3: ALLOCATE90 secondsAssign content to paragraphs using the planning gridCompleted planning grid
Step 4: FORMULAS60 secondsDecide on salutation, closing formula, and first sentenceThree pre-decided elements written on your planning sheet
Step 5: VERIFY30 secondsCheck that every required point has a paragraph homeVerification checkmarks

Total: 4 minutes 30 seconds to 5 minutes.

Now let us walk through each step in complete detail.

3️⃣ STEP 1: READ — EXTRACT THE FOUR ELEMENTS (60 Seconds)

First Read: General Understanding (30 seconds)

Read the entire prompt once, quickly, for overall comprehension. Do not analyze. Do not plan. Just understand: What is the situation? What am I being asked to do?

Second Read: Element Extraction (30 seconds)

Read the prompt again, this time with a pen in hand (or cursor ready). You are looking for exactly four elements. As you find each one, mark it or note it.

The Four Elements

ElementWhat to Look ForHow to Mark ItExample from a Sample Prompt
WHO you areYour identity in the scenario — name, role, statusUnderline or note: "MOI = ...""Vous êtes Sophie Martin, cliente de la société TéléPlus depuis trois ans" → MOI = cliente fidèle de TéléPlus
WHO you write toThe recipient — name, title, organizationUnderline or note: "DEST = ...""Vous écrivez au directeur du service client de TéléPlus" → DEST = directeur service client
WHY you writeThe triggering event or situationUnderline or note: "POURQUOI = ...""Votre facture de téléphone du mois de mars comporte des erreurs de facturation" → POURQUOI = erreurs facturation mars
WHAT you must includeThe required points — usually bullet pointsNumber them: 1, 2, 3"1. Décrire les erreurs constatées 2. Exprimer votre mécontentement 3. Demander une correction et un geste commercial"

A Complete Extraction Example

Here is a prompt with the four elements extracted:

Prompt:
Vous habitez dans un immeuble au centre-ville de Montréal. Depuis deux mois, l'ascenseur de votre immeuble est en panne et le propriétaire n'a entrepris aucune réparation malgré plusieurs signalements de la part des résidents. Vous écrivez au propriétaire de l'immeuble pour :

  • rappeler la situation et les signalements déjà effectués ;
  • expliquer les conséquences de cette panne sur votre quotidien ;
  • exiger une réparation dans un délai précis.

Rédigez une lettre formelle d'au moins 200 mots.

Extraction:

ElementExtracted Information
MOIRésident(e) d'un immeuble, centre-ville de Montréal
DESTPropriétaire de l'immeuble
POURQUOIAscenseur en panne depuis 2 mois, aucune réparation malgré signalements
POINTS1. Rappeler situation + signalements 2. Conséquences sur quotidien 3. Exiger réparation + délai précis

Time spent: approximately 60 seconds.

⚠️ Common mistake at Step 1: Reading the prompt only once and missing a required point. The prompt above has THREE required points. A candidate who reads quickly might merge points 1 and 2 ("explain the situation and consequences") and forget that point 3 requires a specific deadline — not just "please fix it" but "fix it within X days." The second read catches these details.

The Three Rules of Allocation

Rule 1: Each required point gets ONE paragraph home.

You never split a required point across two paragraphs (confuses the examiner) and you never put two major required points in one paragraph (overcrowds it).

Rule 2: The natural flow is Context → Development → Action.

  • P1 always establishes context (who you are, what happened, why you're writing)
  • P2 always develops the main argument (consequences, evidence, reasoning)
  • P3 always drives toward action (request, demand, solution, proposal)

Rule 3: If there are 3 required points, the allocation is usually 1-1-1. If there are 4, one paragraph gets 2 related points.

6️⃣ STEP 4: FORMULAS — DECIDE YOUR OPENING AND CLOSING (60 Seconds)

Before writing, decide on three critical pre-fabricated elements. These are not improvised — they are selected from your memorized repertoire and written directly onto your planning sheet.

Decision 1: The Salutation

Recipient (from Step 1)Salutation
Unknown person at a companyMadame, Monsieur,
Named director (male)Monsieur le Directeur,
Named director (female)Madame la Directrice,
Mayor (male)Monsieur le Maire,
Mayor (female)Madame la Maire,
Landlord (unknown gender)Madame, Monsieur,
Landlord (known: M. Dupont)Monsieur, or Monsieur Dupont, (semi-formal)
Newspaper editorMadame, Monsieur, or Monsieur le Rédacteur en chef,
School directorMonsieur le Directeur, / Madame la Directrice,
Employer (for cover letter)Madame, Monsieur, (if unknown) or title if known

In our example: Propriétaire, gender unknown → Madame, Monsieur,

Decision 2: The Closing Formula

The closing formula MUST match the salutation. The term of address used in the salutation must reappear in the closing formula. This is the echo rule (fully detailed in Ch. 34).

SalutationMatching Closing Formula
Madame, Monsieur,Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.
Monsieur le Directeur,Je vous prie d'agréer, Monsieur le Directeur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.
Madame la Maire,Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame la Maire, l'expression de mes respectueuses salutations.

In our example: Salutation = Madame, Monsieur, → Closing = Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Write the complete closing formula on your planning sheet NOW. Do not wait until you reach the end of the letter. You will be tired, possibly rushed, and more likely to make an error. Writing it now, while you are calm and focused, guarantees accuracy.

Decision 3: The First Sentence

Select your first sentence structure based on the letter type:

Letter TypeFirst Sentence Template
Complaint"Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de [specific problem], qui [description of ongoing issue]."
Request"Je me permets de vous écrire afin de solliciter [what you want], dans le cadre de [context]."
Suggestion"En tant que [your role], je me permets de vous écrire afin de vous soumettre une proposition concernant [topic]."
Response"Je fais suite à votre courrier du [date] relatif à [topic], et je souhaiterais [accept/decline/negotiate]."
Cover letter"Ayant pris connaissance de l'offre d'emploi parue [where/when], je souhaiterais vous présenter ma candidature au poste de [position]."
Opinion"À la suite de l'article paru dans votre édition du [date] concernant [topic], je me permets de vous faire part de mes réflexions sur ce sujet."

In our example (complaint): "Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de la panne d'ascenseur qui affecte notre immeuble depuis maintenant deux mois, sans qu'aucune mesure corrective n'ait été prise."

Write this first sentence — or at least its key structure — on your planning sheet.

💡 At this point, you have pre-decided three elements that together represent the highest-value parts of your letter (Ch. 4: primacy effect + recency effect). Your salutation is chosen. Your first sentence is drafted. Your closing formula is written. The psychological "frame" of your letter is already in place before you write a single body paragraph.

Time spent: approximately 60 seconds.

7️⃣ STEP 5: VERIFY — THE 30-SECOND CHECKLIST (30 Seconds)

Before you begin writing, perform one final verification. Look at your planning grid and check:

The 6-Point Pre-Writing Verification

#CheckWhat You're VerifyingAction If Failed
1☐ Every required point from the prompt has a paragraph assignedRC complianceReassign content — find a home for the missing point
2☐ No paragraph has MORE than 2 required pointsCC — paragraph focusSplit the overloaded paragraph or move one point elsewhere
3☐ P1 is context, P2 is development, P3 is actionCC — logical flowReorder if necessary
4☐ Salutation matches the recipient typeSOC complianceCorrect the salutation
5☐ Closing formula contains the same term of address as the salutationSOC — echo ruleCorrect the closing formula
6☐ Total estimated word count is 200-280RC — word countIf too short: add a detail to P2. If too long: cut one detail from P2.

If all 6 checks pass, you are ready to write.

Time spent: approximately 30 seconds.

├──────────┼────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ P3       │ REFUTATION of concession                        │
│ Conclus. │ Broader implication / call to reflection         │
│          │ What should be done / What readers should       │

└──────────┴────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

💡 Memorize the template for each letter type BEFORE the exam. When you identify the letter type in Step 2, the corresponding template should appear in your mind automatically. You then simply fill in the content from the specific prompt. This transforms the planning step from a creative exercise (hard under pressure) into a filling-in exercise (easy under pressure).

🔟 COMMON PLANNING MISTAKES AND HOW TO AVOID THEM

Mistake 1: Over-Planning

What happens: The candidate spends 15 minutes creating an elaborate plan with full sentences, detailed notes, and multiple drafts of the first paragraph. They now have only 35 minutes to write, edit, and check.

The rule: Your plan should fit on one-third of a scrap paper page. Keywords and short phrases only. No full sentences except the first sentence and the closing formula. If your plan is longer than 10-12 lines of brief notes, you are over-planning.

What a correct plan looks like physically:

TYPE: Réclamation  REGISTRE: Formel

OBJET: Panne ascenseur — demande réparation urgente

SALUT: Madame, Monsieur,

P1 (~50): résidente 5 ans / asc. panne 2 mois / 
    signalements x3 (oct, nov, déc) / aucune suite

P2 (~65): conséq: pers âgées / courses / sécurité 
    (urgence méd.) / qualité vie / décevant

P3 (~50): exiger répar. 15 jours / obligations légales / 
    sinon démarches juridiques

CLÔTURE: Dans l'attente... agréer, Mme, M., 
    salutations distinguées.

1re PHRASE: Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de 
    la panne d'ascenseur qui...

Total space: about 12 lines. Total time: about 5 minutes. This is correct.

Mistake 2: Under-Planning

What happens: The candidate glances at the prompt, thinks "OK, I know what to write," and starts immediately. By paragraph 2, they realize they are repeating paragraph 1. By paragraph 3, they have forgotten a required point. The closing is rushed.

The rule: ALWAYS draw the grid. Even if you think you know what to write. The grid takes 90 seconds and prevents structural collapse. No candidate has ever regretted planning. Many have regretted not planning.

Mistake 3: Planning Content but Not Formulas

What happens: The candidate plans their paragraph content beautifully but does not pre-decide the salutation, first sentence, or closing formula. When they reach these elements during writing, they hesitate, make errors, or produce mismatched formulas.

The rule: Step 4 (formulas) is not optional. Your salutation, closing formula, and first sentence must be decided BEFORE writing begins. These are the highest-value elements (Ch. 4: primacy + recency effects). They deserve deliberate, calm selection — not last-minute improvisation.

Mistake 4: Ignoring Word Count Estimates

What happens: The candidate allocates content to paragraphs but does not estimate word counts. They write a 100-word P1 (too long — takes up half the letter), a 40-word P2 (underdeveloped), and a 30-word P3 (skeletal). The letter is unbalanced.

The rule: Write approximate word targets in the grid. The targets are:

SectionTargetMinimumMaximum
P1 (Context)~50 words3560
P2 (Development)~65 words5080
P3 (Action)~50 words3565
Pre-closing + Closing~35 words2545
TOTAL CONTENT~200170250

P2 is the longest because it carries the most argumentative weight. P1 and P3 are roughly equal. The closing is the shortest but non-negotiable.

Mistake 5: Planning Only Content, Not Connectors

What happens: The candidate plans WHAT to say in each paragraph but not HOW to connect the paragraphs. The result: three well-planned paragraphs that feel disconnected — like three separate mini-letters instead of one unified letter.

The enhanced planning method: In your grid, note the connector you will use to open P2 and P3:

P1 → [connector: De surcroît / En outre] → P2
P2 → [connector: En conséquence / C'est pourquoi] → P3

Choosing your paragraph-opening connectors during planning ensures that your letter has built-in cohesion from the start. This is a 10-second addition to your planning that directly improves your CC score.

Mistake 6: Changing the Plan Mid-Letter

What happens: The candidate starts writing, decides the plan is wrong, and changes direction mid-letter. The first paragraph follows Plan A. The second paragraph follows Plan B. The letter loses coherence.

The rule: Once you start writing, commit to the plan. The plan may not be perfect, but a consistently executed imperfect plan produces a more coherent letter than a half-executed plan that switches mid-stream. The only exception: if you realize you misidentified the letter type (e.g., you planned a complaint but the prompt asks for a suggestion), stop and re-plan. This is rare if Step 1 was done carefully.

1️⃣4️⃣ B1 vs. B2 vs. C1 — PLANNING AT THREE LEVELS

B1 Planning (or No Planning)

The B1 candidate either does not plan or creates a vague mental outline:

"OK, I need to complain about the elevator. I'll talk about the problem, say I'm unhappy, and ask them to fix it."

Result: The letter addresses the general topic but misses specifics. Required points may be partially covered. Structure is loose. Closing formula is improvised or missing.

B2 Planning (The Method in This Chapter)

The B2 candidate uses the 5-step method:

  • All four elements extracted from the prompt
  • Letter type and register identified
  • Content allocated to paragraphs with keyword notes
  • Salutation, closing formula, and first sentence pre-decided
  • Verification completed

Result: The letter is focused, complete, well-structured, and properly opened and closed. Every required point is addressed. Word count is controlled. Connectors are pre-selected.

C1 Planning (Enhanced Method)

The C1 candidate does everything the B2 candidate does, plus:

  • Plans specific vocabulary choices for each paragraph (formal synonyms pre-selected)
  • Plans grammar structures to deploy (e.g., "I'll use a subjunctive in P2 with bien que, and a passive in P3")
  • Plans the concession-refutation structure specifically
  • Plans the pre-closing sentence as a distinct element
  • Considers the overall rhetorical arc (how the letter builds persuasive momentum from P1 to P3)

Result: The letter is not just structurally sound — it is rhetorically crafted. Each paragraph builds on the previous one. Grammar and vocabulary choices are deliberate, not accidental.

💡 You are aiming for B2 planning. But incorporating even one C1 planning element — such as pre-selecting a grammar structure to deploy — can push your score upward. The easiest C1 element to add: during Step 3, note in your P2 section "BIEN QUE + subjonctif" as a reminder to use a concession structure. This one addition takes 5 seconds and directly targets a GRAM positive trigger (Ch. 4, Trigger #6).
