📖 THE EXAM MOMENT

You have written your letter. You are reasonably satisfied. The salutation is in place, the paragraphs seem logical, the closing formula feels right. You hand in your booklet or click "Submit."

Now what happens?

Your letter enters a process you never see. An examiner — a trained, certified evaluator — opens your response. They do not read it the way you read a novel. They do not read it the way a friend reads a message. They read it with a scoring grid in front of them — a grid with specific criteria, specific descriptors, and specific point values. They read your letter through the lens of that grid.

If you do not know what is on that grid, you are writing blind. You are hoping that "good French" will earn points, without knowing which aspects of "good French" are being measured, or how much each aspect is worth.

This chapter removes the blindfold. After reading it, you will know exactly what the examiner is looking for, in what order, and with what weight. Every technique taught in Chapters 3–42 maps directly to what you learn here.

1️⃣ THE FIVE EVALUATION CRITERIA — OVERVIEW

TEF Expression Écrite Section B is evaluated on five criteria. These are not arbitrary categories invented by a textbook. They are derived from the Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR), which the TEF is aligned with. Every CEFR-aligned exam uses some version of these criteria.

#Criterion (French)Criterion (English)What It MeasuresShort Code Used in This Guide

2Cohérence et cohésionCoherence & CohesionIs your letter logically organized and connected?CC

💡 Memorize these five short codes (RC, CC, VOC, GRAM, SOC). Every chapter in this guide will reference them. When you see "This technique scores under CC and VOC," you will know instantly which scoring criteria benefit.

2️⃣ HOW SCORING WORKS — THE BAND SYSTEM

Each criterion is scored on a band scale, typically from 0 to 3 or 0 to 4 depending on the TEF administration version. The most common system uses four bands per criterion (0, 1, 2, 3), where:

BandGeneral Meaning
0Criterion not met at all — absent, incomprehensible, or entirely inappropriate
1Criterion partially met — significant deficiencies, limited competence visible
2Criterion adequately met — competent performance with some errors or limitations
3Criterion fully met — strong, confident performance with minimal issues

The total score for Section B is the sum across all five criteria, which is then mapped to a TEF level (B1, B2, C1, etc.) using the TEF's proprietary conversion table.

What This Means Strategically

If each criterion is scored 0–3 and there are 5 criteria, your maximum raw score is 15 points. This means:

Score RangeApproximate LevelWhat It Signals
0–4Below B1Fundamental problems across multiple criteria
5–7B1Basic competence, significant limitations
8–11B2Competent formal writing, some errors acceptable
12–15C1 / C1+Sophisticated, near-native formal writing

⚠️ Critical insight: You do not need a perfect score on every criterion to reach B2. A typical B2 performance might look like: RC=3, CC=2, VOC=2, GRAM=2, SOC=2 = 11/15. This means you can have some imperfections in vocabulary, grammar, and cohesion and still score B2, as long as you complete the task fully and maintain appropriate register.

The Strategic Implication

This scoring structure reveals a crucial priority order:

PriorityCriterionWhy
🥇 FirstRC (Task Completion)A score of 0 here means the letter fails regardless of how beautiful the French is
🥈 SecondSOC (Sociolinguistic Appropriateness)A formal letter written in informal register is fundamentally broken
🥉 ThirdCC (Coherence & Cohesion)A disorganized letter cannot communicate effectively
FourthVOC (Lexical Range)Important but recoverable — limited vocabulary reduces your score but doesn't destroy it
FifthGRAM (Grammar)Errors matter, but a few grammar mistakes in an otherwise strong letter are survivable

💡 This is counterintuitive for many candidates. Most students spend 80% of their preparation time on grammar and vocabulary. But the scoring matrix tells you that task completion, register, and organization together are worth 60% of your score. A grammatically perfect letter that misses a required point, uses informal language, or lacks paragraph structure will score lower than a letter with some grammar errors that fully addresses the prompt in proper formal register with clear organization.

3️⃣ CRITERION 1: RESPECT DE LA CONSIGNE (Task Completion) — RC

What the Examiner Is Checking

The examiner asks themselves a series of yes/no questions:

QuestionYes = Points MaintainedNo = Points Lost
Did the candidate write a letter (not an essay, not a paragraph, not a list)?✅❌ Structural failure
Did the candidate address all required points listed in the prompt?✅❌ Content gap
Did the candidate write to the correct recipient?✅❌ Task misunderstanding
Did the candidate adopt the correct role (customer, resident, applicant, etc.)?✅❌ Task misunderstanding
Did the candidate write the correct type of letter (complaint, request, etc.)?✅❌ Complete misdirection
Did the candidate meet the minimum word count (200 words)?✅❌ Automatic penalty
Is the content relevant to the situation described?✅❌ Off-topic material

The Band Descriptors for RC

BandDescriptorWhat It Looks Like in Practice
0Task not attempted, or response is entirely off-topic, or response is not a letterThe candidate wrote an essay about customer service instead of a complaint letter to a specific company. Or the candidate wrote 3 sentences and stopped.
1Task partially addressed — one or more required points missing, or letter type misidentified, or significant content gapsThe prompt asked for three things: recall the facts, express dissatisfaction, and request a solution. The candidate recalled the facts and expressed dissatisfaction but never requested anything. Two out of three = Band 1.
2Task adequately addressed — all required points present but some are underdeveloped, or minor relevance issuesAll three required points appear, but the "request a solution" point is only one vague sentence: "J'espère que vous allez résoudre ce problème." It is present but thin.
3Task fully and effectively addressed — all required points developed with appropriate detail, correct letter type, correct recipient, correct role, adequate word countAll three points are clearly developed. The facts are recalled with specifics (dates, order number). The dissatisfaction is expressed with measured but clear language. The requested solution is specific (replacement or refund, with a timeframe).

How to Guarantee a Band 3 on RC

This is the easiest criterion to max out, because it is the most mechanical. Follow these rules:

Rule 1: The Bullet Point Checklist

During your 5-minute planning phase (Ch. 5), copy each required point from the prompt onto your planning sheet. Number them. Assign each one to a specific paragraph. After writing, verify each one is present.

Rule 2: The Direct Address Test

For each required point, ask: "If the examiner highlights my letter looking for this point, will they find it within 5 seconds?" If the answer is no — if the point is buried, implied, or vague — make it more explicit.

Rule 3: The Letter Format Test

Before submitting, visually scan your letter. Does it have:

  • ☐ A sender block?
  • ☐ A recipient block?
  • ☐ A date?
  • ☐ An Objet line?
  • ☐ A salutation?
  • ☐ At least 3 body paragraphs?
  • ☐ A closing formula?
  • ☐ A signature?

If any box is unchecked, you are losing RC points for structural incompleteness.

Rule 4: The Word Count Verification

If paper-based: count your words (method described in Ch. 1). If below 200, expand your weakest paragraph by adding one more sentence of development. If computer-based: check the word counter.

🔴 What to never do under RC: Do not invent content that contradicts the prompt. If the prompt says you ordered a product on March 15, do not write that you ordered it in January. If the prompt says you are a resident of Lyon, do not write that you live in Paris. Contradicting the prompt's facts is treated as a task completion failure.

4️⃣ CRITERION 2: COHÉRENCE ET COHÉSION (Coherence & Cohesion) — CC

The Difference Between Coherence and Cohesion

These are two different things measured under one criterion. Understanding the difference is essential.

ConceptDefinitionHow It Manifests in Your Letter
CoherenceThe logical organization of ideas — do they follow a rational order?Paragraph 1 sets up context → Paragraph 2 develops the argument → Paragraph 3 proposes a solution. Each paragraph has ONE main idea. The reader never thinks "Why is this here?"
CohesionThe linguistic connections between sentences and paragraphs — are they linked with appropriate devices?Logical connectors (cependant, en outre, par conséquent), pronoun references (cette situation, celui-ci), lexical echoes (repeating key terms), and transition sentences

An Analogy

Think of coherence as the blueprint of a building — the rooms are in logical places (kitchen near dining room, not inside the bathroom). Cohesion is the hallways and doors — the physical connections that let you move from room to room.

A letter can be coherent but lack cohesion: the ideas are in the right order, but there are no connectors linking them — the reader must guess the relationships. A letter can have cohesion markers but lack coherence: connectors are present (cependant, de plus, en effet) but the ideas themselves are in a random order — the connectors create an illusion of logic where none exists.

You need both.

The Band Descriptors for CC

BandDescriptorWhat It Looks Like
0No discernible organization; ideas are scattered randomly; no connectors; impossible to follow the logicA stream of consciousness — complaints mixed with requests mixed with background information, all in one unparagraphed block with no connectors.
1Some organization visible but inconsistent; paragraphs may exist but their function is unclear; few connectors, or connectors used incorrectlyThree paragraphs exist, but P1 contains a demand, P2 provides background, and P3 restates the demand. The order makes no sense. A few connectors appear (mais, et, aussi) but no sophisticated ones.
2Clear organization — each paragraph has a discernible purpose; adequate use of connectors; minor lapses in logical flowThe letter follows Context → Argument → Solution order. Connectors are present and mostly correct (cependant, en effet, par conséquent). One transition between paragraphs feels slightly abrupt.
3Sophisticated organization — ideas build on each other progressively; varied and precise connectors; smooth transitions; the reader never hesitates about the logical flowEach paragraph clearly serves its function. Connectors are varied (not the same three repeated), precise (the right connector for the right logical relationship), and well-placed. Transitions between paragraphs feel natural. The letter reads as one unified argument, not three separate blocks.

What the Examiner Physically Does When Evaluating CC

The examiner performs two mental operations:

Operation 1: The Paragraph Function Test

They mentally label each paragraph:

  • "P1 = context ✓"
  • "P2 = argument/development ✓"
  • "P3 = request/solution ✓"

If any paragraph resists labeling — if it contains a mix of functions — the coherence score drops.

Operation 2: The Connector Audit

They scan for connectors. Not just their presence, but their accuracy. Using en effet (which introduces evidence/confirmation) where you mean en revanche (which introduces contrast) is worse than using no connector at all, because it actively misleads the reader.

Connector ErrorWhy It's Harmful
Using en effet to introduce a contrasting ideaEn effet means "indeed / in fact" (confirmation). Using it for contrast confuses the logical relationship.
Using par contre in formal writingPar contre is considered informal by many French style authorities. En revanche is the formal equivalent.
Using mais at the start of every contrasting sentenceShows limited range — the candidate knows only one opposition connector.
Using de plus and en outre and par ailleurs in the same letter to mean the same thingWhile technically correct, it suggests the candidate memorized a list without understanding the nuances. De plus adds to the same point, en outre adds a new dimension, par ailleurs shifts to a related but different aspect.

💡 The complete connector system is taught in Ch. 25. For now, understand that CC is not about scattering connectors through your letter — it is about using the RIGHT connector for the RIGHT logical relationship.

How to Build a Band 3 CC Score

TechniqueWhat It DoesWhere Taught

One main idea per paragraphPrevents idea-mixing that destroys coherenceCh. 13

5️⃣ CRITERION 3: ÉTENDUE ET MAÎTRISE DU VOCABULAIRE (Lexical Range & Control) — VOC

What the Examiner Is Checking

Two sub-dimensions:

Sub-DimensionQuestion the Examiner Asks
Range (Étendue)Does the candidate use varied vocabulary, or do they repeat the same words? Do they have access to precise, topic-specific terms?
Control (Maîtrise)When the candidate uses a word, is it the RIGHT word? Is the meaning accurate? Is the collocation natural?

The Band Descriptors for VOC

BandDescriptorWhat It Looks Like
0Vocabulary so limited that communication fails; constant repetition of basic words; frequent meaning errorsThe candidate uses chose (thing) for every noun, faire (to do/make) for every verb, and bien/mal (good/bad) for every adjective. Key words are used incorrectly, creating confusion.
1Basic vocabulary sufficient for the general topic but lacking precision; noticeable repetition; some meaning errors or false friendsThe candidate can write about a complaint but uses "le produit est mauvais" instead of "le produit est défectueux"; writes "j'ai un problème" three times instead of varying with "j'ai rencontré une difficulté", "cette situation est problématique", "un dysfonctionnement s'est produit".
2Adequate vocabulary with some variety and precision; occasional gaps or imprecisions but meaning is always clear; some topic-specific terms used correctlyThe candidate uses "défectueux" for a broken product, "retard de livraison" for a late delivery, "service après-vente" for customer service. Some repetition exists but is not excessive. Occasional imprecision ("demander" where "solliciter" would be more precise) but meaning is never lost.
3Rich, varied, and precise vocabulary; topic-specific terms used naturally; collocations are accurate; no significant repetition; word choice enhances the letter's effectivenessThe candidate writes "un manquement à vos engagements contractuels" instead of "vous n'avez pas fait ce que vous aviez promis"; uses "procéder au remboursement" instead of "me rembourser"; varies between "constater", "observer", "remarquer" instead of using "voir" three times.

The Vocabulary Trap — Why "Difficult" Words Can Backfire

Many candidates believe that using complex, rare vocabulary will impress the examiner. This is half true and half dangerous.

Half true: Using precise, formal vocabulary appropriate to the context DOES score points. Writing "je vous saurais gré de" instead of "je voudrais que vous" demonstrates lexical sophistication.

Half dangerous: Using a complex word incorrectly is worse than using a simple word correctly. The examiner's assessment of VOC includes control, not just range.

StrategyRisk LevelExample
Using simple words correctly🟢 Safe — scores Band 1-2"Je suis mécontent de cette situation." — Correct, clear, but basic.
Using precise formal words correctly🟢🟢 Ideal — scores Band 2-3"Cette situation est tout à fait regrettable." — Precise, formal, natural.
Using complex words incorrectly🔴 Dangerous — can drop to Band 0-1"Cette situation est inadmissible et je suis très dégoûté." — "Dégoûté" means physically disgusted, not formally displeased. Wrong register, wrong connotation.

💡 The golden rule for VOC: Never use a word you are not sure about. A precise simple word always beats an imprecise complex word.

The Repetition Problem

The examiner is trained to notice repetition. Here is what repetition looks like and how to fix it:

Repetitive Version (Band 1)Varied Version (Band 2-3)
"J'ai un problème. Ce problème est grave. À cause de ce problème, je vous écris.""J'ai rencontré une difficulté importante. Cette situation est particulièrement préoccupante. C'est la raison pour laquelle je me permets de vous écrire."
"Je veux un remboursement. Je veux aussi des excuses. Je veux une réponse rapide.""Je souhaiterais obtenir un remboursement. J'apprécierais également de recevoir des excuses formelles. Enfin, je vous saurais gré de me faire parvenir une réponse dans les meilleurs délais."

The varied versions use synonyms, restructured sentences, and different verb constructions to avoid repetition while maintaining clarity. This is exactly what Band 2-3 requires.

6️⃣ CRITERION 4: CORRECTION GRAMMATICALE (Grammatical Accuracy) — GRAM

What the Examiner Is Checking

Again, two sub-dimensions:

Sub-DimensionQuestion
Range of structuresDoes the candidate use varied grammatical structures, or only simple S-V-O sentences?
AccuracyWhen the candidate uses a structure, is it correct? Are agreements, conjugations, tenses, and moods accurate?

The Band Descriptors for GRAM

BandDescriptorWhat It Looks Like
0Errors so frequent and severe that meaning is regularly lost; basic structures are incorrectSubject-verb agreement errors ("les produits est arrivé"), tense confusion throughout, no complex structures attempted. The examiner must re-read sentences to guess the meaning.
1Basic structures are mostly correct but complex structures are avoided or attempted with frequent errors; errors sometimes affect meaningSimple sentences are correct ("J'ai commandé un produit"), but attempts at complex structures fail ("Je voudrais que vous me remboursez" — should be "remboursiez", subjunctive). Tense selection is sometimes wrong.
2Good control of standard structures; complex structures attempted with reasonable success; errors occur but rarely affect meaningThe candidate uses conditional ("je souhaiterais"), relative clauses ("le produit que j'ai commandé"), and some subjunctive correctly. Occasional errors in agreement or tense, but the reader always understands the intended meaning.
3Confident control of complex structures; varied tenses and moods used accurately; errors are rare and minorThe candidate uses passive voice ("aucune solution ne m'a été proposée"), conditional past ("j'aurais apprécié que"), subjunctive after triggers ("il est inadmissible que ce produit soit arrivé"), and complex relative clauses — all correctly. Minor errors (a missed agreement on a past participle) may exist but are isolated.

The Grammar Errors That Cost the Most Points

Not all errors are equal. Some errors are "minor" (the examiner notes them but moves on). Others are "major" (they affect meaning or signal fundamental gaps).

Error TypeSeverityExampleWhy It's Costly
Subject-verb agreement failure🔴 MAJOR"Les informations que vous m'avez envoyé" (should be envoyées)Signals weak grasp of a fundamental rule
Subjunctive avoidance or failure🟠 SIGNIFICANT"Il faut que vous faites" (should be fassiez)Subjunctive is THE B2 grammar marker — getting it wrong is conspicuous
Tense confusion (passé composé vs imparfait)🟠 SIGNIFICANT"Quand j'ai reçu le colis, il a été endommagé" (should be était endommagé)Affects narrative clarity
Gender errors on common nouns🟡 MODERATE"un situation" (should be une)Noticeable but doesn't usually affect meaning
Missing ne in negation🟠 SIGNIFICANT"J'ai pas reçu de réponse"This is the #1 register violation — it signals spoken/informal French in a formal letter
Preposition errors🟡 MODERATE"Je m'adresse à vous de vous demander"Awkward but meaning usually recoverable
Spelling errors on common words🟡 MODERATE"adresse" written as "addresse"Signals carelessness or anglicism

🔴 The single most damaging grammar error in a formal letter is dropping the "ne" in negation. In spoken French, "j'ai pas reçu" is universal. In written formal French, it is unacceptable. Writing "je n'ai pas reçu" is not just grammatically correct — it is a register signal. The examiner reads the retained ne and thinks: "This candidate understands formal writing." They read the missing ne and think: "This candidate is writing the way they speak." This single error affects BOTH the GRAM criterion and the SOC criterion simultaneously.

7️⃣ CRITERION 5: ADAPTATION SOCIOLINGUISTIQUE (Sociolinguistic Appropriateness) — SOC

Why This Criterion Exists

This is the criterion that makes the TEF letter task unique. An essay tests your French. A letter tests your French AND your ability to adapt that French to a social context.

The SOC criterion asks: Does this letter sound like it was written by someone who understands how formal communication works in French-speaking culture?

What the Examiner Is Checking

ElementWhat They Look ForWhere in the Letter
Register consistencyIs the entire letter in formal/semi-formal register, from first word to last?Everywhere — one informal sentence in an otherwise formal letter is a SOC failure
Appropriate salutationDoes the salutation match the recipient's status and the letter type?Opening line (Ch. 10)
Appropriate closing formulaDoes the closing formula match the salutation and the letter's tone?Final line (Ch. 34)
Tone appropriatenessIs the tone appropriate for the situation? (A complaint should be firm but not aggressive; a cover letter should be confident but not arrogant; a suggestion should be constructive but not presumptuous)Throughout body paragraphs
Politeness conventionsDoes the candidate use the conditional for requests? Indirect formulations for demands? The appropriate level of deference for the recipient?Body paragraphs, especially P3 (Ch. 26-30)
Letter format conventionsDoes the letter look like a French formal letter? (Header, Objet, paragraphing, closing formula structure)Physical layout (Ch. 6-9, 35)

The Band Descriptors for SOC

BandDescriptorWhat It Looks Like
0No awareness of register or convention; the text reads as informal/spoken French put on paper; no letter formatting elements"Salut, je vous écris car j'ai un truc qui marche pas et c'est vraiment nul. Faut que vous me remboursez direct. Merci bye." — This is not an exaggeration; candidates who have never studied formal French writing conventions produce text like this.
1Some awareness of register but inconsistent; formal elements appear alongside informal ones; some letter conventions present but incompleteThe candidate writes "Madame, Monsieur," (correct salutation) but then writes "Je veux que vous me remboursiez parce que c'est pas normal" (informal demand, missing ne). The closing is "Cordialement" alone. The letter has an Objet line but no sender/recipient block.
2Generally appropriate register maintained; letter conventions mostly respected; tone is suitable for the situation; occasional minor register slipsThe letter is consistently formal. The salutation, Objet, and closing formula are all present and correct. The body uses conditional politeness ("je souhaiterais") and formal vocabulary. One or two minor slips (using "OK" or "en fait" once) are present but do not break the overall formal tone.
3Fully appropriate register maintained throughout; all letter conventions respected; tone perfectly calibrated to the situation; the letter reads as a natural, culturally competent piece of French formal communicationEvery element is in place. The register never wavers. The tone is precisely calibrated: firm but respectful for a complaint, polite but justified for a request, collaborative but specific for a suggestion. The closing formula matches the opening salutation. The candidate demonstrates awareness of French epistolary culture — the letter could be sent as-is to a real recipient.

The Register Break — The Most Common SOC Failure

A "register break" occurs when a candidate switches from formal to informal language (or vice versa) within the same letter. It is the most common reason for losing SOC points.

Why register breaks happen:

CauseExampleHow to Prevent
Fatigue — The candidate starts formal but gets tired and slips into natural speech patternsP1: "Je me permets de vous écrire..." → P3: "Faut vraiment que ça change."Write the closing formula FIRST (Ch. 34). Knowing the letter must end formally keeps you disciplined throughout.
Emotional involvement — The complaint scenario triggers genuine frustration and the candidate starts writing emotionally"C'est vraiment scandaleux et j'en ai marre de cette situation !"Remember: you are playing a ROLE. The "you" in the letter is a character. Maintain the character's formal voice even if you personally feel frustrated.
Vocabulary gaps — The candidate doesn't know the formal word and defaults to the informal one"Le truc que j'ai commandé" instead of "L'article que j'ai commandé"Build your formal vocabulary bank (Ch. 14-18, 20-24, 26-30). If you don't know the formal word, use a simple but neutral word — never an informal one.
Connector gaps — The candidate uses informal connectors because they don't know formal ones"Et puis aussi...", "Bon, en gros...", "Du coup..."Memorize the connector system (Ch. 25). "Du coup" is the #1 informal connector that candidates mistakenly use in formal letters. Replace with "Par conséquent" or "En conséquence."

8️⃣ THE "INVISIBLE" SIXTH CRITERION — OVERALL IMPRESSION

What It Is

The five criteria above are explicit — they appear on the scoring grid. But there is a sixth factor that influences scoring: the examiner's overall impression (impression d'ensemble).

This is not officially scored as a separate criterion, but research on examiner behavior shows that it affects how they assign scores within each band. When an examiner is deciding between Band 2 and Band 3 on a criterion, the overall impression of the letter pushes them one way or the other.

What Creates a Positive Overall Impression

FactorHow It Works
Strong openingThe examiner's first impression is formed in the first 10 seconds. A clean header, correct salutation, and confident first sentence create a "halo effect" — the examiner subconsciously expects the rest to be good. This is the primacy effect from cognitive psychology.
Strong closingThe last thing the examiner reads before scoring is the closing formula. A perfect closing formula — complete, matching the salutation, correctly structured — creates a positive final impression. This is the recency effect.
Visual cleanlinessA well-paragraphed, cleanly written (or typed) letter looks competent before a single word is read. Conversely, a dense, unparagraphed block of text looks incompetent before a single word is read.
Consistent qualityA letter that maintains the same level throughout scores better in overall impression than a letter that starts brilliantly and deteriorates (suggesting the candidate ran out of steam).
Appropriate lengthA letter that is 230-270 words looks like the candidate knew what they were doing. A letter that is barely 200 words looks like the candidate struggled to fill the space. A letter over 350 words looks like the candidate couldn't control their output.

What Creates a Negative Overall Impression

FactorHow It Works
Missing structural elementsNo Objet line, no closing formula, no paragraphing — the letter immediately looks amateurish.
Visible corrections everywhere (paper-based)Multiple cross-outs, arrows, inserted words — signals panic and poor planning.
Starting strong, ending weakThe opening is formal and polished, but the closing is rushed or informal — the recency effect means the weak ending overshadows the strong opening.
Repetitive vocabularyEven if individual sentences are correct, the examiner notices when "problème" appears 5 times. It creates a monotonous impression.
Off-topic digressionsA paragraph about how much the candidate loves France in the middle of a complaint letter about a defective product — it signals loss of control.

💡 The strategic takeaway: Invest disproportionately in your opening (first 2 sentences) and your closing (closing formula). These two elements create the first and last impressions that color the examiner's reading of everything in between.

9️⃣ HOW THE EXAMINER ACTUALLY READS YOUR LETTER — THE TWO-PASS SYSTEM

Understanding the examiner's reading process helps you write strategically.

First Pass: The Overview Scan (10-15 seconds)

The examiner does NOT start by reading word by word. They scan the letter visually. In these 10-15 seconds, they register:

What They SeeWhat They Conclude
Header present, formatted correctly✅ "This candidate knows letter conventions" (SOC +)
Objet line present✅ "Formal letter structure understood" (SOC +, RC +)
Correct salutation✅ "Register is likely appropriate" (SOC +)
3-4 visible paragraphs✅ "Organization is likely adequate" (CC +)
Closing formula visible at the end✅ "Letter structure is complete" (SOC +, RC +)
Adequate length (fills most of the space)✅ "Word count likely met" (RC +)
Clean presentation✅ Positive overall impression

Or alternatively:

What They SeeWhat They Conclude
No header⚠️ "Missing structural element" (SOC −, RC −)
No Objet line⚠️ "Candidate may not know letter conventions" (SOC −)
One large block of text, no paragraphs⚠️ "Coherence likely problematic" (CC −)
Very short text⚠️ "Word count may not be met" (RC −)
Abrupt ending, no closing formula visible⚠️ "Incomplete letter" (SOC −, RC −)

🔴 Before the examiner has read a single word of your content, they have already formed a preliminary impression based on the visual structure of your letter. This is why formatting (Ch. 6-9, 35) is not optional decoration — it is your first scoring opportunity.

Second Pass: The Detailed Read (2-4 minutes)

Now the examiner reads word by word, sentence by sentence. During this pass, they:

  1. Check RC: Are all required points addressed? Is the letter type correct? Is the role maintained?
  2. Evaluate CC: Do the paragraphs follow a logical order? Are connectors present and correct?
  3. Assess VOC: Is vocabulary varied and precise? Are there repetitions? Are formal terms used appropriately?
  4. Assess GRAM: Are structures correct? Are complex structures attempted? Do errors affect meaning?
  5. Confirm SOC: Is the register consistent? Is the tone appropriate? Do the salutation and closing formula match?

They may annotate the letter (underlining errors, marking connectors, noting gaps). Then they assign a score for each criterion.

Third Pass (If Needed): The Verification

If the examiner is uncertain about a score (e.g., "Is this CC a 2 or a 3?"), they may re-read specific sections. This is where consistent quality matters — if the examiner re-reads and finds the same level of competence they found the first time, they are more likely to assign the higher score.

1️⃣1️⃣ B1 vs. B2 vs. C1 — THE SAME LETTER SCORED AT THREE LEVELS

To crystallize what the scoring matrix means in practice, here is how the same complaint letter scenario produces three different scores:

The Prompt

You ordered a product online. It arrived damaged and late. You write to the company to complain and request a solution.

B1 Performance (Score: ~6/15)

CriterionScoreWhy
RC2All points addressed but underdeveloped; letter format partially respected (salutation present, closing formula incomplete)
CC1Ideas present but poorly organized; minimal connectors (mais, et, aussi); no clear paragraph functions
VOC1Basic vocabulary, frequent repetition (problème ×4), no formal terminology
GRAM1Simple structures only; missing ne in negations; tense errors in narration
SOC1Salutation correct but tone inconsistent; informal expressions mixed with formal attempts; closing formula is just "Cordialement"

B2 Performance (Score: ~11/15)

CriterionScoreWhy
RC3All points fully addressed with specific details; complete letter format; adequate word count
CC2Clear paragraph structure (Context → Argument → Request); varied connectors (or, de surcroît, en conséquence); one slightly abrupt transition
VOC2Good range (défectueux, retard de livraison, service après-vente); minimal repetition; occasional imprecision
GRAM2Conditional politeness, relative clauses, some subjunctive; occasional agreement errors but meaning always clear
SOC2Consistent formal register; appropriate salutation and closing formula (matching); one minor register slip ("ça" instead of "cela")

C1 Performance (Score: ~14/15)

CriterionScoreWhy
RC3All points addressed with rich, specific development; perfect letter format
CC3Sophisticated organization; varied, precise connectors; seamless transitions; the letter reads as one unified argument
VOC3Rich vocabulary (manquement aux engagements, préjudice subi, mise en conformité); natural collocations; no repetition
GRAM3Complex structures used confidently (passive, conditional past, subjunctive, complex relatives); virtually error-free
SOC2-3Perfect register throughout; culturally natural tone; the letter could be sent to a real French company without modification

💡 Notice the pattern: The jump from B1 to B2 is primarily about STRUCTURE (RC, CC) and REGISTER (SOC). The jump from B2 to C1 is primarily about SOPHISTICATION (VOC, GRAM). This means: if you are currently B1, focus first on letter format, paragraph organization, and register consistency. Grammar and vocabulary refinement come second.

1️⃣2️⃣ WHAT COULD GO WRONG — SCORING MISTAKES CANDIDATES MAKE

Mistake 1: Preparing Only Grammar and Vocabulary

What happens: The candidate has excellent grammar and rich vocabulary but writes a letter with no header, no Objet line, a basic salutation, and no closing formula. They score 3 on GRAM, 3 on VOC, but 1 on RC, 1 on SOC, and 2 on CC. Total: 10/15 — low B2 at best, despite excellent language skills.

The lesson: Letter conventions (RC, SOC) are not "extras" — they are core requirements.

Mistake 2: Memorizing Closing Formulas Without Understanding Matching Rules

What happens: The candidate memorizes "Veuillez agréer, Monsieur, l'expression de mes sentiments distingués" and uses it in every letter. But when the salutation is "Madame la Directrice," the closing formula must contain "Madame la Directrice" — not "Monsieur." The mismatch costs SOC points.

The lesson: The closing formula is not a standalone element — it is part of a system that includes the salutation (Ch. 10 + Ch. 34).

Mistake 3: Using Connectors as Decoration

What happens: The candidate scatters connectors throughout the letter to "look organized" — but uses them incorrectly. "En effet" (confirmation) is used where "cependant" (contrast) is needed. "Par conséquent" (consequence) appears where there is no cause-effect relationship. The examiner sees connectors but they signal the wrong relationships. CC score drops.

The lesson: Connectors must be ACCURATE, not just PRESENT. Fewer correct connectors score higher than many incorrect ones.

Mistake 4: Writing Too Much

What happens: The candidate writes 400 words, believing more is better. The letter contains digressions, repetitions, and filler. The examiner reads more errors (GRAM −), more repetition (VOC −), more off-topic content (RC −), and a diffuse structure (CC −).

The lesson: 230-270 words of focused, well-structured content beats 400 words of unfocused content every time.
