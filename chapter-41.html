📖 THE EXAM MOMENT — THE FIFTH COMMUNICATIVE POSTURE

You have now studied four complete letters representing four postures: the aggrieved customer (complaint), the deferential petitioner (request), the constructive citizen (suggestion), and the professional applicant (cover letter). Now you encounter the fifth and final posture.

You are a concerned reader expressing an opinion.

This letter type is unique in the TEF universe because it sits at the intersection of two tasks:

1️⃣ THE PROMPT

Situation :
Vous avez lu un article dans le journal Le Courrier de l'Ouest affirmant que les réseaux sociaux ont un impact majoritairement négatif sur les jeunes et qu'il faudrait interdire leur utilisation aux moins de seize ans. De nombreux lecteurs ont réagi à cet article. Vous souhaitez exprimer votre point de vue, qui est plus nuancé : vous reconnaissez certains dangers des réseaux sociaux, mais vous estimez que l'éducation au numérique est une meilleure solution que l'interdiction.

Consigne :
Vous écrivez une lettre au rédacteur en chef du journal Le Courrier de l'Ouest pour exprimer votre opinion. Dans votre lettre, vous devez :

  • faire référence à l'article et exposer votre position ;
  • reconnaître les dangers des réseaux sociaux tout en défendant leur utilité ;
  • proposer l'éducation au numérique comme alternative à l'interdiction.

Rédigez une lettre formelle d'au moins 200 mots.

Objet : Réaction à l'article sur les réseaux sociaux et les jeunes — Édition du 20 mars 2024

Monsieur le Rédacteur en chef,

À la suite de l'article paru dans votre édition du 20 mars dernier, consacré à l'impact des réseaux sociaux sur les jeunes, je souhaiterais apporter un point de vue que j'estime plus nuancé que la position défendue par l'auteur. En tant que père d'un adolescent et enseignant dans le secondaire, je suis quotidiennement confronté à cette problématique et ne saurais me satisfaire d'une approche aussi catégorique que l'interdiction pure et simple.

Certes, les dangers des réseaux sociaux pour les jeunes sont réels et ne doivent pas être minimisés. L'exposition précoce à des contenus inappropriés, le cyberharcèlement et les risques d'addiction constituent des menaces sérieuses pour le développement des adolescents. Cependant, il serait réducteur de ne considérer que les aspects négatifs de ces plateformes. En effet, les réseaux sociaux offrent également aux jeunes un espace d'expression créative, facilitent l'accès à l'information et permettent de développer des formes nouvelles de solidarité et d'engagement citoyen. Il me semble dès lors essentiel d'adopter une approche qui ne se limite pas à la prohibition.

C'est pourquoi je suis convaincu que l'éducation au numérique représente une alternative bien plus efficace que l'interdiction. Il est en effet indispensable que les jeunes apprennent à exercer leur esprit critique face aux contenus qu'ils consultent, à protéger leurs données personnelles et à utiliser ces outils de manière responsable. De tels apprentissages, dispensés dès l'école primaire et soutenus par un accompagnement parental éclairé, permettraient de former des citoyens numériques autonomes, capables de tirer profit des réseaux sociaux tout en se prémunissant contre leurs dérives.

J'espère que ces réflexions contribueront au débat sur cette question essentielle et encourageront d'autres lecteurs à partager leur point de vue.

Veuillez agréer, Monsieur le Rédacteur en chef, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Marc LEFÈVRE

Sentence 1

À la suite de l'article paru dans votre édition du 20 mars dernier, consacré à l'impact des réseaux sociaux sur les jeunes, je souhaiterais apporter un point de vue que j'estime plus nuancé que la position défendue par l'auteur.

À la suite de l'article paru dans votre édition du 20 mars dernier,"Following the article published in your edition of March 20." The standard opinion letter opening (Ch. 11, Ch. 18). "Paru" (published — past participle of "paraître") is more literary than "publié" — a subtle register elevation. "Votre édition du 20 mars dernier" — specific date reference, matching the Objet line and the date timeline.Task Completion — Point 1 (article referenced); Vocabulary — à la suite de, paru (formal); Coherence — date consistency

"J'estime" (I consider/I believe) is a formal opinion verb — more elevated than "je pense" (I think) or "je crois" (I believe). "Plus nuancé" — the comparative directly echoes the prompt's description ("votre point de vue, qui est plus nuancé"). Strategic function of Sentence 1: This sentence accomplishes three things simultaneously: (1) references the article (Required Point 1), (2) states the writer's position (nuanced, not purely negative or positive), and (3) establishes a tone of respectful disagreement (not attacking the author, but offering a different perspective). This triple function in a single sentence is efficient and demonstrates control.

Sentence 3

Certes, les dangers des réseaux sociaux pour les jeunes sont réels et ne doivent pas être minimisés.

SegmentAnnotationTEF Criterion
Certes,THE CONCESSION MARKER. "Certes" (certainly/admittedly) is the opening word of the concession-refutation structure (Ch. 24). It signals: "I am about to acknowledge something that supports the opposing view." This single word tells the examiner: "This candidate knows the concession-refutation technique." It is the most important word in the entire paragraph — it establishes the argumentative framework.Coherence — concession-refutation initiated; Vocabulary — B2 argumentation marker
les dangers des réseaux sociaux pour les jeunes sont réels"The dangers of social media for young people are real." The concession itself — the writer acknowledges that the article's premise (social media are dangerous for youth) has merit. This is not weakness — it is intellectual honesty. A writer who refuses to acknowledge any validity in the opposing view appears dogmatic. A writer who concedes valid points appears reasonable — and reasonable writers are more persuasive.Coherence — intellectual honesty; Task Completion — Point 2 (acknowledge dangers)
et ne doivent pas être minimisés"And must not be minimized." Passive voice (ne doivent pas être minimisés) + modal verb (devoir). This strengthens the concession: the writer is not just acknowledging the dangers; they are insisting that the dangers are serious and should not be dismissed. This is a powerful rhetorical move — by strongly conceding, the writer earns the right to strongly refute.Grammar — passive with modal; Sociolinguistic — strengthened concession

Concession strength analysis: Sentences 3–4 form a strong concession — the writer devotes two full sentences to acknowledging the opposing view's valid points. This is strategically important: a weak concession ("Yes, there are some problems, but...") sounds dismissive. A strong concession ("The dangers are real, serious, and specific — they include X, Y, and Z, and they must not be minimized") sounds intellectually honest. The stronger the concession, the more credible the refutation that follows.

Sentence 5

Cependant, il serait réducteur de ne considérer que les aspects négatifs de ces plateformes.

SegmentAnnotationTEF Criterion
Cependant,THE REFUTATION MARKER. "Cependant" (however) is the pivot word — it signals the transition from concession to refutation. The "Certes... cependant..." pair is the complete concession-refutation structure (Ch. 24). Together, they say: "Admittedly X is true. However, Y is also true." This is the most important argumentative structure in French formal writing.Coherence — refutation initiated; Vocabulary — B2 opposition connector
il serait réducteur de ne considérer que les aspects négatifs de ces plateformes"It would be reductive to consider only the negative aspects of these platforms." Impersonal conditional (il serait réducteur) — the same type of construction from Ch. 39 (il conviendrait), here used for argumentation rather than proposal. "Réducteur" (reductive/simplistic) is a powerful formal adjective — it means "oversimplified, failing to capture the full picture." The writer is not saying the article is wrong; they are saying it is incomplete. This is a more sophisticated critique than direct contradiction. "Ne... que" (only) — the restrictive construction. "Ne considérer que les aspects négatifs" = "to consider only the negative aspects."Grammar — impersonal conditional, ne...que restrictive; Vocabulary — réducteur (B2/C1 argumentation adjective); Coherence — the refutation challenges the article's scope, not its facts

1️⃣1️⃣ WHAT COULD GO WRONG — OPINION LETTER-SPECIFIC PITFALLS

Beyond the universal closing disasters (Ch. 36) and the general pitfalls applicable to all letter types, the opinion letter has six specific pitfalls that candidates must avoid.

Pitfall 1: Taking an Extreme Position

The error:

❌ Les réseaux sociaux sont une catastrophe absolue pour les jeunes. Il faut les interdire immédiatement et punir les parents qui laissent leurs enfants les utiliser.

Why it fails: The position is extreme, absolute, and aggressive. The prompt specifically says the writer's view is "plus nuancé" (more nuanced). Even if the prompt did not specify nuance, extreme positions in formal French writing signal lack of analytical depth. The TEF evaluates the quality of argumentation, not the strength of conviction. A nuanced position with evidence scores higher than an extreme position with passion.

What the examiner thinks: "The candidate cannot analyze a complex issue. They default to absolute statements instead of developing a reasoned argument. This is B1-level thinking expressed in formal-looking language."

Scoring impact:

CriterionImpact
Respect de la consigne🔴 Severe — the prompt requires a nuanced position
Cohérence et cohésion🟠 Moderate — no concession-refutation means no argumentation structure

The fix (from this chapter): Acknowledge both sides (Certes... Cependant...), then propose a balanced alternative. The strongest opinion letters are those that demonstrate the writer has considered multiple perspectives before arriving at their conclusion.

Pitfall 2: Forgetting the Concession

The error:

❌ L'article a complètement tort. Les réseaux sociaux sont formidables pour les jeunes. Ils apprennent beaucoup de choses en ligne et c'est très bien.

Why it fails: No concession — the writer dismisses the article entirely without acknowledging any valid points. This violates Required Point 2 ("reconnaître les dangers... tout en défendant leur utilité"). Beyond the prompt requirement, a refutation without concession sounds dogmatic and unconvincing. The reader thinks: "This person has not considered the other side."

What the examiner thinks: "The candidate cannot perform the concession-refutation technique. They present only one side of the argument. This is a coherence failure — the argumentation is unbalanced."

Scoring impact:

CriterionImpact
Respect de la consigne🔴 Severe — Point 2 requires acknowledging dangers
Cohérence et cohésion🔴 Severe — one-sided argument lacks structural balance

The fix (from this chapter): Always concede before refuting. Even two sentences of concession ("Certes, les dangers sont réels... Le cyberharcèlement constitue une menace sérieuse...") transform the letter from dismissive to analytical. The concession does not weaken your argument — it strengthens it by demonstrating intellectual honesty.

Pitfall 3: Forgetting to Reference the Article

The error:

❌ Je vous écris pour parler des réseaux sociaux et des jeunes. Je pense que l'éducation est importante.

Why it fails: No reference to the article that triggered the letter. The opinion letter is a response to published content — without the reference, it becomes a standalone essay in letter format. Required Point 1 explicitly requires referencing the article. The editor needs to know which article you are responding to.

What the examiner thinks: "The candidate has not read the prompt carefully. Point 1 requires referencing the article — this is missing."

Scoring impact:

CriterionImpact
Respect de la consigne🟠 Moderate — Point 1 partially unaddressed

The fix (from this chapter): Open with "À la suite de l'article paru dans votre édition du..." — this immediately establishes the letter as a response to specific content. Include the article's date and topic to demonstrate precise referencing.

Pitfall 4: Writing an Essay Instead of a Letter

The error:

❌ [No header, no salutation, no closing formula]
Les réseaux sociaux sont un sujet de débat important dans notre société. D'une part, ils présentent des dangers. D'autre part, ils offrent des avantages. En conclusion, l'éducation est la meilleure solution.

Why it fails: This is an essay, not a letter. The opinion letter is the type most susceptible to this error because its content (argumentation) resembles Section A (essay writing). But the task is Section B — a letter. All letter conventions must be present: header, salutation, body paragraphs, closing formula, signature. The candidate has produced good argumentation in the wrong format.

What the examiner thinks: "The candidate confused Section A and Section B. The argumentation is acceptable, but the letter structure is entirely missing. This is a task completion failure."

Scoring impact:

CriterionImpact
Respect de la consigne🔴 Severe — the task requires a letter, not an essay
Adaptation sociolinguistique🔴 Severe — no letter conventions demonstrated

The fix: Write the letter structure first (header, salutation, closing formula, signature), then fill in the argumentative content. The structure is a letter; the content is argumentation. Both are required. On paper, physically write the header at the top and the closing formula at the bottom before writing any body content.

The error:

❌ Franchement, je trouve ça n'importe quoi d'interdire les réseaux sociaux. Les jeunes, ils s'en fichent des interdictions de toute façon.

Pitfall 6: Failing to Propose an Alternative

The error:

❌ L'interdiction n'est pas une bonne idée. Les réseaux sociaux ont aussi des avantages. Voilà mon opinion.

Why it fails: The writer criticizes the article's position but offers nothing in its place. Required Point 3 specifically requires proposing digital education as an alternative. Beyond the prompt requirement, an opinion letter that only criticizes without proposing is intellectually incomplete — it says "you're wrong" without saying "here's what's right."

What the examiner thinks: "The candidate can identify problems but cannot propose solutions. Point 3 is unaddressed. The argument is incomplete."

Scoring impact:

CriterionImpact
Respect de la consigne🔴 Severe — Point 3 entirely missing
Cohérence et cohésion🟠 Moderate — the argument has no resolution

The fix (from this chapter): After the concession-refutation (P2), dedicate an entire paragraph (P3) to the alternative. Name it specifically ("l'éducation au numérique"), describe what it involves (three specific skills: critical thinking, data protection, responsible use), and explain how it would be implemented (school programs from primary level + informed parental support). The more concrete the alternative, the more persuasive the argument.

Chapter 41 is the only chapter that demonstrates the concession-refutation structure, the subjunctive in opinion writing, the conviction progression technique, and the bridge between Section B and Section A in a complete letter.

The key lessons unique to this chapter:

  1. The concession-refutation (Certes... Cependant...) is the opinion letter's defining structure. No other letter type requires it. No other argumentative technique carries as much weight with the examiner. Using it correctly signals B2 argumentation competence instantly.

  2. Strong concession produces strong refutation. Devoting two full sentences to acknowledging the opposing view's valid points (Sentences 3–4) makes the refutation (Sentences 5–7) more credible. Weak concession ("Yes, there are some problems, but...") produces weak persuasion. The valley-and-recovery emotional pattern is unique to this letter type and gives it its distinctive rhetorical power.

  3. The opinion letter uses the most impersonal constructions of all letter types. Three impersonal constructions (il serait réducteur, il me semble, il est indispensable) in one letter — because the opinion letter deals with general principles, not personal situations. Impersonal constructions present ideas as universally relevant rather than individually motivated.

  4. The subjunctive appears naturally in opinion writing. Expressions of necessity (il est indispensable que), doubt, and judgment trigger the subjunctive — and these expressions cluster in opinion letters where the writer argues for what should happen. This makes the opinion letter the most natural context for demonstrating subjunctive mastery.

  5. Ne saurais is the elevated conditional that signals B2/C1 register. This construction appears only in the opinion letter because it expresses intellectual impossibility — "I cannot accept this position because my understanding of the issue prevents me." It is a powerful, formal, and memorable construction that no other letter type naturally calls for.

  6. Tout en + present participle expresses the nuance that defines the opinion letter. "Benefiting from social media while protecting against their excesses" — this simultaneous contrast is the grammatical expression of the nuanced position the prompt requires. It captures in one construction what the entire letter argues across three paragraphs.

  7. The conviction progression (modest → strong) mirrors the argumentative development. You earn the right to be firmly convinced by first observing, then analyzing, then concluding. Starting with "je suis convaincu" before presenting evidence is presumptuous. Arriving at "je suis convaincu" after evidence is earned. The examiner recognizes this progression as argumentative maturity.

  8. The opinion letter is the bridge to Section A. Every argumentation skill demonstrated here — concession-refutation, opinion expressions, logical connectors, impersonal constructions, subjunctive triggers, conviction progression — transfers directly to essay writing. Mastering the opinion letter means mastering 70% of Section A's skill requirements.
