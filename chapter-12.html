📖 THE EXAM MOMENT

You have studied the header (Ch. 6-9), the salutation (Ch. 10), and the first sentence (Ch. 11). You understand what each element should look like when done correctly. But knowing what is correct is only half the battle. The other half is knowing what is wrong — and why it is wrong — so that you can recognize errors in your own writing before the examiner does.

This chapter is different from the previous ones. It is not about building something. It is about preventing destruction. Every mistake cataloged here is something that real TEF candidates have done — repeatedly — and that has cost them real points. Some of these mistakes are catastrophic (costing 3-5 points across criteria). Others are moderate (costing 1-2 points). All of them are avoidable.

Think of this chapter as a minefield map. The territory between your header and your first paragraph is dense with potential errors. This chapter marks every mine so you can walk through safely.

2️⃣ THE 15 OPENING DISASTERS — COMPLETE CATALOG

❌ MISTAKE #1: Using "Bonjour" as a Written Salutation

Severity: 🔴 CATASTROPHIC
Criteria affected: SOC (severe), RC (moderate)

What it looks like:

Bonjour,

Je vous écris pour vous informer de...

Or worse:

Bonjour Monsieur,

Je vous écris...

Why candidates make it:

In spoken French and in informal emails, Bonjour is universal. Candidates who have spent more time speaking French than writing formal French naturally reach for Bonjour as their default greeting. It feels polite. It feels natural. It feels safe.

It is none of these things in a formal letter.

Why it costs points:

Bonjour is an oral greeting. It belongs to spoken interaction — when you walk into a room, when you answer the phone, when you begin a face-to-face conversation. In written formal French, the salutation must identify the recipient by title or form of address. Bonjour identifies no one. It establishes no relationship. It declares no register.

The examiner reads Bonjour and immediately registers: "This candidate does not know the difference between spoken and written formal conventions." This is a fundamental SOC failure — not a minor slip, but a structural misunderstanding of the task.

The correction:

Instead of...Write...
Bonjour,Madame, Monsieur, (unknown recipient)
Bonjour Monsieur,Monsieur, or Monsieur le Directeur,
Bonjour Madame la Directrice,Madame la Directrice,
Bonjour à tous,Mesdames, Messieurs,

The prevention strategy:

During Step 4 of your planning method (Ch. 5), you pre-select your salutation from the bank in Ch. 10. Bonjour is not in the bank. If you follow the planning method, you will never write Bonjour because you will have already chosen the correct salutation before your pen touches the letter body.

🔴 This is the #1 most common opening disaster on the TEF. It is also the most easily preventable. Memorize ONE salutation — Madame, Monsieur, — and use it whenever you are unsure. It is universally correct for formal letters.

❌ MISTAKE #2: Using "Cher/Chère" with an Unknown Recipient

Severity: 🟠 SEVERE
Criteria affected: SOC (significant)

What it looks like:

Cher Monsieur le Directeur,

Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de...

Or:

Chère Madame,

J'ai l'honneur de porter à votre connaissance...

Why candidates make it:

Direct translation from English. "Dear Sir" = Cher Monsieur. It feels like the "formal" thing to do because in English, "Dear" IS the formal salutation opener.

Why it costs points:

As explained in Ch. 10, Section 6, Cher/Chère in French implies personal acquaintance and warmth. Using it with someone you have never met signals either:

  • Inappropriate familiarity (you are acting as though you know the person when you do not)
  • Anglicism (you are translating English conventions into French)

Neither impression helps your SOC score.

The correction:

Instead of...Write...
Cher Monsieur le Directeur,Monsieur le Directeur,
Chère Madame, (to a stranger)Madame,
Cher Monsieur Dupont, (first contact)Monsieur Dupont, or Monsieur,

When Cher/Chère IS acceptable:

Only when the prompt establishes an existing personal or professional relationship:

  • "Vous écrivez à un collègue avec qui vous travaillez depuis trois ans" → Cher collègue, is acceptable
  • "Vous écrivez à votre professeur, M. Durand, que vous connaissez bien" → Cher Monsieur Durand, is acceptable

If the prompt does not establish such a relationship, do not use Cher/Chère.

The prevention strategy:

Ask yourself: "Does the prompt say I KNOW this person personally?" If yes → Cher/Chère is possible. If no → use the title-based salutation without Cher/Chère. When in doubt, leave it out.

❌ MISTAKE #3: Missing the Objet Line Entirely

Severity: 🟠 SEVERE
Criteria affected: SOC (significant), RC (moderate)

What it looks like:

                              Toulouse, le 15 mars 2025

Madame, Monsieur,

Je me permets de vous écrire...

The date jumps directly to the salutation. There is no Objet line.

Why candidates make it:

Many candidates have never been taught that French formal letters include an Objet line. In English, formal letters do not have a subject line (emails do, but letters typically do not). Candidates transfer the English convention.

Others know the Objet line exists but forget it in the rush to start writing the body.

Why it costs points:

The Objet line is a structural element of French formal letters (Ch. 9). Its absence is visible during the examiner's first scan (Ch. 4). It creates a gap in the visual structure that the examiner registers as "missing element." Under SOC, it signals incomplete convention awareness. Under RC, it represents a missing component of the letter format.

Beyond the scoring impact, the missing Objet line means the examiner enters your first paragraph without knowing your purpose. They must discover it from your text rather than from the label. This reduces cognitive fluency (Ch. 4) and makes your letter harder to process.

The correction:

Insert the Objet line between the date and the salutation:

                              Toulouse, le 15 mars 2025

Objet : Réclamation concernant la commande n° 4521

Madame, Monsieur,

The prevention strategy:

The Objet line is part of your Phase 2 writing sequence (Ch. 5/6/7/8/9). After writing the date, write the Objet IMMEDIATELY — before the salutation, before the first sentence, before anything else. Make it automatic: Date → Objet → Salutation. No exceptions.

❌ MISTAKE #4: No Sender or Recipient Block — Letter Starts with Salutation

Severity: 🟠 SEVERE
Criteria affected: SOC (significant), RC (significant)

What it looks like:

Madame, Monsieur,

Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de la commande n° 4521...

The letter begins directly with the salutation. There is no sender block, no recipient block, no date, no Objet line. The entire header is missing.

Why candidates make it:

Some candidates view the letter as "the text you write" — starting from the salutation and ending with the closing formula. They do not realize that the header (sender, recipient, date, Objet) is part of the letter. Others run out of time for the header because they did not plan (Ch. 5) and started writing the body immediately.

Why it costs points:

A letter without a header is structurally incomplete. It fails the "Does this look like a letter?" test that the examiner performs during the visual scan (Ch. 4). The examiner sees a text that starts with a salutation and ends with a closing formula — but without the header, it looks like a fragment of a letter, not a complete one.

This costs points on RC (incomplete format) and SOC (missing conventions). It also eliminates the primacy effect benefit (Ch. 4) — without a header, the first visual impression is "incomplete."

The correction:

Always include the complete header before the salutation:

[Sender block]

                    [Recipient block]

                    [City], le [date]

Objet : [Subject]

[Salutation],

The prevention strategy:

Follow the Phase 2 writing sequence from Ch. 6-9. Write the header FIRST, before any content. The header takes 3-4 minutes. It is the structural foundation of your letter.

If you are running critically short on time and must choose between a complete header and a complete body, include at minimum:

  • The salutation (absolutely essential)
  • The Objet line (highly important)
  • A brief sender identification (name + city at minimum)

Even a minimal header is better than no header.

❌ MISTAKE #5: Mismatching the Salutation and the Closing Formula

Severity: 🟠 SEVERE
Criteria affected: SOC (severe)

What it looks like:

Opening:

Monsieur le Directeur,

Closing:

Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

The salutation says Monsieur le Directeur but the closing says Madame, Monsieur. The terms of address do not match.

Why candidates make it:

Candidates memorize one closing formula (usually "...agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées") and use it in every letter, regardless of the salutation. They do not realize that the closing formula must echo the salutation's term of address.

Why it costs points:

The three-point consistency chain (Ch. 7, Ch. 10) is a fundamental rule of French epistolary convention:

Recipient Block ↔ Salutation ↔ Closing Formula

All three must use the same form of address. Breaking this chain signals that the candidate has memorized formulas without understanding the system. Under SOC, this is a significant error — it demonstrates surface-level knowledge rather than genuine sociolinguistic competence.

The correction:

SalutationClosing Must Contain
Monsieur le Directeur,...agréer, Monsieur le Directeur, l'expression de...
Madame, Monsieur,...agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de...
Madame la Maire,...agréer, Madame la Maire, l'expression de...
Madame,...agréer, Madame, l'expression de...

The prevention strategy:

In Step 4 of your planning method (Ch. 5), you choose BOTH the salutation AND the closing formula at the same time. Write them both on your planning sheet. Before writing, verify that the term of address is identical in both. This 10-second check prevents one of the most damaging SOC errors.

❌ MISTAKE #6: Writing the First Sentence as a Copy of the Prompt

Severity: 🟡 MODERATE TO SEVERE
Criteria affected: VOC (significant), RC (moderate)

What it looks like:

Prompt: "Vous avez commandé un appareil électroménager sur le site Internet de la société Électro-Plus. L'appareil est arrivé avec trois semaines de retard et il était endommagé à la réception."

First sentence: "J'ai commandé un appareil électroménager sur le site Internet de la société Électro-Plus. L'appareil est arrivé avec trois semaines de retard et il était endommagé à la réception."

The candidate has copied the prompt almost word for word, changing only "Vous avez" to "J'ai."

Why candidates make it:

Under exam pressure, paraphrasing feels risky. The prompt provides the information in clear French. Copying it feels safe — "at least it's correct." But correct copying is not what the TEF evaluates. The TEF evaluates your ability to produce French, not to reproduce it.

Why it costs points:

The examiner recognizes prompt text immediately — they have the prompt in front of them. When they see the same words in your letter, they register: "This candidate cannot paraphrase." Under VOC, copying demonstrates zero vocabulary range (the words are not yours). Under RC, it can suggest that the candidate is struggling to reformulate and may not fully understand the task at a productive level.

The correction:

Paraphrase the prompt information using your own vocabulary and formal structures:

Prompt TextYour Paraphrase
"Vous avez commandé un appareil électroménager sur le site Internet de la société Électro-Plus""Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de la commande n° 4521, effectuée le 15 mars dernier sur votre site"
"L'appareil est arrivé avec trois semaines de retard""dont la livraison a accusé un retard de trois semaines par rapport au délai annoncé"
"il était endommagé à la réception""et dont l'article présentait de nombreux dommages à la réception"

The prevention strategy:

When reading the prompt (Step 1 of planning, Ch. 5), extract facts (dates, names, reference numbers) but NOT sentences. On your planning grid, note keywords, not phrases. When you write, reconstruct the information in your own words. The facts remain the same; the expression changes.

💡 A useful technique: Read the prompt, then close your eyes and think about what happened in your OWN words. Then write those words. This forces you to paraphrase naturally rather than copying.

❌ MISTAKE #7: Starting with "Je suis..." + Emotion

Severity: 🟡 MODERATE
Criteria affected: SOC (moderate), VOC (moderate)

What it looks like:

Madame, Monsieur,

Je suis très mécontent de votre service.

Or:

Monsieur le Directeur,

Je suis furieux parce que mon produit est arrivé cassé.

Why candidates make it:

Emotional expression feels natural when you are playing the role of a dissatisfied customer. English complaint letters often start with "I am writing to express my dissatisfaction..." — which begins with the emotion. Candidates transfer this approach.

Why it costs points:

In French formal convention, the first sentence establishes context (what happened), not emotion (how you feel). Emotion appears later — in Paragraph 2 or 3 — and always in measured, formal language (Ch. 3, Dimension 4: Tonal Register).

Starting with emotion before context creates several problems:

  • It sounds reactive rather than measured — undermining the formal tone
  • It misses the opportunity to demonstrate formal vocabulary in the first sentence
  • "Je suis très mécontent" is B1-level vocabulary — it does not demonstrate B2 range
  • "Furieux" and "cassé" are informal register markers

The correction:

Emotion-First Opening (Wrong)Context-First Opening (Correct)
Je suis très mécontent de votre service.Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet des difficultés que j'ai rencontrées avec votre service client.
Je suis furieux parce que mon produit est arrivé cassé.Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de la commande n° 4521, dont le produit livré présentait de nombreux dommages.
Je suis déçu par votre hôtel.Je me permets de vous écrire concernant mon séjour du 15 au 18 mars dans votre établissement, qui s'est avéré particulièrement décevant.

The rule: Facts first. Feelings second. And feelings always in formal vocabulary.

The prevention strategy:

Use one of the six default first sentence structures from Ch. 11. None of them start with "Je suis + emotion." All of them start with context or purpose. If you follow the templates, you automatically avoid this error.

❌ MISTAKE #8: Using "Mademoiselle"

Severity: 🟡 MODERATE
Criteria affected: SOC (moderate)

What it looks like:

Mademoiselle la Directrice,

Or in the body:

...je vous prie d'agréer, Mademoiselle, l'expression de...

Why candidates make it:

In older French textbooks and in some cultural representations, Mademoiselle is presented as the form of address for unmarried women. Candidates who learned French from older materials may use it thinking it is respectful.

Why it costs points:

Since 2012, French government guidelines have recommended abandoning Mademoiselle in official correspondence. The term is considered:

  • Discriminatory — it reveals marital status, which is irrelevant in professional/administrative contexts
  • Outdated — modern French uses Madame for all women regardless of age or marital status
  • Potentially offensive — some women find the distinction between Madame and Mademoiselle patronizing

Using Mademoiselle on the TEF signals that the candidate is using outdated conventions — a SOC concern.

The correction:

Always use Madame for any female recipient. Never use Mademoiselle.

Instead of...Write...
Mademoiselle,Madame,
Mademoiselle la Directrice,Madame la Directrice,
Chère Mademoiselle,Chère Madame, (if Cher/Chère is appropriate) or Madame,

The prevention strategy:

Simple: remove Mademoiselle from your vocabulary for formal letters. It does not exist in your repertoire. There is only Madame.

❌ MISTAKE #9: Capitalizing the Month in the Date Line

Severity: 🟡 MODERATE
Criteria affected: SOC (minor-moderate)

What it looks like:

Toulouse, le 15 Mars 2025

Why candidates make it:

In English, months are capitalized: March, April, May. English speakers automatically capitalize months in French without realizing it is wrong.

Why it costs points:

As explained in Ch. 8, French months are common nouns — they are not capitalized. Capitalizing the month is a visible anglicism that signals English-language interference. Under SOC, it suggests that the candidate is applying English conventions rather than French ones.

In isolation, this is a minor error. But it rarely appears in isolation. A candidate who capitalizes the month often also makes other anglicisms (capitalizing days of the week, using English-style date order, etc.). The examiner sees a pattern.

The correction:

Toulouse, le 15 mars 2025

Lowercase mars. Always.

The prevention strategy:

Memorize the rule from Ch. 8: French months and days of the week are ALWAYS lowercase. Write it on your planning sheet if needed: "mois = minuscule."

❌ MISTAKE #10: Merging the Salutation with the First Sentence

Severity: 🟡 MODERATE
Criteria affected: SOC (moderate), CC (minor)

What it looks like:

Madame, Monsieur, je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de la commande n° 4521 dont le produit livré présentait de nombreux dommages...

The salutation and the first sentence are on the same line, merged into one continuous text.

Why candidates make it:

In spoken French, you might say "Monsieur, je voudrais..." in one breath. Candidates transfer this spoken flow to written format. On computer-based exams, candidates sometimes forget to press Enter after the salutation.

Why it costs points:

The salutation is a standalone structural element (Ch. 10). It sits on its own line, ends with a comma, and is followed by a blank line before the first paragraph. Merging it with the first sentence eliminates this structural separation and makes the letter look like a single block of text beginning with a greeting.

Under SOC, it signals that the candidate does not understand the visual conventions of French letter formatting. Under CC, it reduces visual organization — the examiner cannot immediately distinguish the salutation from the body.

The correction:

Madame, Monsieur,

Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de la commande n° 4521...

Salutation on its own line. Blank line. First paragraph begins.

The prevention strategy:

After writing the salutation, physically skip a line (paper) or press Enter twice (computer) before writing anything else. Make the blank line a reflex.

❌ MISTAKE #11: Using "À qui de droit" as a Salutation

Severity: 🟡 MODERATE
Criteria affected: SOC (moderate), RC (minor)

What it looks like:

À qui de droit,

Je me permets de vous écrire...

Why candidates make it:

Direct translation of "To whom it may concern." In English, this is a standard formal opening when the recipient is unknown. Candidates assume the French equivalent works the same way.

Why it costs points:

À qui de droit exists in French, but it is extremely impersonal and is typically used only in legal documents or mass-distributed notices. In a letter to a specific organization (which every TEF prompt describes), it signals:

  • The candidate did not identify the recipient (RC concern)
  • The candidate chose the laziest possible salutation instead of the appropriate one (SOC concern)

TEF prompts ALWAYS tell you who to write to — a company, a director, a mayor, an editor. There is always enough information to choose a proper salutation. À qui de droit is never necessary.

The correction:

Use the appropriate salutation based on the recipient (Ch. 10):

Instead of...Write...
À qui de droit, (to a company)Madame, Monsieur,
À qui de droit, (to a director)Monsieur le Directeur, or Madame la Directrice,
À qui de droit, (to any identified recipient)The appropriate title-based salutation

The prevention strategy:

Step 1 of your planning method (Ch. 5) extracts the recipient. Step 4 selects the salutation based on the recipient. If you follow the planning method, À qui de droit never enters consideration.

❌ MISTAKE #12: Forgetting the Comma After the Salutation

Severity: 🟡 MINOR-MODERATE
Criteria affected: SOC (minor)

What it looks like:

Madame, Monsieur

Je me permets de vous écrire...

Or:

Monsieur le Directeur

Je me permets...

The salutation has no comma at the end.

Why candidates make it:

In English, some letter formats use a colon after the salutation ("Dear Sir:") or no punctuation at all. Candidates may not realize that French salutations require a comma.

Why it costs points:

The comma after the salutation is a French convention. Its absence makes the salutation look incomplete. Under SOC, it is a minor but noticeable formatting error.

This is one of the least damaging errors on this list, but it contributes to the overall impression. Combined with other small errors, it can tip a borderline SOC score downward.

The correction:

Always end the salutation with a comma:

Madame, Monsieur, Monsieur le Directeur,

The prevention strategy:

When writing the salutation, write the comma as part of the salutation itself — not as an afterthought. Think of "Madame, Monsieur," as one unit, including both commas (internal and final).

❌ MISTAKE #13: Writing "Object :" Instead of "Objet :"

Severity: 🟡 MINOR-MODERATE
Criteria affected: SOC (minor), VOC (minor)

What it looks like:

Object : Réclamation concernant la commande n° 4521

Or:

Sujet : Réclamation concernant la commande n° 4521

Or:

RE : Réclamation concernant la commande n° 4521

Why candidates make it:

  • "Object" — English spelling transferred to French
  • "Sujet" — Logical French translation of "subject" but not the convention for letter subject lines
  • "RE:" — Email/English convention

Why it costs points:

The standard term for a letter subject line in French is Objet — not Object, not Sujet, not RE. Using any other term signals either anglicism (Object, RE) or unfamiliarity with the convention (Sujet).

The correction:

Always use Objet with a capital O:

Objet : Réclamation concernant la commande n° 4521

The prevention strategy:

Memorize the word: Objet. Five letters. O-B-J-E-T. Not Object. Not Sujet. Objet.

❌ MISTAKE #14: Using Informal Vocabulary in the First Sentence

Severity: 🟡 MODERATE TO SEVERE (depends on extent)
Criteria affected: SOC (significant), VOC (significant)

What it looks like:

Madame, Monsieur,

Je vous écris parce que j'ai un truc qui marche pas et du coup je suis pas content.

Why candidates make it:

The candidate's spoken French is significantly stronger than their written formal French. Under exam pressure, they default to their spoken register. The planning method was either not used or not followed.

Why it costs points:

This first sentence contains five register violations in one sentence:

ViolationRegister BreakCorrect Alternative
un truc🔴 Familier — slang for "a thing"un article, un produit, un appareil
qui marche pas🔴 Missing ne — spoken Frenchqui ne fonctionne pas
du coup🔴 Informal connectorpar conséquent, en conséquence
je suis pas content🔴 Missing ne + basic vocabularyje suis mécontent / cette situation est regrettable
parce que🟡 Not wrong but basicen raison de, étant donné que

Five register breaks in the first sentence sets a catastrophic anchor (Ch. 4). The examiner's initial classification is "weak register awareness" and confirmation bias ensures they find more problems throughout the letter.

The correction:

Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet du produit commandé sous la référence n° 4521, qui ne fonctionne pas correctement. Cette situation est d'autant plus regrettable que j'étais, jusqu'à présent, un client satisfait de vos services.

The prevention strategy:

Use the first sentence templates from Ch. 11. They are pre-built in formal register. No improvisation is needed. The 10-point register check from Ch. 3 should be applied to your first sentence before moving to the second sentence.

❌ MISTAKE #15: Including the Sender's Age, Nationality, or Personal Details in the Header

Severity: 🟡 MINOR
Criteria affected: SOC (minor)

What it looks like:

Sophie MARTIN
Née le 15 avril 1985
Nationalité française
45, rue des Lilas
31000 Toulouse

Or:

Sophie MARTIN
32 ans, célibataire
45, rue des Lilas
31000 Toulouse

Why candidates make it:

Confusion between a letter header and a CV header. Candidates who have recently studied CV formatting transfer those elements (date of birth, nationality, marital status) to the letter header.

Why it costs points:

The sender block of a letter contains contact information: name, address, and optionally phone/email (Ch. 6). Personal data such as date of birth, age, nationality, and marital status do NOT belong in a letter header. They belong on a CV — and only when relevant.

Including personal data in the letter header makes the header look like a CV header, which signals confusion about document types. Under SOC, it is a minor but noticeable convention error.

The correction:

Remove all personal data from the sender block. Include only:

  • Name
  • Address
  • Phone/email (optional)
Sophie MARTIN
45, rue des Lilas
31000 Toulouse

The prevention strategy:

Remember: the sender block is about how to reach you, not who you are. Name = identification. Address = location. Phone/email = contact method. Everything else (age, nationality, profession, marital status) belongs in the letter body if relevant, or on a separate CV.

6️⃣ B1 vs. B2 vs. C1 — ERROR PATTERNS AT THREE LEVELS

B1 Error Pattern (3-5 Mistakes Typical)

The B1 candidate typically makes:

  • Bonjour as salutation (Mistake #1)
  • Missing Objet line (Mistake #3)
  • Informal vocabulary in first sentence (Mistake #14)
  • Possibly missing header elements (Mistake #4)
  • Possibly copied prompt text (Mistake #6)

Result: The opening signals fundamental gaps in formal writing conventions. The anchor is negative. SOC is 0-1.

B2 Error Pattern (0-1 Mistakes Typical)

The B2 candidate typically makes:

  • Possibly capitalizing the month (Mistake #9) — a residual anglicism
  • Possibly a slightly vague Objet line (not a "mistake" per se, but a missed opportunity)
  • All other elements are correct

Result: The opening signals competence. Minor imperfections exist but do not break the formal impression. The anchor is positive. SOC is 2-3.

C1 Error Pattern (0 Mistakes)

The C1 candidate makes no opening errors. Every element is present, correctly formatted, and consistent. The first sentence is sophisticated, using participial openings or literary constructions.

Result: The opening signals mastery. The anchor is strongly positive. SOC is 3.

7️⃣ THE EMERGENCY PROTOCOL — WHAT TO DO IF YOU REALIZE YOU MADE AN OPENING MISTAKE

On Paper

If you notice a mistake after writing it:

Mistake TypeEmergency Fix
Wrong salutation (Bonjour)Draw a single clean line through Bonjour. Write the correct salutation (Madame, Monsieur,) above or below it. One clean correction is professional — it shows you caught your own error.
Missing Objet lineIf there is space between the date and the salutation, insert it. If not, write it in the margin with an arrow indicating its position. This is not ideal but is better than a missing Objet.
Capitalized monthDraw a single line through the capital letter and write the lowercase version next to it. Minimal visual disruption.
Mismatched closing formulaFix the closing formula when you reach it — this is easier than fixing the salutation. Change the closing to match the salutation.

On Computer

If you notice a mistake:

Mistake TypeEmergency Fix
Any mistakeSimply edit it. The computer allows unlimited corrections. There is no trace of the original error. This is one of the major advantages of computer-based testing.

💡 The key principle: A corrected error is always better than an uncorrected error. On paper, a single clean correction line shows the examiner that you know the rule — you simply made a slip that you caught. An uncorrected error shows the examiner that you do not know the rule at all. Always correct if you notice the mistake.
