📖 THE EXAM MOMENT

You have written your header, your salutation, and your Paragraph 1. You followed the guidance from Chapters 13–18. Your P1 is solid: you established context, referenced the relevant situation, set the tone, and previewed your direction.

Now you look at the blank space below P1. You know what you want to argue in P2. You know your main point. You know the evidence you want to present.

But you freeze.

How do you get from P1 to P2?

Do you just... start a new paragraph? Do you need a transition word? A transition sentence? Should you repeat something from P1? Should P2 begin with a connector?

This moment — the gap between the end of P1 and the beginning of P2 — is where many TEF candidates lose cohesion points without understanding why. Their P1 is good. Their P2 is good. But the letter reads as two separate blocks rather than one flowing argument. The examiner marks the cohesion score down, and the candidate never knows what went wrong.

This chapter eliminates that problem permanently.

1️⃣ WHAT IS THE CHALLENGE, IN PLAIN LANGUAGE?

The Paragraph Gap Problem

In spoken French, transitions happen naturally. You say "Alors..." or "Du coup..." or "En fait..." and the conversation flows. In formal written French, these oral connectors are prohibited (Ch. 3). You need written transition mechanisms that create logical flow between paragraphs.

The challenge is threefold:

Challenge 1: The shift in function
P1 and P2 serve fundamentally different functions. P1 establishes context (who, what, why). P2 develops an argument (evidence, reasoning, persuasion). Moving from context to argument requires a functional pivot — a signal to the reader that you are now moving from "here is the situation" to "here is what I think about it" or "here is the evidence."

Challenge 2: The cohesion requirement
The TEF scoring criterion Cohérence et cohésion evaluates whether your letter reads as a unified text or as a collection of separate paragraphs. The transition between P1 and P2 is the first major test of this criterion. If the examiner senses a "jump" between P1 and P2, the cohesion score drops.

Challenge 3: The register constraint
The oral transitions you instinctively reach for (alors, du coup, en fait, bon) are all prohibited in formal register. You need formal transition mechanisms that feel natural, not forced.

What "Transition" Actually Means

A transition is not just a connector word at the start of P2. It is a logical bridge — a mechanism that:

  1. Closes the P1 idea (signals that the context is complete)
  2. Opens the P2 idea (signals that the argument is beginning)
  3. Connects the two (shows the logical relationship: cause, consequence, contrast, elaboration)

This bridge can be achieved through three different mechanisms, each operating at a different level:

MechanismLevelHow It WorksExample
Connector-led transitionWord/phrase levelA logical connector at the start of P2 signals the relationshipEn effet, ... / C'est pourquoi ... / Or, ...
Pronoun reference transitionSentence levelP2's first sentence refers back to a key element from P1 using a pronoun or demonstrativeCette situation... / Face à ce constat... / C'est dans ce contexte que...
Thematic echo transitionConceptual levelP2's opening picks up a word or concept from P1's closing and develops it in a new directionP1 ends with "...l'entretien des espaces verts." P2 begins with "L'état actuel de ces espaces..."

The most effective transitions combine two or even all three mechanisms simultaneously. This chapter teaches you how to use each one, and how to combine them.

2️⃣ WHY THIS SPECIFIC TRANSITION MATTERS MORE THAN ANY OTHER

The P1-P2 Transition Is the Most Important Transition in Your Letter

Your letter contains several transitions:

  • Salutation → P1 (handled by the first sentence hook, Ch. 11)
  • P1 → P2 (this chapter)
  • P2 → P3 (handled within Block 5 chapters)
  • P3 → Closing (handled in Ch. 32)

Of these, the P1 → P2 transition carries the most weight for three reasons:

Reason 1: It marks the shift from setup to substance
P1 is the setup. P2 is where you prove you can argue, analyze, and persuade. The transition between them is where the examiner first sees your argumentative ability. A smooth transition signals intellectual maturity. An abrupt transition signals a writer who can describe situations but cannot build arguments.

Reason 2: It is the first paragraph break the examiner encounters
The examiner reads your letter linearly. The first paragraph break they encounter is the P1-P2 boundary. If this break is smooth, they develop confidence that the letter is well-structured. If it is jarring, they develop concern — and they read P2 and P3 with heightened critical attention.

Reason 3: It demonstrates a skill that B1 writers lack
B1-level writers typically write paragraphs as independent units. They state one thing, then state another thing. The paragraphs are adjacent but not connected. B2-level writers create paragraphs that are linked — each paragraph grows from the previous one. The P1-P2 transition is the clearest place where this B2 skill is visible or invisible.

What the Examiner Sees

Here is what the P1-P2 boundary looks like at three levels:

B1 (paragraphs as separate blocks):

[P1]...J'ai commandé un produit qui est arrivé en retard et abîmé.

[P2] Le service client de votre entreprise est mauvais. J'ai appelé trois fois...

The examiner's reaction: "There is no connection between these paragraphs. The candidate jumped from describing the problem to attacking customer service. Why? How does one lead to the other?"

B2 (paragraphs as linked blocks):

[P1]...J'ai commandé un produit qui est arrivé avec un retard considérable et présentait plusieurs dommages visibles.

[P2] Face à cette situation, j'ai naturellement tenté de joindre votre service client afin de trouver une solution. Or, malgré deux appels téléphoniques...

The examiner's reaction: "The candidate moves logically from the problem to their attempt to resolve it. 'Face à cette situation' bridges the two paragraphs. 'Or' introduces a new complication. The logic flows."

C1 (paragraphs as an organic argument):

[P1]...un appareil présentant plusieurs dommages visibles, rendant son utilisation impossible.

[P2] C'est dans ce contexte que j'ai pris l'initiative de contacter votre service client, convaincu qu'une solution rapide serait trouvée. Force est de constater, cependant, que mes deux tentatives de communication téléphonique se sont avérées infructueuses...

The examiner's reaction: "Excellent cohesion. 'C'est dans ce contexte que' connects directly to P1. 'Convaincu qu'une solution rapide serait trouvée' sets up the contrast with what actually happened. 'Force est de constater, cependant, que' delivers the contrast elegantly. This is a writer who thinks in paragraphs, not sentences."

3️⃣ MECHANISM 1: CONNECTOR-LED TRANSITIONS

What They Are

A connector-led transition uses a logical connector at the very beginning of P2 to signal the relationship between P1 and P2. This is the simplest and most common transition mechanism.

Which Connectors Work at the P1-P2 Boundary

Not all connectors are appropriate for paragraph-opening positions. Some connectors work within sentences or between sentences within a paragraph, but are too weak to bridge paragraphs. Others are strong enough to carry the weight of a paragraph transition.

Connectors That Work at the P1-P2 Boundary (Paragraph-Level Strength):

ConnectorMeaningLogical RelationshipBest For Which Letter Types
En effetIndeed / In factElaboration — P2 develops or provides evidence for what P1 statedAll types — when P2 explains or proves P1's claim
OrHowever / Now (formal)Complication — P2 introduces a problem or contradiction within the situation described in P1Complaints (the complication after the context), Opinion letters (the counterpoint)
C'est pourquoiThis is why / For this reasonConsequence — P2 is a direct result of the situation described in P1Suggestions (concern → proposal), Requests (need → request justification)
C'est la raison pour laquelleThis is the reason whyConsequence (more formal than c'est pourquoi)Any type — formal variant
CependantHoweverContrast/Opposition — P2 presents a different angle from P1Opinion letters (concession → disagreement), Response letters (acknowledgment → objection)
ToutefoisHowever / NeverthelessContrast/Opposition (slightly more formal than cependant)Same as cependant — interchangeable in most contexts
NéanmoinsNeverthelessContrast/Opposition (strongest of the three)When the contrast is strong — firm disagreement, significant complication
En outreFurthermore / MoreoverAddition — P2 adds a new dimension to P1When P2 introduces additional evidence or a second problem
De plusFurthermore / In additionAddition (slightly less formal than en outre)Same function, wider usage
De surcroîtMoreover / What is moreAddition (very formal, often introduces an escalation)Complaints — when P2 adds a worse problem on top of P1's problem
Par ailleursFurthermore / BesidesAddition of a different angle — P2 adds a point that is related but distinctWhen P2 shifts to a different aspect of the same issue
AinsiThus / In this wayIllustration/Consequence — P2 illustrates or follows from P1When P2 provides a concrete example of P1's general statement
En conséquenceAs a consequenceDirect consequenceWhen P2 describes the direct result of P1's situation

Connectors That Do NOT Work at the P1-P2 Boundary

ConnectorWhy It Fails as a Paragraph Opener
Aussi (meaning "also")Too weak — "also" does not create a logical bridge, just adds
PuisSequential/narrative — implies time sequence, not logical connection. Belongs in storytelling, not argumentation.
EnsuiteSame as puis — time-based, not logic-based
EtNever begin a paragraph with "Et" in formal writing. It is a sentence-level coordinator, not a paragraph-level connector.
MaisDebated. Some formal writing uses "Mais" at paragraph start, but in TEF formal letters, "Cependant," "Toutefois," or "Néanmoins" are safer and score higher under Étendue du vocabulaire.
AlorsOral register. Absolute prohibition in formal letter writing.
Du coupStrictly informal/oral. Absolute prohibition. (Ch. 3)
DoncCan work in some formal contexts, but at paragraph-opening position it sounds slightly abrupt. "C'est pourquoi" or "En conséquence" are better choices.
En faitOften misused. In formal French, "en fait" means "in reality" (not "actually" as in oral English-influenced French). At paragraph-opening position, it can work but "En réalité" is safer and clearer.
Par contreDebated in formal writing. Many style guides consider "par contre" informal. Use "en revanche" instead for formal register.

4️⃣ MECHANISM 2: PRONOUN REFERENCE TRANSITIONS

What They Are

A pronoun reference transition uses a demonstrative pronoun, demonstrative adjective, or reference phrase at the start of P2 that points back to something specific in P1. This creates a direct lexical link between the two paragraphs.

Why They Are More Sophisticated Than Connector-Led Transitions

Connector-led transitions signal the logical relationship between paragraphs. Pronoun reference transitions do something more: they create textual cohesion by making P2 literally refer to P1's content. This is what the TEF criterion Cohérence et cohésion specifically evaluates — not just logical flow, but textual interconnection.

The Demonstrative Reference System

Demonstrative adjectives (ce, cette, ces) + noun:

French StructureWhat It ReferencesExample
Cette situationA situation described in P1P1 describes a late delivery → P2: Cette situation m'a conduit à contacter votre service client.
Ce constatAn observation made in P1P1 observes that green spaces are poorly maintained → P2: Ce constat m'amène à formuler les propositions suivantes.
Cette décisionA decision mentioned in P1P1 mentions a rent increase → P2: Cette décision soulève plusieurs interrogations.
Ce problèmeA problem identified in P1P1 identifies a product defect → P2: Ce problème a eu des conséquences directes sur...
Ces circonstancesThe overall circumstances of P1P1 describes a complex situation → P2: Dans ces circonstances, il m'a semblé nécessaire de...
Ces observationsObservations reported in P1P1 lists several things noticed → P2: Ces observations m'ont conduit à réfléchir à des solutions.
Cet articleAn article referenced in P1P1 references a newspaper article → P2: Cet article, bien qu'il soulève des points intéressants, me semble...
Cette annonceA job posting referenced in P1P1 references a job posting → P2: Les missions décrites dans cette annonce correspondent à...

The "Face à" / "Devant" family (preposition + demonstrative reference):

French StructureMeaningEffect
Face à cette situation,...Faced with this situation,...Frames P2 as a response to P1's situation. Very effective for complaints and requests.
Face à ce constat,...Faced with this observation,...Frames P2 as a reaction to P1's observation. Very effective for suggestions and opinion letters.
Devant cette situation,...Confronted with this situation,...Slightly more formal than "face à." Same function.
Au vu de ces éléments,...In light of these elements,...Formal. Frames P2 as a logical conclusion drawn from P1's evidence.
Fort(e) de ce constat,...Strengthened by this observation,...Frames P2 as empowered by P1's evidence. Effective for suggestions and opinion letters where P1's observation gives you standing to propose or argue.
À la lumière de ces observations,...In the light of these observations,...Very formal. Same function as "au vu de." Elegant variation.

The "C'est dans ce contexte que" family (emphatic reference):

French StructureMeaningEffect
C'est dans ce contexte que...It is in this context that...Emphatic — places P2 directly within P1's framework. Very effective.
C'est dans cette perspective que...It is in this perspective that...Forward-looking — P1 established a direction, P2 continues in that direction.
C'est pour cette raison que...It is for this reason that...Causal — P1 is the reason, P2 is the consequence.

5️⃣ MECHANISM 3: THEMATIC ECHO TRANSITIONS

What They Are

A thematic echo transition occurs when P2's opening picks up a word or concept from P1's closing and develops it in a new direction. Unlike pronoun references (which use demonstratives like "cette situation"), thematic echoes use the actual vocabulary from P1 — either repeating a key word or using a synonym.

Why They Are the Most Elegant Transition Mechanism

Thematic echoes create a sense of organic flow — the text reads as if one idea naturally grows into the next, without the visible "machinery" of a connector or demonstrative. The examiner experiences seamless cohesion rather than signposted transitions.

However, they are also the hardest to execute. They require you to plan your paragraph endings and beginnings together — which is why the planning method (Ch. 5) is essential.

How Thematic Echoes Work

The principle: The last significant noun or concept in P1 reappears (in identical or synonymous form) in the first sentence of P2.

Example (Complaint Letter):

[End of P1]...l'appareil présentait plusieurs dommages visibles.

[Start of P2] Ces dommages, qui rendent l'utilisation de l'appareil impossible, m'ont conduit à contacter immédiatement votre service client.

The word "dommages" appears at the end of P1 and the beginning of P2, creating a lexical bridge.

Example (Suggestion Letter):

[End of P1]...l'entretien des espaces verts mériterait d'être amélioré.

[Start of P2] L'état actuel de ces espaces témoigne en effet d'un manque d'investissement qui affecte la qualité de vie de l'ensemble des résidents.

The concept "espaces verts" echoes as "ces espaces" — a demonstrative + shortened form that maintains the reference while avoiding exact repetition.

Example (Opinion Letter):

[End of P1]...une vision plus nuancée de cette question.

[Start of P2] Cette question est d'autant plus importante qu'elle touche à des aspects fondamentaux de notre vie sociale.

The word "question" bridges the paragraphs.

The Synonym Echo Technique

Exact repetition can feel heavy. The synonym echo uses a near-synonym or reformulation to create the bridge while demonstrating lexical range:

P1 Ending WordP2 Opening SynonymEffect

6️⃣ COMBINING MECHANISMS — THE TRIPLE-LAYER TRANSITION

The most effective P1-P2 transitions combine two or three mechanisms simultaneously. Here is how:

Double-Layer: Connector + Pronoun Reference

[P1]...l'appareil présentait plusieurs dommages visibles.

[P2] De surcroît, cette situation a été aggravée par l'attitude de votre service client.

  • Connector: De surcroît (addition/escalation)
  • Pronoun reference: cette situation (points back to P1)

Double-Layer: Pronoun Reference + Thematic Echo

[P1]...l'entretien des espaces verts mériterait d'être amélioré.

[P2] Face à la dégradation progressive de ces espaces, il me semble urgent de prendre des mesures concrètes.

  • Pronoun reference: Face à + ces espaces (demonstrative pointing back)
  • Thematic echo: espaces echoes espaces verts

Triple-Layer: Connector + Pronoun Reference + Thematic Echo

[P1]...la conclusion selon laquelle les réseaux sociaux nuiraient aux relations interpersonnelles me semble réductrice.

[P2] En effet, si ces plateformes peuvent certes modifier la nature de nos interactions, elles offrent également de nouvelles possibilités de lien social que l'article passe sous silence.

  • Connector: En effet (elaboration/evidence)
  • Pronoun reference: ces plateformes (demonstrative replacing réseaux sociaux)
  • Thematic echo: interactions echoes relations; lien social echoes relations interpersonnelles

This triple-layer transition creates seamless cohesion. The examiner reads from P1 to P2 without sensing any gap. Every element in P2's opening sentence connects to something in P1.

7️⃣ THE TRANSITION DECISION TREE — CHOOSING YOUR MECHANISM

When you finish writing P1 and prepare to write P2, use this decision tree:

START: What is the logical relationship between P1 and P2?
│
├── P2 ADDS EVIDENCE to P1's claim
│   └── Use: En effet / De fait + pronoun reference
│       Example: "En effet, cette situation a eu des conséquences..."
│
├── P2 ADDS A NEW PROBLEM on top of P1's problem
│   └── Use: De surcroît / En outre / De plus + pronoun reference
│       Example: "De surcroît, cette difficulté s'est trouvée aggravée par..."
│
├── P2 INTRODUCES A COMPLICATION within P1's narrative
│   └── Use: Or, + pronoun reference
│       Example: "Or, malgré mes tentatives de résolution, cette situation..."
│
├── P2 CONTRASTS with P1 (different angle, disagreement)
│   └── Use: Cependant / Toutefois / Néanmoins + pronoun reference
│       Example: "Cependant, cette analyse me semble incomplète..."
│
├── P2 IS A CONSEQUENCE of P1's situation
│   └── Use: C'est pourquoi / C'est dans ce contexte que + thematic echo
│       Example: "C'est pourquoi je me permets de formuler les observations..."
│
├── P2 DEVELOPS what P1 previewed
│   └── Use: Pronoun reference + thematic echo (no connector needed)
│       Example: "Ces préoccupations portent sur plusieurs aspects..."
│
└── P2 ILLUSTRATES P1's general statement with specifics
    └── Use: Ainsi / En effet + specific example
        Example: "Ainsi, l'éclairage public de notre rue principale..."

9️⃣ GRAMMAR FOCUS: THE GRAMMATICAL STRUCTURES BEHIND STRONG TRANSITIONS

Structure 1: The "C'est... que/qui" Emphatic Construction in Transitions

This cleft sentence construction (introduced in Ch. 17 for cover letters) is one of the most powerful transition tools because it simultaneously creates emphasis AND reference:

Pattern: C'est + [reference to P1 element] + qui/que + [P2's main clause]

ExampleWhat It Does
C'est dans ce contexte que j'ai décidé de vous écrire.References P1's situation, announces P2's action
C'est précisément ce point que je souhaiterais développer.References P1's final idea, announces P2's elaboration
C'est pour cette raison que ma candidature me semble pertinente.References P1's observation, announces P2's claim
C'est cette réalité qui m'amène à contester les conclusions de l'article.References P1's evidence, announces P2's argument

Grammar note: "Qui" is used when the referenced element is the subject of the subordinate clause. "Que" is used when it is the object or follows a preposition phrase.

  • C'est cette situation qui m'a conduit à... (situation = subject of conduire)
  • C'est cette situation que je souhaite dénoncer. (situation = object of dénoncer)
  • C'est dans ce contexte que je me permets de... (dans ce contexte = prepositional phrase)

Structure 2: The Past Participle / Present Participle as Transition Bridge

Participial constructions create seamless bridges by embedding the reference to P1 within P2's grammatical structure:

Past participle (referring to P1's completed action/state):

ExampleP1 ReferenceP2 Development
Confronté(e) à cette situation, j'ai tenté de...cette situation = P1j'ai tenté de = P2 action
Convaincu(e) de la nécessité d'agir, je me permets de...nécessité d'agir = P1's concernje me permets de = P2 action
Déçu(e) par l'absence de réponse, je me vois dans l'obligation de...absence de réponse = P1's failed resolutionje me vois dans l'obligation de = P2 escalation

Present participle (creating simultaneous reference):

ExampleFunction
Souhaitant approfondir cette question, je me permets de vous présenter...Links P1's topic to P2's elaboration
Constatant que cette situation perdure, j'ai décidé de...Links P1's observation to P2's action
Estimant que cette analyse mérite d'être nuancée, je souhaiterais...Links P1's reaction to P2's argument

Agreement reminder: Past participles in appositive position agree with the subject (je):

  • Male writer: Confronté, Convaincu, Déçu
  • Female writer: Confrontée, Convaincue, Déçue

Present participles do NOT agree: Souhaitant, Constatant, Estimant — invariable regardless of the subject's gender.

Structure 3: The Relative Clause as Extended Bridge

When you need a more elaborate transition, a relative clause can bridge paragraphs by extending P1's final element into P2's territory:

Pattern: P1 ends with noun X. P2 begins: X, [relative clause developing X], [main clause of P2]

ExampleHow It Works
[P1]...les dommages constatés à la réception. [P2] Ces dommages, qui rendent l'appareil totalement inutilisable, m'ont conduit à contacter immédiatement votre service client.The relative clause (qui rendent l'appareil totalement inutilisable) develops the P1 reference (dommages) while the main clause (m'ont conduit à contacter) launches P2's content.
[P1]...les propositions que je souhaiterais formuler. [P2] Ces propositions, qui s'appuient sur mes observations quotidiennes, portent sur trois aspects principaux.The relative clause grounds the proposals in evidence; the main clause announces the structure of P2.
[P1]...votre article consacré aux réseaux sociaux. [P2] Cet article, bien qu'il soulève des préoccupations légitimes, me semble néanmoins offrir une vision trop unidimensionnelle du phénomène.The concessive relative clause (bien qu'il soulève...) acknowledges the article's value while the main clause pivots to disagreement. Note: bien que + subjunctive (soulève) — B2/C1 grammar.

🔟 WHAT COULD GO WRONG — TRANSITION-SPECIFIC ERRORS

Error 1: No Transition At All (The "Jump Cut")

What happens: P1 ends. P2 begins with completely new content and no reference to P1.

[P1]...l'appareil est arrivé endommagé.

[P2] Votre publicité promet une livraison rapide et des produits de qualité.

Why it's wrong: The reader experiences a "jump" — from the specific delivery problem to a general statement about advertising. There is no logical bridge. The examiner marks Cohérence et cohésion down.

Fix: Add a bridge that connects the specific experience to the general promise:

[P2] Or, cette expérience contraste fortement avec les engagements affichés dans votre publicité, qui promet une livraison rapide et des produits de qualité.

Error 2: Repeating P1's Content Instead of Transitioning

What happens: P2's opening sentence says the same thing as P1's closing sentence, just with different words.

[P1]...le produit est arrivé en retard et endommagé.

[P2] Le produit que j'ai reçu était en retard et abîmé. De plus, j'ai essayé d'appeler...

Why it's wrong: The first sentence of P2 is pure repetition. It wastes word count and makes the examiner feel the letter is going in circles. The transition should advance the text, not repeat it.

Fix: Start P2 with the new information, using a reference to P1 rather than a restatement:

[P2] Face à cette situation, j'ai tenté à deux reprises de joindre votre service client...

"Cette situation" references P1 without repeating it. P2 immediately advances to new content.

Error 3: Using an Oral Transition Marker

❌ Oral MarkerWhy It Fails✅ Formal Replacement
Alors, j'ai appelé...Oral register — Ch. 3 prohibitionC'est pourquoi j'ai contacté...
Du coup, j'ai décidé de...Strictly informal — absolute prohibitionEn conséquence, j'ai pris la décision de...
Bon, le problème c'est que...Conversational filler — catastrophic in formal writingLe problème principal réside dans le fait que...
En gros, l'article dit que...Oral summary markerL'article soutient que...
Bref, je ne suis pas d'accord.Oral conclusion markerEn somme, cette analyse me semble contestable.
Après, il y a aussi le fait que...Oral addition marker ("après" as connector is informal)Par ailleurs, il convient de souligner que...

Error 4: Using the Wrong Connector for the Logical Relationship

What happens: The candidate uses a contrast connector when the relationship is actually elaboration, or an addition connector when the relationship is actually consequence.

[P1]...l'appareil est arrivé endommagé.

❌ [P2] Cependant, j'ai contacté votre service client pour signaler ce problème.

Why it's wrong: "Cependant" signals contrast or opposition. But contacting customer service after receiving a damaged product is not a contrast — it is a consequence or a natural next step. There is no opposition between receiving a damaged product and calling to complain about it.

✅ [P2] C'est pourquoi j'ai contacté votre service client pour signaler ce problème.

Or:

✅ [P2] Face à cette situation, j'ai naturellement contacté votre service client.

Error 5: Over-Transitioning (The "Signpost Overload")

What happens: The candidate uses multiple transition mechanisms where one would suffice, creating a heavy, mechanical feel.

❌ [P2] C'est pourquoi, face à cette situation et au vu de ces éléments, en conséquence, j'ai décidé de contacter votre service client.

Why it's wrong: Four transition mechanisms in one sentence. The sentence collapses under its own weight. One or two mechanisms are enough.

The rule: Use one or two transition mechanisms per paragraph boundary. The transition should feel like a bridge, not a drawbridge with chains, pulleys, and a moat.

Error 6: Starting P2 with "Je" After P1 Also Started with "Je"

What happens: Both P1 and P2 begin with "Je...", creating a monotonous, self-centered rhythm.

[P1] Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de...

[P2] Je souhaiterais maintenant vous expliquer...

Why it's a problem: While not grammatically incorrect, starting consecutive paragraphs with "Je" is stylistically weak. It signals limited sentence variety and makes the letter feel like a list of personal actions rather than a structured argument.

Fixes:

Instead of "Je..." at P2 startUse
Je souhaiterais expliquer...Plusieurs raisons motivent cette démarche. (impersonal opener)
Je vais maintenant parler de...En effet, cette situation mérite d'être examinée plus en détail. (connector + impersonal)
Je pense que...Force est de constater que... (impersonal) or Il me semble que... (displaced subject)
J'ai aussi remarqué que...De surcroît, un autre aspect mérite d'être souligné. (connector + impersonal)

1️⃣1️⃣ B1 vs. B2 vs. C1 — THE SAME TRANSITION AT THREE LEVELS

The Scenario

Complaint letter. P1 describes ordering a laptop online that arrived two weeks late with a cracked screen.

B1 Level

[P1]...Le produit est arrivé en retard et l'écran était cassé.

[P2] J'ai appelé le service client. La personne m'a dit qu'elle ne pouvait rien faire.

Diagnosis:

  • ❌ No transition mechanism — complete "jump cut"
  • ❌ P2 starts with "J'ai" (same structure as likely P1 opening)
  • ❌ No connector between paragraphs
  • ❌ No reference to P1's content
  • ❌ Oral register ("La personne m'a dit")
  • Cohesion score: low

B2 Level

[P1]...l'appareil commandé est arrivé avec un retard considérable et présentait un écran fissuré rendant toute utilisation impossible.

[P2] Face à cette situation, j'ai immédiatement tenté de joindre votre service client par téléphone. Or, malgré deux appels effectués les 15 et 18 mars, aucune solution satisfaisante ne m'a été proposée.

Diagnosis:

  • ✅ Pronoun reference transition: "Face à cette situation"
  • ✅ Connector within P2: "Or" (complication)
  • ✅ Specific details (dates of calls)
  • ✅ Passive voice for emphasis: "aucune solution... ne m'a été proposée"
  • ✅ Formal vocabulary: "joindre," "effectués"
  • ✅ P2 begins with a prepositional phrase, not "Je"
  • Cohesion score: strong

C1 Level

[P1]...un appareil présentant un écran fissuré, rendant de facto toute utilisation impossible.

[P2] C'est dans ce contexte particulièrement décevant que j'ai pris l'initiative de contacter votre service client, convaincu qu'un dialogue direct permettrait de résoudre ce différend dans les meilleurs délais. Force est de constater, cependant, que mes deux tentatives — les 15 et 18 mars — se sont soldées par un constat d'échec : aucun de mes interlocuteurs n'a été en mesure de me proposer une solution concrète, se contentant de m'orienter vers un formulaire en ligne resté sans réponse à ce jour.

Diagnosis:

  • ✅ Triple-layer transition: emphatic reference (C'est dans ce contexte que) + participial bridge (convaincu qu'un dialogue direct permettrait) + thematic echo (résoudre ce différend develops P1's problem)
  • ✅ Contrast within P2: "Force est de constater, cependant, que"
  • ✅ Specificity with elegant presentation: "mes deux tentatives — les 15 et 18 mars —" (dashes for parenthetical dates)
  • ✅ Complex grammar: pronominal passive (se sont soldées par), present participle (se contentant de), past participle agreement (restée)
  • ✅ Narrative sophistication: sets up an expectation (dialogue would resolve the issue) then defeats it (but the calls failed)
  • Cohesion score: excellent

1️⃣2️⃣ THE SPECIAL CASE: IMPLICIT TRANSITIONS

When No Explicit Transition Is Needed

In some letter types, particularly cover letters, the transition from P1 to P2 can be implicit — meaning no connector, demonstrative, or echo is needed because the logical flow is self-evident.

When implicit transitions work:

P1 ContentP2 ContentWhy the Transition Is Self-Evident
"I am applying for the position of X. I have Y years of experience.""During my time at [Company], I developed skills in..."The reader expects evidence after a qualification claim. No bridge needed.
"I accept your proposal.""Several aspects of this proposal are particularly attractive."Acceptance naturally leads to explanation of why.

When implicit transitions do NOT work:

P1 ContentP2 ContentWhy a Bridge Is Needed
"I ordered a product that arrived damaged.""Your advertising promises quality."The shift from personal experience to advertising critique needs a logical connector ("Or, cette expérience contraste avec...")
"I read your article about social media.""Social media platforms offer many benefits."The shift from article reference to counter-argument needs a position signal ("Cependant, il me semble que...")

The rule: If the relationship between P1 and P2 is chronological (this happened, then this happened) or claim → evidence, the transition can be implicit. If the relationship involves contrast, complication, consequence, or a change of perspective, an explicit transition is mandatory.

💡 TEF strategy: When in doubt, use an explicit transition. An unnecessary but well-executed transition never loses points. A missing necessary transition always does.

1️⃣3️⃣ THE "OR" CONNECTOR — A DEEP DIVE

Why "Or" Deserves Special Attention

Among all formal French connectors, "Or" is perhaps the most misunderstood by non-native speakers and the most valued by examiners. It deserves dedicated treatment because it is the signature connector of formal French argumentation and it appears most naturally at the P1-P2 boundary.

What "Or" Means

"Or" does NOT mean "or" (the English conjunction of choice). It has no English equivalent. The closest translations are:

  • "Now" (in the sense of "now, it turns out that...")
  • "However" (but weaker than cependant)
  • "But as it happens"

What it signals:
"Or" introduces a fact or observation that complicates, contradicts, or reframes what was previously stated. It is the connector of unexpected turns and complicating realities.

How "Or" Works in the P1-P2 Transition

Pattern: P1 establishes a situation or expectation. P2 begins with "Or," introducing a fact that disrupts or complicates that situation.

P1 (Expectation/Situation)Or, + P2 (Complication)
L'appareil devait être livré sous dix jours.Or, je ne l'ai reçu qu'au bout de cinq semaines.
Votre annonce promettait un produit de haute qualité.Or, l'article reçu présentait de nombreuses imperfections.
L'article affirme que les réseaux sociaux nuisent aux relations.Or, de nombreuses études démontrent que ces plateformes peuvent également renforcer le lien social.
Le bail prévoit une révision annuelle du loyer indexée sur l'inflation.Or, l'augmentation de 15 % que vous proposez dépasse largement le taux d'inflation en vigueur.

Grammar and Punctuation of "Or"

  • "Or" is always followed by a comma: Or*,** malgré mes appels...*
  • "Or" typically begins a new sentence, not a clause within a sentence
  • "Or" is never combined with another connector: ❌ Or cependant... ❌ Or toutefois...
  • "Or" belongs to the registre soutenu (formal register) — it is rarely used in spoken French

When NOT to Use "Or"

SituationWhy "Or" Is WrongUse Instead
P2 simply adds more information"Or" signals complication, not additionDe plus, En outre, De surcroît
P2 provides evidence for P1's claim"Or" signals disruption, not supportEn effet, De fait
P2 is a consequence of P1"Or" signals something unexpected, not a logical consequenceC'est pourquoi, En conséquence
P2 offers a parallel or comparison"Or" signals tension, not similarityDe même, Parallèlement

💡 TEF strategy: Use "Or" once per letter, at the P1-P2 boundary, when the transition involves a complication or contradiction. This single usage demonstrates that you understand a high-register connector that most B1 writers do not know. Do not overuse it — two or more uses of "Or" in a single letter feels repetitive.

1️⃣4️⃣ WHAT TO NEVER DO AT THE P1-P2 BOUNDARY

❌ Never Do ThisWhy It's Prohibited
Start P2 with "Premièrement" or "Tout d'abord" if P1 did not announce a list"Premièrement" signals the first item in a series. If P1 did not announce that a series is coming, "Premièrement" feels random. Use it only if P1 ended with something like "Mes réserves portent sur les points suivants" — then "Premièrement" is appropriate.
Start P2 with "Comme je l'ai dit" or "Comme mentionné précédemment"These are essay markers, not letter markers. In a 200-word letter, nothing was said "previously" in any meaningful sense. These phrases waste words and sound like filler.
Use "D'un côté... d'un autre côté" as P1→P2 bridgeThis binary structure (on one hand... on the other hand) works within a paragraph but is too mechanical as a paragraph bridge. It also sounds like a translated English structure (on one hand/on the other hand).
Leave a blank line between P1 and P2 with no logical connectionA blank line is a visual paragraph break. It does not create logical connection. You need both: visual separation AND logical bridging.
Start P2 with exactly the same word that ended P1Exact repetition at the paragraph boundary is heavy-handed. Use a synonym or demonstrative reference instead. P1 ends with "...un retard considérable." → P2 starts with "Ce retard..." (demonstrative) not "Le retard..." (exact repetition without demonstrative).
Use the transition to announce what you will say instead of saying it❌ "Je vais maintenant vous expliquer pourquoi je suis mécontent." — This is a meta-textual announcement that wastes words and belongs to oral presentation register. In formal writing, you do not announce your argument — you make it. Instead of saying "I will now explain why I am dissatisfied," simply begin explaining: ✅ "En effet, cette situation a eu des conséquences directes sur mon activité professionnelle." The explanation IS the transition. The examiner does not need a roadmap for a 200-word letter. Exception: The "Permettez-moi de..." construction (e.g., "Permettez-moi de vous présenter les raisons de mon désaccord") is acceptable because it is a politeness formula, not a meta-textual announcement. It asks permission rather than announcing intent.
Start P2 with a question❌ "Pourquoi est-ce que cette situation est inacceptable ?" — Rhetorical questions at paragraph openings belong to oral debate and essay writing, not to formal letter writing. The recipient is not expected to answer your question. Convert questions to statements: ✅ "Cette situation est d'autant plus préoccupante que..." or "Il convient de s'interroger sur les raisons de..." (if you must reference a question, frame it as an impersonal observation about the need to question).
Use two different connectors at the start of P2❌ "Cependant, en effet, cette situation..." — Two connectors create logical confusion. "Cependant" signals contrast; "en effet" signals confirmation. They cannot coexist. Choose ONE connector that accurately represents the P1→P2 relationship.

1️⃣6️⃣ THE TRANSITION AND PARAGRAPH 2'S INTERNAL STRUCTURE — HOW THEY INTERACT

The Transition Is Not the Whole First Sentence of P2

A common misconception: the transition IS P2's first sentence. In reality, the transition is the opening element of P2's first sentence, which then continues into P2's actual content.

The anatomy of P2's first sentence:

[TRANSITION ELEMENT] + [P2'S FIRST CONTENT POINT]

Examples:

"Face à cette situation,"  +  "j'ai tenté de joindre votre service client."
 ─── transition ───          ─── P2 content begins ───

"En effet,"  +  "plusieurs éléments témoignent de la nécessité d'agir."
 ── trans. ──   ─── P2 content begins ───

"C'est dans ce contexte que"  +  "j'ai pris l'initiative de vous écrire."
 ─── transition ───────────     ─── P2 content begins ───

The transition element takes up 3 to 10 words at most. The rest of the sentence belongs to P2's argument. If your entire first sentence of P2 is transition and nothing else, you have wasted a sentence:

❌ C'est pour toutes ces raisons que je me permets de vous écrire cette lettre. (transition only — no new content)

✅ C'est pour toutes ces raisons que je souhaiterais attirer votre attention sur l'état préoccupant de l'éclairage public dans notre quartier. (transition + first content point of P2)

How the Transition Sets Up P2's Organizational Pattern

The transition you choose can also signal how P2 will be organized internally:

Transition ChoiceWhat It Signals About P2's Structure
En effet, + [single point]P2 will develop ONE main argument with evidence
Mes réserves portent sur les points suivants.P2 will present MULTIPLE points (possibly with internal numbering: en premier lieu, en second lieu)
Plusieurs raisons motivent cette démarche.P2 will present a LIST of reasons
Cette situation a eu deux conséquences principales.P2 will present exactly TWO consequences
Force est de constater que + [observation]P2 will build an argument from an undeniable observation

💡 Planning tip from Ch. 5: During your 5-minute planning phase, decide not only WHAT P2 will contain but HOW it will be organized. Then choose a transition that naturally introduces that organizational pattern. If P2 will present three arguments, your transition can signal this: "Mes réserves tiennent à trois raisons principales." This creates a roadmap that the examiner can follow.

1️⃣7️⃣ PRACTICE EXERCISE — DIAGNOSE AND FIX THESE TRANSITIONS

For each example below, identify the problem and write a corrected version. This exercise prepares you for the self-editing phase of the exam (Ch. 42).

Exercise 1: Complaint Letter

[P1]...le colis est arrivé avec deux semaines de retard et le produit était endommagé.

[P2] Aussi, j'ai appelé votre service client deux fois mais personne n'a pu m'aider.

Problems to identify:

  1. "Aussi" at sentence start meaning "therefore" requires inversion (Aussi ai-je appelé...). Without inversion, "aussi" means "also" — which creates a weak addition rather than a consequence.
  2. "J'ai appelé" — starts with "Je" (stylistically weak after P1 likely also starting with a personal subject)
  3. "Mais personne n'a pu m'aider" — "mais" within the sentence is acceptable, but the overall structure is too simple for B2
  4. No demonstrative reference to P1

Corrected version:

[P2] Face à cette situation, j'ai tenté à deux reprises de joindre votre service client par téléphone. Or, aucun de mes interlocuteurs n'a été en mesure de me proposer une solution satisfaisante.

What changed: Pronoun reference (Face à cette situation), avoidance of "Je" at sentence start, formal vocabulary (joindre, interlocuteurs, en mesure de), "Or" for complication, passive emphasis (aucun... n'a été en mesure de).

Exercise 2: Suggestion Letter

[P1]...je me permets de vous soumettre quelques propositions pour améliorer notre quartier.

[P2] D'abord, le parc est très sale. Les poubelles débordent et l'herbe n'est pas coupée.

Problems to identify:

  1. "D'abord" — too casual as a paragraph opener in formal register. "En premier lieu" or "Tout d'abord" are more formal, but even these feel abrupt without a bridge sentence.
  2. "Le parc est très sale" — informal register ("très sale"), no connection to P1's "propositions"
  3. List-like presentation without analytical framing
  4. No transition mechanism connecting the proposals to evidence

Corrected version:

[P2] En effet, plusieurs constats m'amènent à considérer que des améliorations sont nécessaires. En premier lieu, l'état du parc des Tilleuls témoigne d'un entretien insuffisant : les espaces de verdure sont envahis par les mauvaises herbes et les équipements de propreté, manifestement saturés, ne sont plus en mesure de répondre aux besoins des usagers.

What changed: Connector bridge (En effet) + pronoun reference (plusieurs constats) before the list begins. "En premier lieu" now follows a bridge sentence. Specific park named. Formal vocabulary (témoigne de, entretien insuffisant, envahis, équipements de propreté, saturés). Analytical framing instead of list-like description.

Exercise 3: Opinion Letter

[P1]...je souhaiterais proposer une vision plus nuancée de cette question.

[P2] Les réseaux sociaux ne sont pas seulement négatifs. Ils ont aussi des aspects positifs.

Problems to identify:

  1. No transition mechanism — complete jump from position preview to argument
  2. "Ne sont pas seulement négatifs" — too simple for B2, echoes the prompt rather than reformulating
  3. "Ils ont aussi des aspects positifs" — vague, informal ("des aspects positifs" is B1 vocabulary)
  4. Two short sentences instead of one developed sentence

Corrected version:

[P2] En effet, si les réseaux sociaux comportent certes des risques — isolement numérique, dépendance aux écrans, diffusion de fausses informations —, ils constituent également un vecteur de lien social dont l'article ne tient pas suffisamment compte.

What changed: Connector (En effet) + concession-refutation within one sentence (si... certes... ils constituent également). Specific risks listed in parenthetical dashes (shows you engaged with the article's arguments). Formal vocabulary (comportent, vecteur de lien social, ne tient pas suffisamment compte). One sophisticated sentence replaces two simplistic ones.

Exercise 4: Response Letter (Disagreement)

[P1]...je souhaiterais vous faire part de certaines réserves quant au montant de cette augmentation.

[P2] D'un côté, je comprends que vous avez fait des travaux. D'un autre côté, 15 % c'est vraiment beaucoup.

Problems to identify:

  1. "D'un côté... d'un autre côté" — mechanical binary structure, feels translated from English
  2. "Je comprends que vous avez fait des travaux" — oral register ("vous avez fait des travaux"), indicative after "comprendre que" (correct grammatically but the sentence itself is too informal)
  3. "15 % c'est vraiment beaucoup" — oral register ("c'est vraiment beaucoup")
  4. No reference to P1's "réserves"

Corrected version:

[P2] Ces réserves tiennent principalement au fait que le montant envisagé me semble disproportionné au regard des améliorations effectivement réalisées. Si je ne conteste pas le principe d'une révision du loyer — les travaux de rénovation justifiant en effet un certain ajustement —, une augmentation de l'ordre de 15 % dépasse, à mon sens, ce qui peut être raisonnablement attendu d'un locataire.

What changed: Pronoun reference (Ces réserves) directly echoes P1. Formal vocabulary (disproportionné, au regard de, effectivement réalisées). Concession embedded within dashes (les travaux... justifiant en effet un certain ajustement). Nuanced conclusion (dépasse ce qui peut être raisonnablement attendu) instead of blunt judgment (c'est trop).

1️⃣8️⃣ THE TRANSITION IN THE CONTEXT OF WORD COUNT MANAGEMENT

How Many Words Should the Transition Take?

The transition is an element of P2's first sentence, not a paragraph unto itself. Here is the word budget:

Letter SectionTarget Word Count (from Ch. 1)
P2 total55–70 words
P2 transition element3–12 words
P2 remaining content43–67 words

The danger of over-transitioning:
If your transition takes 20+ words, you are spending P2's word budget on bridging rather than arguing. The transition should be efficient — a few words that create the connection, then immediately into content.

❌ Over-long transition (22 words, no content)✅ Efficient transition (8 words + content)
C'est pour l'ensemble de ces raisons que je me permets aujourd'hui de revenir vers vous afin de vous exposer les conséquences de cette situation.Face à cette situation, les conséquences ont été considérables, tant sur le plan financier que pratique.

The first example uses 22 words just to say "here's why I'm writing about the consequences." The second uses 8 transition words (Face à cette situation) and immediately begins delivering content (les conséquences ont été considérables, tant sur le plan financier que pratique).

The "One Breath" Rule

A useful mental test: can you read the transition element in one breath before arriving at the content? If you run out of breath during the transition, it is too long.

  • "Face à cette situation," → one breath ✅
  • "En effet," → one breath ✅
  • "C'est dans ce contexte que" → one breath ✅
  • "C'est pour l'ensemble de ces raisons, et compte tenu des circonstances que j'ai décrites précédemment, que je me permets de" → multiple breaths ❌ (too long)

1️⃣9️⃣ THE TRANSITION AND THE PLANNING PHASE — PREPARING YOUR BRIDGE DURING THE 5-MINUTE PLAN

Adding the Transition to Your Planning Grid

Chapter 5 taught you to plan your letter using a quick grid. Here is how to integrate the transition into that grid:

PLANNING GRID (with transition)
┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ P1: Context                              │
│ - Who I am: resident, 8 years            │
│ - What happened: late delivery, damaged   │
│ - Impact: can't use the product          │
│                                          │
│ TRANSITION → "Face à cette situation" OR │
│              "Or, malgré mes appels"     │
│                                          │
│ P2: Argument                             │
│ - Called customer service twice           │
│ - No solution offered                    │
│ - Financial impact: paid €500            │
│                                          │
│ TRANSITION → "En conséquence" OR         │
│              "C'est pourquoi"            │
│                                          │
│ P3: Solution/Request                     │
│ - Request: replacement or refund         │
│ - Deadline: 15 days                      │
│ - Escalation warning: consumer rights    │
└──────────────────────────────────────────┘

By noting the transition in your plan, you ensure that:

  1. You do not forget to include one
  2. You choose the right logical connector for the relationship
  3. You can adjust if, during writing, the P1 content shifts slightly from your plan

The Two-Option Strategy

Notice that the planning grid above offers two transition options for each boundary. This is deliberate. During writing, you may find that your P1 ended slightly differently than planned. Having two options gives you flexibility:

  • If P1 ended emphasizing the problem's severity → "Face à cette situation" (pronoun reference)
  • If P1 ended emphasizing your failed attempts to resolve → "Or, malgré mes appels" (complication connector)

Preparing two options takes only 10 seconds during planning but can save you 2 minutes of hesitation during writing.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY — WHAT THIS CHAPTER TEACHES THAT NO OTHER CHAPTER COVERS

Chapter 19 is the only chapter dedicated to the invisible architecture between paragraphs.

Every other chapter teaches you what goes inside a paragraph — the content, the phrases, the grammar. This chapter teaches you what goes between paragraphs — the connective tissue that transforms separate blocks of text into a unified letter.

The core principles:

  1. A transition is not just a connector word. It is a logical bridge that simultaneously closes P1, opens P2, and signals the relationship between them. Three mechanisms create this bridge: connector-led transitions, pronoun reference transitions, and thematic echo transitions. The strongest transitions combine two or three mechanisms.

  2. The P1-P2 transition is the most important transition in your letter because it marks the shift from context to argument — the moment where the examiner first sees your ability to build a case, not just describe a situation.

  3. Different logical relationships require different transition tools. Elaboration uses "En effet." Addition uses "De surcroît." Complication uses "Or." Contrast uses "Cependant." Consequence uses "C'est pourquoi." Choosing the wrong tool for the relationship is worse than using no transition at all.

  4. The transition should be efficient — 3 to 12 words that create the bridge, then immediately into P2's content. Over-transitioning wastes word count. Under-transitioning breaks cohesion.

  5. Plan your transition during the 5-minute planning phase. Note two options on your planning grid. This prevents hesitation during writing and ensures your letter has the logical architecture that the Cohérence et cohésion criterion demands.

The connector "Or" — the signature of formal French argumentation — was given special treatment because it is the most powerful and most misunderstood transition tool available to you. One correct use of "Or" at the P1-P2 boundary immediately signals B2-level formal writing competence.
