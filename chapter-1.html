📖 THE EXAM MOMENT

You are sitting in the examination room. The invigilator has distributed the booklets. You turn to Section B — Expression Écrite, Partie B. Your eyes land on a block of text, maybe 4–6 lines long, printed inside a box or set apart from the rest of the page. Below it, there is a large blank space — or, if computer-based, an empty text field with a word counter.

Your heart rate increases. You have approximately 60 minutes for this section (combined with Section A in some administrations, or standalone in others depending on the test center). You need to produce a letter. Not a paragraph. Not an essay. A letter — with a header, a salutation, structured paragraphs, and a closing formula.

The question is: Do you actually understand what this task is asking you to do?

Most candidates think they do. Most candidates are wrong — not because they lack French ability, but because they misunderstand the nature of the task. This chapter eliminates that misunderstanding permanently.

1️⃣ WHAT IS SECTION B, IN PLAIN LANGUAGE?

The Task Definition

TEF Expression Écrite Section B asks you to write a formal or semi-formal letter in response to a given situation. The situation is described in a prompt (la consigne). The prompt tells you:

  • Who you are (your identity in the scenario)
  • Who you are writing to (the recipient)
  • Why you are writing (the triggering event or need)
  • What you must include (specific points to address — usually 2–4 requirements)

Your letter must be a minimum of 200 words. There is no official maximum, but practical constraints (time, focus, handwriting space) mean that most successful letters fall between 200 and 300 words. Going beyond 300 is possible but risky — more words means more opportunities for errors.

What "Letter" Means at TEF Level

This is not a casual email. This is not a note to a friend. This is not an SMS expanded to 200 words. The TEF Section B letter is a structured document that follows French epistolary conventions — conventions that have specific rules for:

  • How the sender's information appears
  • How the recipient's information appears
  • How the date is formatted
  • How the subject line is written
  • How you greet the recipient
  • How you organize your content into paragraphs
  • How you close the letter
  • How you sign

Every single one of these elements is evaluated. Not just your French grammar. Not just your vocabulary. The structure itself is part of the test.

💡 Think of it this way: If the TEF asked you to write a poem, they would evaluate whether it looks and reads like a poem. Section B asks you to write a letter. They evaluate whether it looks and reads like a French formal letter.

2️⃣ WHY THIS TASK EXISTS — WHAT THE TEF IS REALLY TESTING

The Surface Level Test

On the surface, Section B tests whether you can:

  • Write grammatically correct French
  • Use appropriate vocabulary
  • Organize ideas logically
  • Meet a minimum word count

The Deeper Test

Beneath the surface, Section B tests something far more specific: Can you function in French-language professional and administrative life?

In France, Quebec, Belgium, Switzerland, and every Francophone administrative context, formal letters are not relics of the past. They are living, daily instruments. People write formal letters to:

  • Dispute a bill
  • Apply for a job
  • Request a meeting with a school principal
  • Complain to a landlord about broken heating
  • Propose an initiative to a municipal council
  • Respond to an administrative summons
  • Cancel a contract within the legal cooling-off period

The TEF is, at its core, a test of whether you can participate in Francophone society at a functional level. Section B is the most direct test of this. It simulates real situations you will encounter if you live, work, or study in a French-speaking environment.

What This Means for You as a Candidate

It means the examiner is not reading your letter as a language exercise. They are reading it as if they are the recipient. They ask themselves:

  • "If I were the company director receiving this complaint, would I take this person seriously?"
  • "If I were the mayor reading this suggestion, would I understand what is being proposed?"
  • "If I were the hiring manager reading this cover letter, would I consider this candidate professional?"

Your letter must function as a real letter. It must accomplish its purpose — not just demonstrate your French.

4️⃣ WHAT THE PROMPT LOOKS LIKE — ANATOMY OF THE INSTRUCTIONS

Typical Prompt Structure

TEF prompts for Section B follow a consistent pattern. Here is what you will see (this is a representative example, not an actual past exam item):

Situation :
Vous avez commandé un appareil électroménager sur le site Internet de la société Électro-Plus. L'appareil est arrivé avec trois semaines de retard et il était endommagé à la réception. Vous avez contacté le service client par téléphone, mais on ne vous a proposé aucune solution satisfaisante.

Consigne :
Vous écrivez une lettre au directeur du service client de la société Électro-Plus pour vous plaindre de cette situation. Dans votre lettre, vous devez :

  • rappeler les faits (commande, retard, état du produit) ;
  • exprimer votre mécontentement ;
  • demander une solution précise.

Rédigez une lettre formelle d'au moins 200 mots.

Breaking Down the Prompt — The Four Elements

Every TEF Section B prompt contains four extractable elements. You must identify all four before you begin writing.

Element 1: WHO YOU ARE (Your Identity)

In the example above: You are a customer who ordered a household appliance online.

Sometimes the prompt gives you a name: "Vous êtes Marie Dupont..." Sometimes it does not, and you remain anonymous or choose a name. Either way, your role is defined: customer, resident, parent, employee, citizen, job applicant.

Element 2: WHO YOU ARE WRITING TO (The Recipient)

In the example above: The director of customer service at the company Électro-Plus.

This determines your salutation (Ch. 10), your Objet line (Ch. 9), and your closing formula (Ch. 34). A letter to a company director requires a different register than a letter to a newspaper editor.

Element 3: WHY YOU ARE WRITING (The Trigger)

In the example above: A damaged product arrived late, and phone contact produced no solution.

This is your context — it becomes Paragraph 1 (Ch. 13–18). You must reference this trigger clearly and early.

Element 4: WHAT YOU MUST INCLUDE (The Required Points)

In the example above, three required points:

  1. Recall the facts (order, delay, product condition)
  2. Express dissatisfaction
  3. Request a specific solution

🔴 THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT ELEMENT. These bullet points are your checklist. Every single one must appear in your letter. Missing even one is a direct deduction under Respect de la consigne. The examiner will literally check: "Did the candidate recall the facts? ✓ or ✗. Did the candidate express dissatisfaction? ✓ or ✗. Did the candidate request a solution? ✓ or ✗."

The Hidden Requirements

Beyond the explicit bullet points, every TEF Section B prompt carries hidden requirements that are never stated but always evaluated:

Hidden RequirementWhat It Means
Formal letter formatHeader, date, Objet, salutation, structured paragraphs, closing formula, signature
Appropriate registerFormal or semi-formal French — no slang, no contractions, no informal expressions
Logical organizationIdeas in a coherent order, not scattered randomly
Minimum 200 wordsFalling below 200 triggers an automatic penalty
RelevanceEverything in the letter relates to the prompt — no off-topic digressions

5️⃣ THE 200-WORD MINIMUM — WHAT IT LOOKS LIKE PHYSICALLY

Why Word Count Matters

The 200-word minimum is not arbitrary. It represents the TEF's judgment of how many words are needed to accomplish the task at B2 level. A letter under 200 words almost certainly:

  • Lacks sufficient development of ideas
  • Misses one or more required points
  • Has an incomplete structure (probably missing the closing formula or a body paragraph)

What 200 Words Looks Like

On paper, 200 words of handwritten French fills approximately 20–25 lines of standard lined paper, depending on handwriting size. On screen, it fills roughly one full screen at standard zoom.

Here is a spatial breakdown of a well-structured 200-word letter:

7️⃣ PAPER-BASED vs. COMPUTER-BASED — WHAT CHANGES

TEF can be administered in paper format or computer format depending on the test center. The task is identical, but the practical implications differ.

Paper-Based Exam

FactorImplication
HandwritingMust be legible. If the examiner cannot read a word, it does not exist. Use clear, separated letters. Avoid cursive if your cursive is difficult to read.
Crossing outOne clean line through an error. Do NOT scribble, use correction fluid, or make a mess. A single strikethrough is professional; a scribbled mess suggests panic.
Space managementLeave a blank line between paragraphs. Indent the first line of each paragraph OR leave a full blank line — pick one system and be consistent.
Word countingYou must estimate. Count the words in your first two lines, calculate the average per line, then multiply by total lines. Practice this before exam day.
No editing flexibilityYou cannot insert text between lines easily. This is why planning before writing (Ch. 5) is non-negotiable for paper exams.
Header formattingYou must physically position the sender block (top-left), recipient block (below, right-aligned or left-aligned), and date line. Practice the visual layout on paper before the exam.

Computer-Based Exam

FactorImplication
Word countUsually displayed automatically — use it. Check it at the halfway point and at the end.
EditingYou can insert, delete, and rearrange. This is an advantage, but it can also lead to over-editing and wasted time.
FormattingYou may not have bold/italic/underline options. The Objet line may not be boldable. This is fine — the examiner knows it's computer-based.
Typing speedIf you type slowly in French (accents require special key combinations on non-French keyboards), practice before the exam. Know how to type: é, è, ê, ë, à, â, ù, û, ç, î, ï, ô, œ. Missing accents lose points under grammatical accuracy.
Spell-checkUsually disabled in the exam software. Do not rely on it.
Header formattingUse line breaks to simulate the visual layout. You will not have precise positioning control, but you can separate sections clearly.

8️⃣ WHAT COULD GO WRONG — BEFORE YOU EVEN START WRITING

The most dangerous mistakes happen before the first word of your letter. They happen during prompt reading and task interpretation.

Mistake 1: Misidentifying the Letter Type

What happens: You read the prompt quickly, see the word "problème," and assume it is a complaint. But the prompt actually asks you to propose solutions to a problem you've observed in your neighborhood — making it a suggestion letter, not a complaint. Your entire letter is now misdirected.

How to avoid: Read the prompt twice. The first time for general understanding. The second time specifically to answer: "What am I being asked to DO?" The verb in the consigne is the key: vous plaindre (complain), proposer (suggest), demander (request), répondre (respond), poser votre candidature (apply), exprimer votre opinion (give opinion).

Mistake 2: Ignoring One of the Required Points

What happens: The prompt lists three bullet points. You address two of them beautifully. You forget the third. The examiner deducts points under Respect de la consigne regardless of how good your French is.

How to avoid: During your planning phase (Ch. 5), number the required points and assign each one to a specific paragraph. After writing, check each point off.

Mistake 3: Writing an Essay Instead of a Letter

What happens: You write beautiful French paragraphs with a clear thesis and supporting arguments — but there is no header, no salutation, no closing formula, no signature. You have written an essay. The task asked for a letter. This is a structural failure.

How to avoid: Before writing your first content word, write the header, date, Objet line, and salutation. Before writing your conclusion, write the closing formula and signature. Build the frame before filling it.

Mistake 4: Spending Too Long on Planning, Not Enough on Writing

What happens: You spend 20 minutes creating a perfect plan. You now have 40 minutes for a letter that requires careful writing and editing. You rush. Errors multiply. The closing formula is incomplete or missing.

How to avoid: Planning takes 5 minutes maximum. It is a quick structural sketch, not a detailed outline. You will learn the exact method in Ch. 5.

Mistake 5: Starting to Write Without Knowing Your Closing Formula

What happens: You write a beautiful letter. You arrive at the end. You cannot remember a single closing formula. You write "Merci et bonne journée" — which is catastrophically informal for a formal letter. The closing formula is the last thing the examiner reads, and it is wrong.

How to avoid: Before you begin writing, decide on your closing formula and write it on your planning sheet. You will memorize a reliable set in Ch. 34. Having it ready before you start means you never face this panic.

9️⃣ B1 vs. B2 vs. C1 — HOW THE SAME TASK LOOKS AT THREE LEVELS

Understanding where B2 sits between B1 and C1 helps you understand exactly what the TEF expects. Here is the same prompt — a complaint about a late delivery — handled at three levels:

B1 Level Response (Insufficient for TEF Section B)

Bonjour,

J'ai commandé un produit sur votre site. Le produit est arrivé en retard. Il était cassé. Je suis pas content. Je veux un remboursement. Merci de me répondre vite.

Cordialement,
Pierre

What's wrong:

  • "Bonjour" is not a formal salutation
  • No header, no date, no Objet line
  • "Je suis pas content" — missing ne (register break), and childishly simple
  • "Je veux" — too direct, no conditional politeness
  • "Merci de me répondre vite" — informal and demanding
  • "Cordialement" alone — insufficient closing formula for formal letter
  • No paragraph structure — one block of text
  • Under 200 words
  • No connectors
  • No complex grammar

B2 Level Response (TEF Target)

[Header: Sender, Recipient, Date, formatted correctly]

Objet : Réclamation concernant la commande n° 4521 — produit endommagé

Madame, Monsieur,

Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de la commande n° 4521, passée le 15 mars dernier sur votre site Internet. L'appareil commandé devait être livré sous dix jours ouvrés ; or, je ne l'ai reçu qu'au bout de cinq semaines, soit avec un retard considérable.

De surcroît, à la réception du colis, j'ai constaté que l'appareil présentait plusieurs dommages visibles : la façade était fissurée et le bouton de mise en marche ne fonctionnait pas. J'ai tenté de joindre votre service client par téléphone à deux reprises, mais aucune solution satisfaisante ne m'a été proposée. Cette situation est d'autant plus décevante que j'étais, jusqu'à présent, un client fidèle de votre enseigne.

En conséquence, je vous saurais gré de bien vouloir procéder au remplacement de l'appareil défectueux dans les meilleurs délais ou, à défaut, au remboursement intégral de ma commande. Je reste à votre disposition pour tout renseignement complémentaire et vous prie de trouver ci-joint une copie de la facture correspondante.

Dans l'attente d'une réponse favorable de votre part, je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

[Signature]

What's right:

  • Complete header and formatting
  • Precise Objet line
  • Correct salutation for unknown recipient
  • Context established clearly in P1
  • Logical connectors (or, de surcroît, en conséquence)
  • Conditional politeness (je vous saurais gré de bien vouloir)
  • Complex grammar (passive voice: aucune solution ne m'a été proposée; restrictive: ne...qu'au bout de)
  • Firm but polite demands
  • Closing formula matches salutation perfectly
  • Approximately 230 words of content

C1 Level Response (Above TEF Target — Aspirational)

The C1 letter would include everything the B2 letter has, plus:

  • More sophisticated vocabulary (préjudice subi, manquement aux obligations contractuelles, mise en demeure)
  • Legal/contractual references (conformément à l'article L.217-4 du Code de la consommation)
  • Nuanced conditional structures (Il m'aurait semblé normal que votre service client eût pris les dispositions nécessaires...)
  • Elegant concession-refutation (Bien que je comprenne les contraintes logistiques auxquelles vous pouvez être confrontés, force est de constater que...)
  • Stylistic sophistication (varied sentence lengths, rhetorical rhythm)

💡 Your target is B2. You do not need to write at C1 level to score well on the TEF. But understanding what C1 looks like helps you see the direction of improvement. Every technique in this guide pushes you from B1 toward solid B2, with natural C1 elements appearing as your mastery deepens.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY — WHAT THIS CHAPTER TEACHES THAT NO OTHER CHAPTER COVERS

Chapter 1 is the only chapter that gives you the complete picture before you zoom in.

After this chapter, every subsequent chapter focuses on one specific element — a salutation, a paragraph, a grammar structure, a connector. You will go deep. But you must always remember what you learned here:

  1. The task is to write a functional French letter, not a French exercise. The letter must work as a real letter.
  2. There are exactly six letter types. Identify which one the prompt demands within 30 seconds.
  3. The prompt contains four extractable elements (who you are, who you write to, why, what to include). Extract all four before writing.
  4. The required points in the prompt are a checklist. Miss one, lose points. No exceptions.
  5. 200 words is the floor, not the target. Aim for 230–270.
  6. The letter has a physical structure (header → salutation → P1 → P2 → P3 → closing → signature). Missing any structural element is a visible failure.
  7. B2 is your target. It requires formal register, logical connectors, complex grammar, and complete structure. B1 is not enough. C1 is aspirational.

Everything you learn from Chapter 2 onward is a detailed instruction for one piece of this architecture. The architecture itself — the complete picture — lives here in Chapter 1.
