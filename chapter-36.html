📖 THE EXAM MOMENT

Imagine two candidates sitting in the same examination room. Both have written three strong body paragraphs. Both have used logical connectors effectively. Both have demonstrated B2-level grammar throughout their letters. Both have correctly formatted headers, dates, and Objet lines.

Candidate A writes a correct closing formula, signs properly, and submits.

Candidate B makes one critical error in the closing section — perhaps a mismatched salutation echo, perhaps a register collapse, perhaps a missing signature.

When the examiner reads both letters, the overall impression differs dramatically. Candidate A's letter feels complete, professional, controlled. Candidate B's letter feels almost right but flawed — and because the flaw appears at the very end, it is the last thing the examiner processes before assigning a score.

This is the recency effect in action (Ch. 4). The closing section is disproportionately powerful in shaping the examiner's final impression. A disaster here does not merely cost points on one criterion — it casts a retroactive shadow over the entire letter.

This chapter documents 15 specific closing disasters, each one observed in real candidate performances. For each disaster, you will see:

  • The error — exactly what the candidate wrote
  • Why it is wrong — the specific rule or convention it violates
  • What the examiner thinks — the impression it creates
  • The scoring impact — which criteria are affected and how severely
  • The correction — the exact fix
  • The prevention strategy — how to ensure you never make this error

🔴 DISASTER 1: THE MISSING CLOSING FORMULA

The Error

...Je vous demande donc de procéder au remboursement de ma commande dans les meilleurs délais.

Marie DUPONT

The letter jumps directly from the last argument sentence to the signature. No closing formula exists.

Why It Is Wrong

The closing formula is a structural requirement of a French formal letter — not a decorative option. Its absence is the epistolary equivalent of ending a formal meeting by standing up and walking out without saying goodbye. The letter is structurally incomplete.

What the Examiner Thinks

"The candidate either ran out of time, does not know that a closing formula is required, or does not know how to write one. In any case, the letter fails a fundamental convention of French formal correspondence."

Scoring Impact

CriterionImpact
Adaptation sociolinguistique🔴 Severe — the most visible sociolinguistic convention is absent
Respect de la consigne🔴 Severe — the letter is structurally incomplete as a formal letter
Cohérence et cohésion🟠 Moderate — the letter has no conclusion; it simply stops

The Correction

Add any complete closing formula. Even the simplest correct formula saves the letter:

Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Marie DUPONT

Prevention Strategy

From Ch. 35, Section 12: Always reserve 2 minutes at the end for the closing formula and signature. From Ch. 5: Write your closing formula on your planning sheet before you begin the letter. If you run out of time, the closing formula and signature are priority #1 and #2 — ahead of finishing Paragraph 3.

🔴 DISASTER 2: "CORDIALEMENT" AS THE ENTIRE CLOSING

The Error

...En espérant que vous pourrez résoudre ce problème rapidement.

Cordialement,
Pierre MARTIN

Why It Is Wrong

"Cordialement" is an email sign-off. It belongs in professional emails between colleagues — contexts where the full closing formula would be excessive. In a formal letter — which is what TEF Section B requires — it is catastrophically insufficient.

The word itself means "cordially" — a single adverb. It contains:

  • No verbal opening (Veuillez / Je vous prie)
  • No verb of acceptance (agréer / recevoir)
  • No salutation echo
  • No offering word (l'expression de)
  • No object (salutations / sentiments)

It is not a closing formula. It is the absence of one.

What the Examiner Thinks

"The candidate confuses email conventions with letter conventions. This is a fundamental sociolinguistic error that suggests the candidate has not prepared for the formal letter task."

Scoring Impact

CriterionImpact
Adaptation sociolinguistique🔴 Severe — demonstrates confusion between email and letter registers
Étendue du vocabulaire🟠 Moderate — the candidate's closing vocabulary is limited to one word

The Correction

Replace with any complete formula from Ch. 34:

Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Prevention Strategy

Internalize this absolute rule: "Cordialement" NEVER appears in a TEF formal letter. Not as a closing formula, not as part of a closing formula, not anywhere. If the word enters your mind during the exam, treat it as a warning signal that you are about to make a register error.

The Extended Family of Email Sign-Offs That Are Equally Wrong

Email Sign-OffStatus in Formal Letter
Cordialement❌ Insufficient
Bien cordialement❌ Insufficient
Bien à vous❌ Insufficient
Sincèrement❌ Insufficient
Respectueusement❌ Insufficient — this word does not even exist as a standard French letter closing
Salutations❌ Insufficient — a noun alone is not a formula
Meilleures salutations❌ Insufficient — still not a formula
Avec mes salutations❌ Insufficient — a prepositional phrase, not a sentence

Every single one of these is wrong for a TEF formal letter. They are all email conventions. None of them constitutes a closing formula.

🔴 DISASTER 3: THE MISMATCHED SALUTATION ECHO

The Error

Opening salutation: Monsieur le Directeur,

Closing formula:

Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Why It Is Wrong

The echo rule (Ch. 33, Component 3) requires the exact opening salutation to reappear inside the closing formula. The candidate opened with "Monsieur le Directeur" (a specific, titled male recipient) but closed with "Madame, Monsieur" (a generic, double-gendered address for unknown recipients).

This mismatch tells the examiner: "The candidate memorized a closing formula with 'Madame, Monsieur' and deployed it without checking whether it matches the opening."

What the Examiner Thinks

"The candidate has a template but does not adapt it to the specific letter. This is a coherence failure — the opening and closing do not agree."

Scoring Impact

CriterionImpact
Cohérence et cohésion🟠 Moderate — the structural frame (opening → closing) is broken
Adaptation sociolinguistique🟠 Moderate — the echo rule is a convention the candidate should know

The Correction

Veuillez agréer, Monsieur le Directeur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Prevention Strategy

After writing your closing formula, immediately look back at your opening salutation and verify that the echo matches exactly. This takes 5 seconds and prevents one of the most common closing errors.

All Possible Mismatch Patterns

🔴 DISASTER 4: THE PREPOSITION TRAP — À WITH AGRÉER

The Error

Veuillez agréer, Madame, à l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Why It Is Wrong

As established in Ch. 33, agréer is a direct transitive verb. It takes its object directly, without any preposition. The preposition à belongs with croire, not with agréer.

VerbConstructionCorrect
Agréeragréer + DIRECT OBJECTagréer l'expression de mes salutations ✅
Croirecroire + à + OBJECTcroire à l'expression de mes sentiments ✅

The candidate has contaminated the agréer formula with the croire preposition.

What the Examiner Thinks

"The candidate has seen both formula types and confused them. This is a grammatical error in the most visible sentence of the letter."

Scoring Impact

CriterionImpact
Correction grammaticale🟠 Moderate — incorrect preposition use with a transitive verb
Adaptation sociolinguistique🟡 Minor — the candidate knows a closing formula exists but executes it incorrectly

The Correction

Remove the à:

Veuillez agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Prevention Strategy

Memorize the verb-preposition pairings as a single unit:

  • agréer → NO preposition → agréer l'expression de...
  • croire → à → croire à l'expression de...

If you use agréer (which is the default — see Ch. 34), you will never need the preposition à. The simplest prevention is to always use agréer and never introduce à before l'expression de.

🔴 DISASTER 5: THE REVERSE PREPOSITION TRAP — MISSING À WITH CROIRE

The Error

Je vous prie de croire, Monsieur le Maire, l'expression de mes sentiments respectueux.

Why It Is Wrong

The exact inverse of Disaster 4. Croire requires the preposition à before its object. Without it, the sentence is grammatically incomplete — croire without à in this context means nothing coherent.

What the Examiner Thinks

"The candidate attempted a sophisticated formula (using croire) but made the critical grammatical error of omitting the required preposition."

Scoring Impact

CriterionImpact
Correction grammaticale🟠 Moderate — missing mandatory preposition

The Correction

Add à:

Je vous prie de croire, Monsieur le Maire, à l'expression de mes sentiments respectueux.

Prevention Strategy

If you are not 100% certain about the croire + à construction, do not use croire. Use agréer instead — it is simpler, safer, and universally correct. Croire is a Level 4 refinement (Ch. 34). The TEF does not require it. Formula 3A with agréer earns the same sociolinguistic points without the preposition risk.

🔴 DISASTER 6: THE HYBRID MONSTER — MERGING TWO FORMULA PATTERNS

The Error

Je vous prie de bien vouloir croire agréer, Madame, à l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Why It Is Wrong

The candidate has combined parts of two different formula patterns:

  • Pattern A: Je vous prie de bien vouloir agréer... l'expression de...
  • Pattern B: Je vous prie de croire... à l'expression de...

The result is a sentence with two verbs of acceptance (croire agréer) that is grammatically incoherent. It is the formula equivalent of saying "Please accept believe my greetings."

What the Examiner Thinks

"The candidate has memorized fragments of different formulas and is assembling them incorrectly. This suggests the candidate does not understand the formula's structure — they are guessing."

Scoring Impact

CriterionImpact
Correction grammaticale🔴 Severe — the sentence is ungrammatical
Adaptation sociolinguistique🟠 Moderate — the attempt to use a formula is visible, but the execution fails

The Correction

Choose ONE formula pattern and use it completely:

✅ Je vous prie de bien vouloir agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

OR:

✅ Je vous prie de croire, Madame, à l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Never both verbs in the same formula.

Prevention Strategy

This disaster occurs when candidates have studied multiple formulas without understanding the architecture (Ch. 33). The prevention is architectural understanding: each formula has ONE verb of acceptance. Choose it. Build the formula around it. Do not mix.

The safest prevention: Memorize only Formula 3A (Veuillez agréer... l'expression de mes salutations distinguées). One formula. One verb. No mixing possible.

🟠 DISASTER 7: THE ADJECTIVE AGREEMENT ERROR

The Error

Veuillez agréer, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distingués.

Why It Is Wrong

Salutations is a feminine plural noun. The adjective must agree: distinguées (feminine plural), not distingués (masculine plural).

NounGender/NumberCorrect AdjectiveWrong Adjective
salutationsFeminine pluraldistinguées❌ distingués
sentimentsMasculine pluraldistingués❌ distinguées
considérationFeminine singulardistinguée❌ distingué

What the Examiner Thinks

"A gender agreement error in the closing formula — the most formulaic and predictable sentence in the letter. If the candidate cannot get the agreement right in a memorized formula, it raises questions about their grammatical accuracy throughout."

Scoring Impact

CriterionImpact
Correction grammaticale🟠 Moderate — agreement error

This error is particularly damaging because it occurs in a fixed expression that should have been memorized correctly. Agreement errors in spontaneous writing are more forgivable than agreement errors in formulas.

The Correction

Veuillez agréer, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Prevention Strategy

When memorizing your formulas, memorize the noun + adjective as a single unit:

  • salutations distinguées — always together, always feminine plural
  • sentiments respectueux — always together, always masculine plural
  • sentiments distingués — always together, always masculine plural

Never memorize the adjective separately from the noun. They are a pair.

🟠 DISASTER 8: THE MISSING SALUTATION ECHO

The Error

Veuillez agréer l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Why It Is Wrong

The salutation echo is missing. There is no Madame, no Monsieur, no title between the verb and the object. The formula is grammatically functional but structurally incomplete — it lacks the social acknowledgment of the recipient that the echo provides (Ch. 33, Component 3).

What the Examiner Thinks

"The candidate knows a closing formula but has omitted the salutation echo. This could be a memory gap or a deliberate omission because they are unsure of the echo convention. Either way, the formula is incomplete."

Scoring Impact

CriterionImpact
Adaptation sociolinguistique🟠 Moderate — the convention is partially executed
Cohérence et cohésion🟡 Minor — the opening-closing frame is weakened

The Correction

Insert the salutation echo between commas:

Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Prevention Strategy

When writing your closing formula, follow this physical sequence:

  1. Write "Veuillez agréer,"
  2. STOP. Look at your opening salutation.
  3. Copy it exactly.
  4. Add a comma.
  5. Continue with "l'expression de mes salutations distinguées."

The physical act of stopping after the verb and looking back at the opening prevents this error.

🟠 DISASTER 9: REGISTER COLLAPSE — FORMAL LETTER, INFORMAL CLOSING

The Error

The candidate writes three paragraphs of formal French — correct register, appropriate vocabulary, complex grammar. Then:

Bon, j'espère que ça va s'arranger. Merci d'avance et à bientôt !

Marie

Why It Is Wrong

This is a complete register collapse. The entire letter was written at B2 formal level, and the closing drops to A2 informal level. The words "Bon" (filler), "ça" (informal pronoun), "Merci d'avance" (casual), "à bientôt" (conversational farewell), and the exclamation mark all belong to spoken or casual written French.

The first name alone (Marie) as a signature confirms the register failure.

What the Examiner Thinks

"The candidate maintained formal register through the body paragraphs — possibly through memorized phrases — but collapsed at the closing because they do not have internalized formal closing conventions. The formal body may have been template-driven rather than reflecting genuine register control."

Scoring Impact

CriterionImpact
Adaptation sociolinguistique🔴 Severe — the register inconsistency undermines the entire letter's sociolinguistic credibility
Cohérence et cohésion🟠 Moderate — tonal inconsistency between body and closing

This is one of the most damaging disasters because it retroactively damages the examiner's assessment of the body paragraphs. If the closing is informal, the examiner may reassess the formal body as memorized rather than mastered.

The Correction

Replace the entire closing section:

Je reste à votre disposition pour tout renseignement complémentaire.

Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Marie DUPONT

Prevention Strategy

Register is not a switch you turn on and off. From the first word to the last word, the register must be consistent. The closing section is where register fatigue hits hardest — you are tired, you are running out of time, your brain wants to "relax" into familiar informal patterns.

The antidote is memorization. Your closing section (pre-closing + closing formula + signature) should be so thoroughly memorized that you can write it on autopilot, even when your brain is fatigued. This is why Ch. 32, 33, and 34 emphasized memorizing a small number of formulas to perfection.

🟠 DISASTER 10: THE INCOMPLETE FORMULA — STOPPED MID-SENTENCE

The Error

Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes

The formula is cut off. The candidate ran out of time or space.

Why It Is Wrong

An incomplete closing formula is worse than no closing formula at all. A missing formula suggests the candidate forgot or did not know. An incomplete formula shows the candidate knew what to write but could not finish — suggesting poor time management.

What the Examiner Thinks

"The candidate clearly ran out of time. They knew a closing formula was needed but could not complete it. This indicates a planning failure."

Scoring Impact

CriterionImpact
Respect de la consigne🟠 Moderate — the formula is present but incomplete
Adaptation sociolinguistique🟡 Minor — the attempt is visible, which is better than nothing

The Correction

This error cannot be corrected after the fact — it is a time management failure.

Prevention Strategy

From Ch. 35, Section 12: Always write the closing formula BEFORE you write Paragraph 3. On paper, physically write the closing formula and signature at the bottom of the page first, then write your body paragraphs above it. This guarantees the closing is always complete, even if you run out of time on the body.

On computer: write the closing formula and signature immediately after the salutation, before writing the body. Then go back and insert the body paragraphs above it. The closing is always there, always complete.

🟠 DISASTER 11: THE DOUBLE CLOSING — TWO FORMULAS

The Error

En vous remerciant de l'attention que vous porterez à ma demande, je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Veuillez accepter, Madame, mes remerciements les plus sincères.

Marie DUPONT

Why It Is Wrong

The letter has two closing sentences that both attempt to function as closing formulas. The first is a correct formula. The second is an incorrect quasi-formula ("Veuillez accepter... mes remerciements" is not a standard closing formula — see Ch. 33, Section 5). Having two creates confusion: Which one is the closing? The letter does not know how to end.

What the Examiner Thinks

"The candidate was uncertain about their closing formula and wrote two, hoping one would be right. This suggests insecurity rather than competence."

Scoring Impact

CriterionImpact
Cohérence et cohésion🟠 Moderate — the letter has a redundant ending
Adaptation sociolinguistique🟡 Minor — one formula is correct, so the convention is partially met

The Correction

Keep only the first (correct) formula. Delete the second:

En vous remerciant de l'attention que vous porterez à ma demande, je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Marie DUPONT

Prevention Strategy

One letter = one closing formula. Never two. If you have a thank-you sentence, place it in the pre-closing paragraph (Ch. 32), not as a second closing formula.

🟠 DISASTER 12: THE CLOSING FORMULA SPLIT ACROSS TWO SENTENCES

The Error

Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur. L'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Marie DUPONT

Why It Is Wrong

The closing formula is one sentence. The candidate has split it into two by placing a period after "Monsieur" and starting a new sentence with "L'expression."

The result is:

  • Sentence 1: "Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur." — This means "Please accept, Madam, Sir." Accept what? The object is missing.
  • Sentence 2: "L'expression de mes salutations distinguées." — This is a sentence fragment (no verb).

Both sentences are individually incomplete. Together, they were meant to form one complete formula, but the period destroyed the grammatical unity.

What the Examiner Thinks

"The candidate does not understand that the closing formula is a single syntactic unit. They may have memorized it in fragments and assembled them incorrectly."

Scoring Impact

CriterionImpact
Correction grammaticale🟠 Moderate — the sentence structure is broken
Adaptation sociolinguistique🟠 Moderate — the formula is present but malformed

The Correction

Remove the period after "Monsieur" and replace it with a comma:

Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur*,** l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.*

Prevention Strategy

Understand the formula as one unbroken sentence. The only period is at the very end, after the final adjective. Practice writing the formula as a single flowing sentence, without pausing or breaking it.

🟡 DISASTER 13: USING "SENTIMENTS DÉVOUÉS" IN A COMPLAINT

The Error

Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes sentiments dévoués.

This closing formula appears at the end of a complaint letter about a defective product.

Why It Is Wrong

"Sentiments dévoués" means "devoted feelings" — it implies loyalty, allegiance, and service. Using this in a complaint letter creates a tonal contradiction: you have just spent three paragraphs expressing dissatisfaction, and now you are declaring your devotion to the party that wronged you.

The formula is grammatically correct and structurally complete. The error is contextual — the wrong formula for the wrong situation.

What the Examiner Thinks

"The candidate has memorized a closing formula without understanding what it means. They are using it as a template regardless of context."

Scoring Impact

CriterionImpact
Adaptation sociolinguistique🟡 Minor to moderate — the formula is real but misapplied

The Correction

Replace "sentiments dévoués" with "salutations distinguées":

Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Prevention Strategy

Refer to the letter-type matching table in Ch. 34, Section 3. Complaints always use salutations distinguées — neutral professional distance. Sentiments dévoués is reserved for letters to employers or patrons where a relationship of service exists.

🟡 DISASTER 14: THE "MERCI" ENDING

The Error

...Je vous demande donc de bien vouloir procéder au remboursement intégral.

Merci.

Pierre MARTIN

Why It Is Wrong

"Merci" is a conversational expression of gratitude. It is not a closing formula. It is not a pre-closing sentence. It is a single word that belongs in spoken French or casual written exchanges.

In the context of a formal letter, "Merci." as a standalone sentence is:

  • Registerally inappropriate (too informal)
  • Structurally insufficient (not a formula)
  • Tonally ambiguous (thanking someone who wronged you?)

What the Examiner Thinks

"The candidate does not know any closing formula and is falling back on the most basic expression of politeness they know. This is a B1-level closing in a task that requires B2."

Scoring Impact

CriterionImpact
Adaptation sociolinguistique🔴 Severe — fundamental convention failure

The Correction

Replace "Merci." with:

  1. A proper pre-closing: Je reste à votre disposition pour tout renseignement complémentaire.
  2. A proper closing formula: Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

How to Express Gratitude Formally

If you genuinely want to thank the recipient (e.g., in a request letter or cover letter), do it in the pre-closing paragraph, not as a standalone word:

Informal (Wrong)Formal (Correct)
❌ Merci.✅ Je vous remercie de l'attention que vous porterez à ma demande.
❌ Merci d'avance.✅ Je vous remercie par avance de votre compréhension.
❌ Merci beaucoup pour votre aide.✅ Je vous saurais gré de l'aide que vous pourriez m'apporter.
❌ Merci de me répondre vite.✅ Je vous serais reconnaissant(e) de bien vouloir me répondre dans les meilleurs délais.

The formal versions express gratitude while maintaining register. They belong in the pre-closing paragraph (Ch. 32), followed by a proper closing formula.

🟡 DISASTER 15: CONTENT AFTER THE SIGNATURE

The Error

Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Marie DUPONT

P.S. : J'oubliais de mentionner que j'ai également été surfacturé de 15 euros sur ma dernière commande.

Why It Is Wrong

A post-scriptum (P.S.) in a formal letter signals that the writer forgot something and is adding it as an afterthought. In personal correspondence, this is charming. In formal correspondence, it is a planning failure. It tells the reader: "I did not organize my thoughts before writing."

More critically, the P.S. introduces new factual content (a surcharge of 15 euros) that should have appeared in the body of the letter — specifically in Paragraph 2 (argument development, Ch. 20) or Paragraph 3 (demands, Ch. 26).

What the Examiner Thinks

"The candidate realized they forgot an important point after finishing the letter and added it as a P.S. This demonstrates poor planning. The content should have been integrated into the body paragraphs."

Scoring Impact

CriterionImpact
Cohérence et cohésion🟠 Moderate — the letter's structure is disrupted by an afterthought
Respect de la consigne🟡 Minor — the candidate is attempting to address a required point, just in the wrong place

The Correction

Remove the P.S. and integrate the content into the body:

In Paragraph 2:

De surcroît, j'ai constaté une surfacturation de 15 euros sur ma dernière commande, ce qui aggrave encore cette situation déjà inacceptable.

Prevention Strategy

Never use P.S. in a TEF letter. If you realize you forgot a point after writing the closing formula:

  • On computer: Go back and insert the point into the appropriate body paragraph.
  • On paper: If there is absolutely no way to insert it (no space, no time), leave it out. A letter without one required point but with a clean structure scores better than a letter with all required points but a P.S. that breaks the structure.

The Only Acceptable Post-Signature Element

The ONLY things that may appear after the signature are:

ElementAcceptable?Why
P.J. : [document list]✅ YesThis is a formatting element, not content
Copie à : [recipient list]✅ YesThis is a formatting element, not content
P.S. : [new content]❌ NoThis is new content in the wrong place
Additional sentences or arguments❌ NoAll content belongs in the body

🛡️ THE FIVE-BARRIER DEFENSE SYSTEM

To ensure none of these 15 disasters appears in your letter, implement these five barriers:

Barrier 1: Pre-Exam Memorization

Before exam day, memorize three closing formulas perfectly (Ch. 34, Section 5):

  • Formula 3A (default)
  • Formula 4A (deferential)
  • Formula 2A (semi-formal)

Write each one 15 times by hand. Test yourself from memory. If you can write them perfectly in your sleep, they will survive exam stress.

Barrier 2: Planning Phase Protection

During the 5-minute planning phase (Ch. 5), write your chosen closing formula on your planning sheet. This means it is decided before you write a single word of content. No last-minute scrambling.

Barrier 3: Physical Pre-Placement (Paper Exam)

On a paper-based exam, write your closing formula and signature at the bottom of the page BEFORE writing the body. This guarantees the closing section exists even if you run out of time.

Barrier 4: The Echo Check

After writing your closing formula, immediately look back at your opening salutation. Verify the echo matches character for character. This 5-second check prevents Disasters 3 and 8.

Barrier 5: The Final Scan

In the last minute before submitting, scan the closing section specifically:

CheckTimePrevents Disaster
"Is there a closing formula?"2 seconds#1, #2, #14
"Does the echo match?"3 seconds#3, #8
"Is there a signature?"2 seconds(Missing signature — not numbered but covered in Ch. 35)
"Is the formula one complete sentence?"3 seconds#10, #12
"Is there anything after the signature that shouldn't be there?"2 seconds#15

Total time for the final scan: 12 seconds. Twelve seconds that can save your letter from the most damaging errors in the entire exam.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY — WHAT THIS CHAPTER TEACHES THAT NO OTHER CHAPTER COVERS

Chapter 36 is the only chapter dedicated entirely to what goes wrong — not what goes right.

Every previous chapter in Block 6 (Ch. 32–35) taught you how to construct the closing section correctly. This chapter teaches you how to recognize and prevent the specific errors that candidates actually make. The difference is crucial: knowing the right answer is not the same as knowing all the wrong answers that your brain might produce under pressure.
