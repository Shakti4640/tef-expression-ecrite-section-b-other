📖 THE EXAM MOMENT

Your header is complete. Sender block, recipient block, date line, Objet line — all in place. The examiner's visual scan has registered a well-structured letter. The anchor is set to "competent" (Ch. 4).

Now you write the line that bridges formatting and content. The line that the examiner reads as the first voiced element of your letter — the first moment where they "hear" you speaking to the recipient. Everything before this was visual formatting. This is the moment your letter begins to speak.

The salutation.

In spoken French, you walk into a room and say "Bonjour." In a formal letter, you do not say "Bonjour." You say something very different — something that declares who the recipient is, what your relationship to them is, and what level of formality governs the entire exchange that follows.

The salutation is a contract. It commits you to a register level that must be maintained throughout the letter and must be echoed in the closing formula. Choose the wrong salutation and you have signed the wrong contract — every subsequent element is misaligned.

This chapter gives you the complete bank of salutations for every recipient type, the rules that govern their use, and the matching system that connects each salutation to its corresponding closing formula.

1️⃣ WHAT THE SALUTATION IS AND WHAT IT IS NOT

What It Is

The salutation (la formule d'appel) is the formal greeting that opens the body of the letter. It appears on its own line, followed by a comma, with one blank line before the first paragraph.

What It Is NOT

The Salutation Is NOT...Why Not
BonjourBonjour is an oral greeting. In written formal French, it is inappropriate as a letter salutation. It signals that the candidate does not distinguish between spoken and written conventions. (This was identified as Negative Trigger #2 in Ch. 4.)
SalutInformal/familiar — completely unacceptable in any formal or semi-formal letter
Cher Monsieur / Chère MadameThis is a common anglicism. In English, "Dear Sir/Madam" is standard. In French, Cher/Chère implies personal acquaintance and warmth. Using it with someone you do not know personally is inappropriate in most formal contexts. (Exception: see Section 6 below.)
Bonjour MonsieurA hybrid that combines oral greeting with written address. This is common in emails but is NOT standard in formal letters. For TEF purposes, avoid it.
À qui de droit"To whom it may concern" — this English convention has a French equivalent, but it is extremely impersonal and rarely used in French formal letters. It signals that you do not know who to address, which is a problem you should solve (Ch. 7), not advertise.

The Format

[Blank line after Objet]

[Salutation],

[Blank line]

[First paragraph begins]

Critical formatting rules:

RuleCorrectIncorrect
The salutation sits on its own lineMadame, Monsieur, [new line] [paragraph]Madame, Monsieur, je vous écris... (salutation merged with first sentence)
The salutation ends with a commaMadame, Monsieur*,***Madame, Monsieur (no comma) or Madame, Monsieur*.** (period)
One blank line follows the salutationMadame, Monsieur, [blank line] Je me permets...Madame, Monsieur, Je me permets... (no blank line — looks cramped)

2️⃣ WHY THE SALUTATION MATTERS — THE TRIPLE FUNCTION

The salutation performs three simultaneous functions:

Function 1: Identification

The salutation identifies the recipient by their social/professional status. It tells the examiner: "I know who I am writing to."

SalutationWhat It Identifies
Monsieur le Directeur,A male director — specific professional title acknowledged
Madame la Maire,A female mayor — political title acknowledged
Madame, Monsieur,Unknown individual(s) at an organization — gender not specified
Monsieur,A male individual — gender known, no specific title used

Function 2: Register Declaration

The salutation declares the formality level of the entire letter. It is the first register signal in the content (the header was formatting; this is language).

SalutationRegister Signal
Monsieur le Directeur,🔵 FORMAL — title used, maximum deference
Madame, Monsieur,🔵 FORMAL — standard formal for unknown recipient
Monsieur Dupont,🟢 SEMI-FORMAL — name used, respectful but less ceremonial
Bonjour,🔴 INFORMAL — oral greeting, breaks letter convention

Function 3: Closing Formula Commitment

The salutation commits you to a specific closing formula. Whatever term of address appears in the salutation MUST reappear in the closing formula. This is the echo rule introduced in Ch. 7 and fully detailed in Ch. 34.

SalutationClosing Formula Must Contain
Monsieur le Directeur,...agréer, Monsieur le Directeur, l'expression de...
Madame, Monsieur,...agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de...
Madame la Maire,...agréer, Madame la Maire, l'expression de...

🔴 This is the single most important rule about salutations: the salutation and the closing formula are a MATCHED PAIR. You cannot choose them independently. They are selected together as a system. This is why Step 4 of your planning method (Ch. 5) requires you to decide BOTH the salutation AND the closing formula before writing.

5️⃣ THE "MADAME, MONSIEUR," DEEP DIVE — THE UNIVERSAL DEFAULT

Because Madame, Monsieur, is the most frequently used salutation on the TEF, it deserves a dedicated section.

When to Use It

SituationUse Madame, Monsieur, ?
The prompt does not specify the recipient's gender✅ YES — always
The prompt names an organization but no individual✅ YES
The prompt says "Write to the customer service department"✅ YES
You are writing to a company in general✅ YES
The prompt gives a specific person's name and gender❌ NO — use Monsieur, or Madame, or the title
The prompt gives a specific title (director, mayor, editor)❌ NO — use the title-based salutation

Formatting Rules

Madame, Monsieur,
RuleExplanation
Madame firstModern convention places Madame before Monsieur (alphabetical order). The older convention placed Monsieur first. Both are acceptable, but Madame first is increasingly standard.
Comma betweenA comma separates Madame and Monsieur. This comma is mandatory.
Comma afterA final comma closes the salutation. This comma is mandatory.
No "et" betweenDo NOT write Madame et Monsieur. The conjunction et is not used in formal salutations.
Both capitalizedBoth words take initial capitals: Madame, Monsieur, — not madame, monsieur,

The Echo in the Closing Formula

When you use Madame, Monsieur, as your salutation, your closing formula must contain the same term:

Salutation: Madame, Monsieur,

[body of letter]

Closing: Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de 
         mes salutations distinguées.

The Madame, Monsieur appears in BOTH locations — this is the echo rule.

6️⃣ THE "CHER / CHÈRE" QUESTION — WHEN IS IT ACCEPTABLE?

The English Trap

In English, "Dear Sir" and "Dear Madam" are standard formal openings. English speakers naturally want to translate this as Cher Monsieur / Chère Madame. This translation is misleading.

What "Cher / Chère" Actually Means in French

In French, cher/chère means "dear" in the sense of personal affection or established relationship. It implies that you know the person, have a relationship with them, and feel warmth toward them.

FrenchEnglish TranslationImplication
Cher Monsieur,Dear Sir,"I know you personally and we have a warm professional relationship"
Chère Madame,Dear Madam,Same
Cher collègue,Dear colleague,"We work together and I consider you a peer"
Cher ami,Dear friend,Personal friendship — never in formal correspondence

When "Cher / Chère" Is Acceptable on the TEF

SituationAcceptable?Why
Writing to an unknown company director❌ NOYou do not know this person — cher implies familiarity that does not exist
Writing to a mayor you have never met❌ NOSame reason — no established relationship
Writing to a newspaper editor❌ NOYou are a reader, not a personal acquaintance
Writing to a teacher your child has known for years🟡 POSSIBLEThe prompt establishes an existing relationship; Cher Monsieur Dupont, could be semi-formal
Writing to a colleague about a workplace proposal🟡 POSSIBLEIf the prompt establishes a collegial relationship; Cher collègue, is semi-formal
Writing to a known association president with whom you collaborate🟡 POSSIBLEEstablished relationship; Chère Madame la Présidente, could work

The Safe Rule

🔴 When in doubt, do NOT use Cher/Chère. Use the title-based or name-based salutation instead. "Monsieur le Directeur," is always correct. "Cher Monsieur le Directeur," is only correct if you have a personal relationship with the director — and on the TEF, this is rarely established by the prompt.

The risk-reward calculation:

ChoiceRiskReward
Using Cher/Chère correctlyNone — shows nuanced register awarenessSlight SOC bonus for demonstrating semi-formal competence
Using Cher/Chère incorrectlySOC − for inappropriate familiarityNone
NOT using Cher/Chère (using title-based salutation instead)None — always correctSOC neutral — correct but not distinctive

The safe choice is always to avoid Cher/Chère unless the prompt explicitly establishes a warm, existing relationship. The potential penalty for misuse outweighs the potential benefit of correct use.

🔟 WHAT COULD GO WRONG — SALUTATION ERRORS

Error 1: Using "Bonjour" as a Salutation

How common: Common (15-25% of candidates)

Why it happens: In spoken French and informal emails, Bonjour is a universal greeting. Candidates assume it works in formal letters.

Why it's wrong: Bonjour is an oral greeting. It is not a written formal salutation. In French epistolary convention, the salutation must identify the recipient by title or form of address. Bonjour identifies no one.

Impact: SOC − (severe — signals fundamental unfamiliarity with letter conventions). This is Negative Trigger #2 from Ch. 4.

Fix: Replace Bonjour with the appropriate title-based or form-based salutation. Always.

❌ Wrong✅ Correct
Bonjour,Madame, Monsieur,
Bonjour Monsieur,Monsieur, or Monsieur le Directeur,
Bonjour Madame la Directrice,Madame la Directrice,

Error 2: Using "Cher/Chère" Inappropriately

How common: Common among English speakers (20-30%)

Why it happens: Direct translation of "Dear Sir/Madam."

Why it's wrong: See Section 6 above. Cher/Chère implies personal familiarity. Using it with a stranger is a register error.

Impact: SOC − (moderate — inappropriate familiarity).

Fix: Remove Cher/Chère unless the prompt establishes a personal relationship.

❌ Wrong (No Established Relationship)✅ Correct
Cher Monsieur le Directeur,Monsieur le Directeur,
Chère Madame, (to an unknown recipient)Madame,
Cher Monsieur Dupont, (first contact)Monsieur Dupont, or Monsieur,

Error 3: Including the First Name in the Salutation

How common: Occasional (10-15%)

What it looks like: Monsieur Bernard, or Madame Claire,

Why it's wrong: In French formal correspondence, first names are NEVER used in salutations. You use the title (Monsieur le Directeur), the last name (Monsieur Dupont), or the form of address alone (Monsieur). Using the first name signals informal familiarity — like calling a judge by their first name in court.

Impact: SOC − (register break — inappropriate familiarity)

Fix: Remove the first name. Use title, last name, or form of address only.

❌ Wrong✅ Correct
Monsieur Bernard,Monsieur, or Monsieur le Directeur, or Monsieur Lefèvre,
Madame Claire,Madame, or Madame la Directrice, or Madame Fontaine,

Error 4: Missing the Comma After the Salutation

How common: Very common (30-40%)

What it looks like: Madame, Monsieur

Why it matters: The comma after the salutation is mandatory. It signals the end of the salutation and the beginning of the letter body. Without it, the salutation looks incomplete.

Impact: Minor SOC/formatting issue. Not catastrophic, but noticeable.

Fix: Always end the salutation with a comma.

Error 5: Merging the Salutation with the First Sentence

What it looks like:

Madame, Monsieur, je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de...

Why it's wrong: The salutation is a standalone element. It sits on its own line, followed by a blank line, then the first paragraph begins. Merging them into one line makes the letter look like a single run-on block.

Impact: CC − (visual organization issue), SOC − (convention violation)

Fix: Salutation on its own line. Blank line. Then first paragraph.

Madame, Monsieur,

Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de...

Error 6: Mismatching the Salutation and the Recipient Block

What it looks like:

Recipient block: Monsieur le Directeur
Salutation: Madame, Monsieur,

Why it's wrong: The recipient block identifies a male director. The salutation uses the double form as if gender is unknown. This inconsistency suggests carelessness.

Impact: SOC − (consistency chain broken)

Fix: The salutation must match the recipient block. If the recipient block says Monsieur le Directeur, the salutation says Monsieur le Directeur,.

Error 7: Using "Mademoiselle"

What it looks like: Mademoiselle, or Chère Mademoiselle,

Why it's wrong: Mademoiselle is considered outdated and potentially discriminatory in modern French. Since 2012, French government communications use Madame for all women regardless of age or marital status. Many organizations and institutions have followed this guideline.

Impact: SOC − (outdated convention that may signal cultural unawareness)

Fix: Always use Madame. Never Mademoiselle.

Error 8: Using "À qui de droit"

What it looks like: À qui de droit,

Why it's wrong: This is the French equivalent of "To whom it may concern." While it exists in French, it is extremely impersonal and is almost never used in standard formal letters. It signals that the writer did not bother to identify the recipient — which on the TEF, where the prompt always tells you who to write to, suggests the candidate did not read the prompt carefully.

Impact: SOC − (inappropriately impersonal), RC − (suggests poor prompt reading)

Fix: Identify the recipient from the prompt and use the appropriate title-based salutation. If the prompt gives no specific person, use Madame, Monsieur,.

B1 Salutation

Problems: Oral greeting used as written salutation. No recipient identification. No register signal. SOC − (severe).

B2 Salutation

Monsieur le Directeur,

Correct: Title-based salutation. Identifies the recipient's function. Sets formal register. Matches the recipient block. Ready to be echoed in the closing formula. SOC ✅.

Monsieur le Directeur,

Identical to B2. Like the date line, the salutation does not change between B2 and C1. There is no "more sophisticated" salutation. Monsieur le Directeur is the correct form at every level above B1. What makes a letter C1 is the content and language that follows the salutation, not the salutation itself.

💡 The salutation is a pass/fail element. Either it is correct (and you get full SOC credit for it) or it is incorrect (and you lose SOC points). There is no gradient between "good salutation" and "great salutation." Invest 5 seconds in choosing the right one during planning (Ch. 5, Step 4), write it correctly, and move on to the first sentence — where the real content begins.
