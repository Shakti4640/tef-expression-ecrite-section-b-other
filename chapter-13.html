📖 THE EXAM MOMENT

Your first sentence is written. It identified your purpose, set your register, and launched the letter's content. Now you must complete Paragraph 1 — the paragraph that frames the entire letter.

Think of Paragraph 1 as the foundation of a building. The examiner cannot see the foundation once the building is finished — but without it, the building collapses. Without a solid Paragraph 1, your argument (P2) has no context, your request (P3) has no justification, and your closing has no logical anchor. The examiner reads P2 and thinks: "Why is this person complaining?" — because P1 did not establish the facts. They read P3 and think: "Why should I do what this person asks?" — because P1 did not establish the situation.

A strong Paragraph 1 answers every contextual question before it is asked. After reading P1, the examiner should think: "I understand the situation completely. Now I am ready to hear the argument."

This chapter teaches you the universal principles of Paragraph 1. Chapters 14-18 then apply these principles to each specific letter type.

1️⃣ THE THREE FUNCTIONS OF PARAGRAPH 1

Every Paragraph 1, regardless of letter type, must accomplish exactly three functions:

FunctionWhat It DoesThe Question It Answers for the Examiner
1. IDENTIFYEstablish who you are in relation to the situation"Who is writing this letter, and what is their standing?"
2. SITUATEDescribe the situation, event, or context that triggered the letter"What happened? What is the background?"
3. ANNOUNCEState or preview your purpose — why you are writing"What does this person want?"

Function 1: IDENTIFY — Who You Are

The examiner needs to know your role in the situation. Not your life story. Not your personal details. Your functional identity — the role that gives you the right to write this letter.

Letter TypeYour Functional IdentityExample
ComplaintCustomer, resident, user, subscriber, patient"En tant que cliente de votre société depuis trois ans..."
RequestStudent, employee, resident, member"Actuellement étudiant(e) en troisième année de licence..."
SuggestionResident, parent, citizen, employee, member"En tant que résidente du quartier Saint-Michel depuis dix ans..."
ResponseThe person who received the letter/offer/invitation"J'ai bien reçu votre courrier du 10 mars..."
Cover letterCandidate with specific qualifications"Titulaire d'un master en communication..."
OpinionReader, citizen, professional in the relevant field"Lecteur assidu de votre journal..."

💡 Your functional identity is NOT your personal identity. The examiner does not need to know your age, your family situation, or your hobbies (unless the prompt specifically makes these relevant). They need to know WHY you have standing to write this letter. A complaint from "a customer of three years" carries more weight than a complaint from "Sophie Martin." The role, not the name, establishes standing.

Function 2: SITUATE — What Happened / What Is the Context

The examiner needs the facts — the events, circumstances, or conditions that triggered the letter. This is the narrative foundation.

Letter TypeWhat to SituateExample
ComplaintThe event/problem: what happened, when, where, reference numbers"Le 15 mars dernier, j'ai passé commande d'un aspirateur sur votre site, sous la référence n° 4521."
RequestThe need or opportunity: what you want and what led you to this request"Votre programme de formation, dont j'ai pris connaissance sur votre site Internet, correspond parfaitement à mon projet professionnel."
SuggestionThe current situation you want to improve"Notre quartier, qui compte plus de cinq mille habitants, ne dispose actuellement d'aucun espace vert accessible."
ResponseWhat you received and from whom"Votre courrier du 10 mars, relatif aux nouvelles conditions de mon contrat de bail, a retenu toute mon attention."
Cover letterThe job posting and your relevant background"L'offre d'emploi, parue le 12 avril sur le site Indeed, concerne un poste qui correspond pleinement à mes compétences et à mon expérience."
OpinionThe article/event/issue that prompted your reaction"Votre article du 10 mars, consacré à la fermeture des commerces de proximité, soulève une question essentielle."

Function 3: ANNOUNCE — Why You Are Writing

The examiner needs to know your purpose — not in detail (that comes in P2 and P3), but in preview. A one-clause announcement that tells the reader where the letter is going.

Letter TypePurpose AnnouncementExample
ComplaintI am writing to report a problem and seek resolution"...ce qui m'amène à vous écrire afin de vous faire part de mon mécontentement et de solliciter une solution appropriée."
RequestI am writing to ask for something specific"...c'est pourquoi je me permets de solliciter votre aide à ce sujet."
SuggestionI am writing to propose an improvement"...ce constat m'amène à vous soumettre une proposition susceptible d'améliorer cette situation."
ResponseI am writing to respond to what you sent me"...et je souhaiterais vous faire part de ma position à ce sujet."
Cover letterI am applying for this position"...c'est la raison pour laquelle je souhaiterais vous présenter ma candidature."
OpinionI am writing to share my perspective"...et je souhaiterais apporter ma contribution à ce débat."

⚠️ Function 3 (ANNOUNCE) is often embedded in the first sentence (Ch. 11) rather than being a separate sentence. If your first sentence already announced your purpose (e.g., "Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de..."), you do not need to announce it again. In this case, P1 focuses on Functions 1 (IDENTIFY) and 2 (SITUATE) after the first sentence.

At 50 words, P1 occupies approximately 20-25% of a 230-word letter. This is the right proportion — enough to establish context without dominating the letter.

🔴 A common mistake is writing a 100-word P1 that tells the entire story in detail, leaving only 100 words for P2 and P3 combined. This produces an unbalanced letter: rich context, thin argument, skeletal request. The examiner notices the imbalance under CC. Keep P1 focused and concise. Details go in P2.

3️⃣ THE TONE-SETTING FUNCTION — HOW P1 DETERMINES THE EXAMINER'S IMPRESSION

The "Tone Lock" Phenomenon

The tone of Paragraph 1 creates a tonal expectation for the rest of the letter. Whatever emotional register P1 establishes, the examiner expects to find the same register in P2, P3, and the closing. This is the "tone lock."

P1 ToneExaminer's ExpectationWhat Happens If the Tone Shifts
Measured, factual, formal"This will be a professional, well-reasoned letter"If P2 suddenly becomes emotional or aggressive, the shift is jarring. CC drops. SOC drops.
Emotional, frustrated, informal"This person is upset. The letter may be disorganized."If P2 suddenly becomes calm and formal, the shift is confusing — but at least it's an improvement. The damage from P1's tone remains, however.
Collaborative, constructive, warm"This is a suggestion or request. The person is being positive."If P3 suddenly becomes demanding or threatening, the tone shift undermines the collaborative spirit.

The Lesson

Set the right tone in P1 and maintain it throughout. The right tone for each letter type:

Letter TypeP1 ToneWhat It Sounds Like
ComplaintMeasured displeasure — factual description with controlled disappointment"...cette situation est d'autant plus regrettable que..." — Not angry. Disappointed. Controlled.
RequestRespectful initiative — polite explanation of why you need something"...je me permets de solliciter..." — Not demanding. Requesting. Justified.
SuggestionConstructive engagement — positive framing of a problem you want to solve"...je souhaiterais vous soumettre une proposition susceptible de..." — Not criticizing. Proposing.
ResponseDiplomatic consideration — acknowledging what you received, signaling your position"...j'ai pris connaissance avec intérêt de votre proposition..." — Not rushed. Thoughtful.
Cover letterProfessional confidence — clear statement of candidacy and qualification"...mon parcours professionnel me semble correspondre aux exigences de ce poste..." — Not arrogant. Confident.
OpinionEngaged reflection — thoughtful positioning on an issue"...cette question me semble mériter une réflexion approfondie..." — Not aggressive. Reflective.

4️⃣ THE "CONTEXT CONTRACT" — WHAT P1 PROMISES THE EXAMINER

When the examiner finishes reading P1, they have unconsciously formed a contract — a set of expectations about what the rest of the letter will contain. This contract is based on what P1 established.

The Contract Terms

What P1 EstablishesWhat the Examiner Expects Next
"I am a customer who received a damaged product"P2 will describe the damage and its impact. P3 will request a refund or replacement.
"I am a parent concerned about school music programs being cut"P2 will argue why music education matters. P3 will propose alternatives.
"I am a resident writing to the mayor about the lack of green spaces"P2 will explain the benefits of green spaces. P3 will propose a specific project.
"I saw your job posting and I am qualified"P2 will present specific qualifications. P3 will express motivation and availability.

Why the Contract Matters for CC

If your letter fulfills the contract — if P2 and P3 deliver what P1 promised — the examiner perceives coherence. The letter feels unified, purposeful, and well-organized. CC score rises.

If your letter breaks the contract — if P2 goes in an unexpected direction or P3 doesn't follow from P2 — the examiner perceives incoherence. The letter feels scattered, disjointed, or confused. CC score drops.

💡 Think of P1 as a movie trailer. It previews the main content without giving everything away. The audience (examiner) forms expectations. The movie (P2 + P3) must deliver on those expectations. If the trailer promises an action movie and the movie is a romantic comedy, the audience is confused. If the trailer promises an action movie and the movie delivers action, the audience is satisfied.

5️⃣ SENTENCE 2 — THE CONTEXT EXPANSION SENTENCE

Your first sentence (Ch. 11) has identified your purpose and set your register. Sentence 2 of Paragraph 1 expands the context with specific facts.

What "Specific Facts" Means

Specific facts are concrete, verifiable details drawn from the prompt. They include:

Type of FactExampleWhy It Matters
Dates"le 15 mars dernier", "depuis le mois de janvier"Establishes timeline — when the problem started or when the event occurred
Reference numbers"commande n° 4521", "contrat n° AB-7890"Shows attention to detail and connects to real administrative records
Quantities/Amounts"un montant de 250 euros", "trois tentatives de contact"Adds precision and credibility
Locations"dans votre établissement du 15, rue de la Paix", "dans les parties communes de l'immeuble"Grounds the situation in a specific place
People involved"votre service client", "les résidents de l'immeuble", "l'agent d'accueil"Identifies the actors in the situation
Previous actions"malgré trois appels téléphoniques", "à la suite de notre entretien du 10 mars"Shows that you have already attempted to resolve the situation

The Sentence 2 Template

[Specific fact 1] + [Specific fact 2] + [Connector or relative clause linking them]

Examples:

ScenarioSentence 2
Late delivery complaintEn effet, l'article commandé, dont la livraison était prévue sous dix jours ouvrés, ne m'est parvenu qu'au bout de cinq semaines, soit un retard de plus de trois semaines.
Hotel complaintLors de notre arrivée le 15 mars, nous avons constaté que la chambre réservée ne correspondait en aucun point à la description figurant sur votre site Internet.
Building maintenanceDepuis le mois de janvier dernier, l'ascenseur de notre immeuble est hors service et, malgré les signalements répétés adressés à votre agence par les résidents, aucune intervention n'a été effectuée à ce jour.
School music programJ'ai en effet appris que la direction de l'établissement envisageait de supprimer l'ensemble des cours de musique à compter de la rentrée prochaine, en raison de contraintes budgétaires.
Job applicationCe poste, qui allie créativité et compétences rédactionnelles, correspond parfaitement à mon profil et à l'expérience que j'ai acquise au cours de mes trois années en agence de communication.

Grammar Focus: The Tenses of Sentence 2

Sentence 2 is where narrative tenses appear. The tense choices signal your grammatical competence to the examiner.

TenseWhen to Use in P1ExampleWhy
Passé composéFor completed past events — things that happened at a specific time"J'ai commandé un produit le 15 mars." / "Nous avons constaté que..."Narrates the facts — what happened
ImparfaitFor background conditions — things that were ongoing or descriptive"La livraison était prévue sous dix jours." / "La chambre ne correspondait pas à la description."Describes the context — what the situation was like
PresentFor current situations — things that are still happening"L'ascenseur est hors service depuis deux mois." / "Je suis actuellement étudiant(e)."Describes the present reality
Plus-que-parfaitFor events that happened before the main past event"L'article que j'avais commandé n'est jamais arrivé."Establishes what happened earlier — shows temporal sophistication
Passif (passive voice)For what was done to you — shifts focus from actor to action"Aucune réponse ne m'a été apportée." / "Le produit m'a été livré endommagé."Formal register + avoids direct accusation

💡 The passé composé / imparfait distinction is one of the most important grammar signals in P1. Using them correctly shows the examiner that you understand French narrative tense system. Using only passé composé (no imparfait) is a B1 limitation. Using both correctly is a B2 marker.

Correct PC/Imparfait interplay in P1:

J'ai passé commande [PC — completed action] d'un aspirateur sur votre site le 15 mars. L'article, dont la livraison était prévue [IMP — background condition] sous dix jours, ne m'est parvenu [PC — completed result] qu'au bout de cinq semaines.

The examiner reads this and registers: "Correct tense usage — passé composé for events, imparfait for conditions. B2 grammar."

6️⃣ SENTENCE 3 (OPTIONAL) — THE TRANSITIONAL ASSESSMENT

Not every P1 needs a third sentence. But when the context requires a brief assessment or transition toward P2, a third sentence is valuable.

When to Include Sentence 3

SituationInclude Sentence 3?What It Does

Sentence 3 Templates

The Assessment Sentence — briefly evaluates the situation:

TemplateExample

The Transition Sentence — bridges toward P2:

TemplateExample

⚠️ Sentence 3 is a TOOL, not a requirement. If your P1 flows naturally into P2 with just 2 sentences, do not add a third sentence for the sake of length. A 40-word P1 with 2 strong sentences is better than a 65-word P1 with a third sentence that adds nothing.

7️⃣ THE SPECIFICITY PRINCIPLE — WHY VAGUE P1s LOSE POINTS

The Vagueness Problem

Many candidates write P1s that are technically correct but fatally vague:

Je vous écris parce que j'ai un problème avec un produit que j'ai acheté chez vous. Le produit ne fonctionne pas bien et je ne suis pas satisfait.

This P1 is:

  • Grammatically acceptable
  • Topically relevant
  • Registered at courant level (not quite formal, not informal)

But it is completely vague. The examiner reads it and knows only that "there is a problem with a product." They do not know:

  • What product
  • When it was purchased
  • What the problem is specifically
  • What "ne fonctionne pas bien" means
  • What order number or reference applies

The Specificity Solution

The same P1 rewritten with specifics:

Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de la commande n° 4521, passée le 15 mars dernier sur votre site Internet, portant sur un aspirateur de marque CleanPro. À la réception du colis, j'ai constaté que l'appareil présentait une fissure sur la façade avant et que le bouton de mise en marche ne fonctionnait pas.

This P1 is:

  • Specific: product (aspirateur CleanPro), date (15 mars), reference (n° 4521), problems (fissure + bouton)
  • Formal register: je me permets de, portant sur, à la réception, j'ai constaté, présentait
  • Well-structured: first sentence = purpose + reference; second sentence = specific problems

The Specificity Rule

Every fact mentioned in the prompt should appear in your letter, and most of them belong in P1.

Prompt FactWhere It GoesWhy
DatesP1 — Sentence 2Establishes timeline
Reference numbersP1 — Sentence 1 or 2Shows precision
Specific details about the problemP1 — Sentence 2Grounds the complaint in reality
Names of people/companiesP1 — Sentence 1 (or Objet line)Identifies the parties
Previous attempts to resolveP1 — Sentence 2 or 3Shows you've already tried
Consequences and impactP2 — NOT P1P1 is for facts, P2 is for impact
Your request/demandP3 — NOT P1P1 is for context, P3 is for action

8️⃣ THE "BEFORE AND AFTER" — WEAK P1 vs. STRONG P1

Example 1: Complaint About a Hotel Stay

BEFORE (Weak P1):

Je vous écris pour vous dire que j'ai séjourné dans votre hôtel et que ça s'est mal passé. La chambre n'était pas bien et le service était mauvais. Je suis déçu.

Problems:

  • "Pour vous dire" — informal
  • "Ça s'est mal passé" — ça is informal (Ch. 3); "mal passé" is vague
  • "N'était pas bien" — vague; what does "not good" mean?
  • "Le service était mauvais" — vague; which service? How?
  • "Je suis déçu" — emotion before specifics; basic vocabulary
  • No dates, no room number, no reservation reference
  • Three sentences but all vague — no information density

AFTER (Strong P1):

Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet du séjour que j'ai effectué dans votre établissement du 15 au 18 mars dernier, sous la réservation n° H-2025-0893. Contrairement à la description figurant sur votre site Internet, la chambre qui m'a été attribuée ne disposait ni de la vue sur mer annoncée, ni d'une climatisation fonctionnelle, ce qui a considérablement affecté la qualité de mon séjour.

What changed:

  • "Je me permets de" — formal opener ✅
  • "Du 15 au 18 mars dernier" — specific dates ✅
  • "Sous la réservation n° H-2025-0893" — reference number ✅
  • "Contrairement à la description" — formal comparison structure ✅
  • "Ne disposait ni de... ni de..." — formal double negation ✅
  • "Climatisation fonctionnelle" — specific and formal vocabulary ✅
  • "Considérablement affecté" — measured assessment, not emotional outburst ✅

Example 2: Request for Schedule Change

BEFORE (Weak P1):

Bonjour, je travaille chez vous et j'aimerais changer mes horaires parce que c'est difficile pour moi en ce moment.

Problems:

  • "Bonjour" — not a salutation (Mistake #1 from Ch. 12)
  • "Je travaille chez vous" — vague; what department? What role? Since when?
  • "J'aimerais changer" — basic; no conditional sophistication
  • "C'est difficile pour moi" — vague; what is difficult? Why?
  • "En ce moment" — informal time marker
  • No formal register markers at all

AFTER (Strong P1):

Employée au sein du service comptabilité depuis septembre 2022, je me permets de vous adresser la présente demande d'aménagement de mes horaires de travail. En effet, ma situation familiale a récemment évolué, m'imposant de nouvelles contraintes qui rendent difficilement conciliables mes obligations professionnelles et personnelles.

What changed:

  • "Employée au sein du service comptabilité depuis septembre 2022" — specific role, department, and duration ✅
  • "La présente demande" — elevated formal vocabulary ✅
  • "Aménagement de mes horaires" — precise formal term ✅
  • "En effet" — formal connector ✅
  • "Ma situation familiale a récemment évolué" — formal, measured explanation ✅
  • "M'imposant de nouvelles contraintes" — participial construction, formal vocabulary ✅
  • "Difficilement conciliables" — sophisticated adjective ✅
  • "Obligations professionnelles et personnelles" — formal register ✅

9️⃣ THE P1 → P2 TRANSITION — HOW TO END P1 SO THAT P2 BEGINS NATURALLY

The Problem of the "Hard Cut"

Many candidates end P1 abruptly and begin P2 with no connection:

P1: ...l'appareil présentait de nombreux dommages à la réception.

P2: L'appareil avait une fissure sur la façade.

This is a "hard cut" — P2 starts without any connector or transition. The examiner must infer the relationship between the paragraphs. Cognitive fluency drops (Ch. 4). CC score drops.

The Solution: The Transitional Bridge

The last sentence of P1 should contain a forward-looking element that anticipates P2's content. OR the first sentence of P2 should contain a backward-looking connector that references P1. Ideally, both.

Strategy 1: P1's last sentence previews P2

P1 ending: ...ce qui a considérablement affecté la qualité de mon séjour, comme je souhaiterais vous l'exposer ci-après.

P2 beginning: En premier lieu, la chambre ne disposait pas de...

The phrase "comme je souhaiterais vous l'exposer ci-après" previews that details are coming in P2.

Strategy 2: P2's first sentence references P1

P1 ending: ...l'appareil présentait de nombreux dommages à la réception.

P2 beginning: De surcroît, les conséquences de cette situation ne se limitent pas au seul état du produit.

The connector "De surcroît" links P2 to P1 by signaling addition.

Strategy 3: P1 ends with an assessment that naturally leads to P2's development

P1 ending: Cette situation est d'autant plus préoccupante qu'elle affecte l'ensemble des résidents de l'immeuble.

P2 beginning: En effet, les conséquences de cette panne sont multiples et touchent particulièrement les personnes âgées.

The assessment "d'autant plus préoccupante" sets up P2's detailed explanation of consequences.

Connectors That Bridge P1 → P2

ConnectorFunctionExample at Start of P2
En effet,Elaboration — confirms and expands on P1's claim"En effet, les conséquences de cette situation sont considérables."
De surcroît, / En outre, / De plus,Addition — adds new information to what P1 established"De surcroît, cette panne a des répercussions importantes sur..."
Or,Contrast/complication — introduces a problem or unexpected element"Or, malgré nos signalements répétés, aucune mesure n'a été prise."
Cependant, / Toutefois,Concession — acknowledges something before arguing"Toutefois, je tiens à souligner que cette situation..."
Force est de constater queAssessment — formal way to introduce an observation"Force est de constater que cette situation a des conséquences..."
C'est pourquoi / C'est la raison pour laquelleConsequence — explains why P2's argument follows from P1's facts"C'est pourquoi je souhaiterais vous exposer les raisons de..."

💡 The full connector system is taught in Ch. 25. For now, memorize 2-3 connectors that work at the P1 → P2 junction. The most versatile: En effet (for elaboration), De surcroît (for addition), and Or (for introducing a problem or complication).

1️⃣1️⃣ THE ROLE OF P1 IN THE THREE-PARAGRAPH SYSTEM

P1 does not exist in isolation. It is the first third of a three-paragraph argumentative system:

P1: CONTEXT (This is what happened / This is the situation)
      │
      ↓ The examiner now understands the background
      │
P2: ARGUMENT (This is why it matters / This is my reasoning)
      │
      ↓ The examiner now understands your position
      │
P3: ACTION (This is what I want / This is my proposal)
      │
      ↓ The examiner now knows your desired outcome

Each paragraph has one function. P1's function is exclusively context. It does not argue. It does not request. It does not propose. It establishes the factual foundation on which P2's argument and P3's action will be built.

What Does NOT Belong in P1

ElementWhere It BelongsWhy NOT in P1
Detailed description of consequences/impactP2P1 establishes what happened; P2 explains why it matters
Your emotional reaction (developed)P2P1 mentions the situation; P2 explores its impact on you
Your specific demand/requestP3P1 sets the stage; P3 delivers the action
Your proposed solutionP3P1 describes the problem; P3 proposes the fix
Evidence and examples supporting your argumentP2P1 provides facts; P2 analyzes them
Deadline or escalation warningP3P1 introduces the situation; P3 sets expectations

🔴 The most common P1 error is putting EVERYTHING in P1 — context, argument, and request all in one paragraph. This leaves nothing for P2 and P3. The result: one bloated paragraph followed by two skeletal paragraphs (or no P3 at all). The examiner sees an unbalanced letter. CC drops. RC may drop if required points are underdeveloped.

1️⃣2️⃣ B1 vs. B2 vs. C1 — PARAGRAPH 1 AT THREE LEVELS

Same Scenario: Complaint About a Broken Elevator

B1 Paragraph 1:

Je vous écris parce que l'ascenseur de mon immeuble est cassé depuis longtemps et personne ne fait rien. C'est un gros problème pour moi.

Analysis:

  • "Parce que" — basic connector
  • "Est cassé" — basic vocabulary (cassé is informal; en panne / hors service is formal)
  • "Depuis longtemps" — vague (how long exactly?)
  • "Personne ne fait rien" — informal phrasing
  • "C'est un gros problème" — B1 vocabulary (gros problème is childish in formal context)
  • No dates, no specifics, no standing established
  • GRAM: basic tenses only, no complex structures
  • SOC: courant register, not formal

B2 Paragraph 1:

En tant que résidante de l'immeuble situé au 18, avenue Jean-Jaurès depuis septembre 2020, je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de la panne d'ascenseur qui affecte notre immeuble depuis maintenant deux mois. Malgré les signalements répétés adressés à votre agence par plusieurs résidents, dont un courrier recommandé envoyé le 15 février dernier, aucune intervention n'a été effectuée à ce jour.

Analysis:

  • "En tant que résidante" — establishes standing ✅
  • "Situé au 18, avenue Jean-Jaurès" — specific address ✅
  • "Depuis septembre 2020" — specific duration ✅
  • "Je me permets de" — formal opener ✅
  • "La panne d'ascenseur" — precise formal vocabulary ✅
  • "Depuis maintenant deux mois" — specific timeline ✅
  • "Malgré les signalements répétés" — formal + shows previous action ✅
  • "Dont un courrier recommandé" — formal relative clause + specific detail ✅
  • "Aucune intervention n'a été effectuée" — formal negation (aucune...ne) + passive voice ✅
  • Tenses: present (affecte) for ongoing situation, PC (envoyé, effectuée) for completed actions ✅

C1 Paragraph 1:

Résidant au 18, avenue Jean-Jaurès depuis bientôt cinq ans, je me permets de porter à votre attention une situation devenue particulièrement préoccupante : l'ascenseur de notre immeuble, dont vous assurez la gestion, est hors service depuis le 15 janvier dernier, soit plus de deux mois à ce jour. En dépit de multiples signalements — tant oraux qu'écrits, le dernier en date étant un courrier recommandé avec accusé de réception adressé à vos services le 15 février — aucune mesure corrective n'a été entreprise, laissant les résidents dans une situation de plus en plus difficile.

1️⃣3️⃣ THE PARAGRAPH 1 WRITING ROUTINE — TIMED EXECUTION

On exam day, P1 should take approximately 8-10 minutes to write (including the first sentence from Ch. 11). Here is the execution routine:

MINUTE 1: Write the first sentence
  → From your planning sheet (Ch. 5, Step 4)
  → Formal opener + purpose identification
  → ~15-20 words

MINUTES 2-4: Write Sentence 2 (context expansion)
  → Add specific facts from the prompt (dates, references, details)
  → Use appropriate tenses (PC for events, IMP for conditions)
  → ~20-30 words

MINUTES 5-6: Write Sentence 3 (if needed)
  → Assessment or transition toward P2
  → ~10-15 words

MINUTES 7-8: Review P1
  → Check the 10-point quality checklist (Section 10 above)
  → Verify register consistency
  → Verify tense accuracy
  → Estimate word count (~40-60 words)

P1 is complete. Begin P2.

1️⃣4️⃣ WHAT COULD GO WRONG — P1-SPECIFIC ERRORS

Error 1: The "Everything Paragraph"

What it looks like: P1 contains context, argument, emotional reaction, demands, and closing sentiments — all in one 150-word paragraph. P2 and P3 barely exist.

Why it happens: The candidate did not plan (Ch. 5) and wrote everything that came to mind in the first paragraph.

Impact: CC − (no paragraph structure), RC − (required points may be addressed but not developed)

Fix: During planning, assign ONE function to each paragraph: P1 = context, P2 = argument, P3 = action. Stick to the plan.

Error 2: The "Empty Context"

What it looks like: P1 exists but contains no specific information:

Je vous écris au sujet d'un problème que j'ai rencontré récemment avec votre entreprise.

Why it happens: The candidate is being "safe" by staying vague, or has not read the prompt carefully enough to extract specifics.

Impact: RC − (prompt details not incorporated), VOC − (no demonstration of precise vocabulary), CC − (P2 has no factual foundation to build on)

Fix: Extract specific facts during Step 1 of planning (Ch. 5). Include them in P1's Sentence 2.

Error 3: The "Accusatory Opening"

What it looks like:

Vous m'avez vendu un produit défectueux et vous refusez de me rembourser. C'est inacceptable.

Why it happens: The candidate is emotionally engaged with the complaint scenario and writes from a place of anger.

Impact: SOC − (aggressive tone inappropriate for formal letter), CC − (argument before context)

Fix: Replace direct accusations ("Vous m'avez vendu") with factual descriptions using passive voice or impersonal constructions ("Le produit livré s'est avéré défectueux" / "Aucune solution ne m'a été proposée"). Describe what happened, not who is to blame. The blame is implicit in the facts.

Error 4: The "Off-Topic Context"

What it looks like:

Je me permets de vous écrire au sujet de votre service client. J'habite à Toulouse depuis dix ans et j'aime beaucoup cette ville. Ma famille et moi sommes des consommateurs responsables qui valorisons la qualité.

Why it happens: The candidate is padding P1 with irrelevant personal information to meet the word count.

Impact: RC − (off-topic content), CC − (irrelevant information disrupts coherence)

Fix: Every sentence in P1 must serve one of the three functions (IDENTIFY, SITUATE, ANNOUNCE). If a sentence does not serve any of these functions, it does not belong in P1. "I like Toulouse" and "my family values quality" serve none of them.
