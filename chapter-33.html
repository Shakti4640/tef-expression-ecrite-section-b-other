📖 THE EXAM MOMENT

You have written a strong letter. Your header is correct (Ch. 6–9). Your salutation is precise (Ch. 10). Your first sentence hooked the reader (Ch. 11). Your three body paragraphs established context, built an argument, and proposed a solution (Ch. 13–30). Your pre-closing paragraph bridged gracefully toward the end (Ch. 32).

Now you arrive at the final sentence. The closing formula. The formule de politesse.

And this is where something strange happens. Many candidates — even those who have written beautifully up to this point — suddenly panic. They know they need a closing formula, but they cannot remember the exact wording. They know it involves words like agréer and salutations and distinguées, but the pieces are jumbled. So they write something like:

❌ Veuillez recevoir mes salutations les plus agréées.

Or:

❌ Je vous prie d'accepter l'assurance de mes sentiments les plus distingués.

Or, in desperation:

❌ Cordialement.

Each of these is wrong. Not slightly wrong — structurally, lexically, or registerally wrong in ways that an examiner recognizes instantly. And because the closing formula is the last thing the examiner reads, these errors cast a shadow backward over the entire letter.

This chapter ensures that never happens to you. Not by asking you to memorize formulas blindly (that comes in Ch. 34), but by teaching you how the closing formula works as a system — so that even if your memory falters under exam pressure, you can reconstruct a correct formula from understanding.

1️⃣ WHY THE CLOSING FORMULA EXISTS — A CULTURAL KEY

The Question You Should Be Asking

If you come from an English-speaking background, your instinct for ending a letter is:

Sincerely,
Best regards,
Yours faithfully,

One or two words. Simple. Quick. Done.

Then you encounter French closing formulas and see:

Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame la Directrice, l'expression de mes sentiments distingués.

And your first reaction is: Why? Why is this so elaborate? Why can't I just write "Cordialement"?

The answer lies in French communicative culture, and understanding this culture is not optional — it is the foundation of the entire formula system.

The Principle: Writing Is a Social Performance

In Francophone formal culture, a letter is not merely a transmission of information. It is a social act — an interaction between two people of defined social positions. The closing formula exists to acknowledge the social relationship between the writer and the recipient.

When you write "Je vous prie d'agréer, Monsieur le Directeur, l'expression de mes sentiments respectueux," you are literally saying:

"I ask you to accept, Director Sir, the expression of my respectful feelings."

This is not empty ceremony. Each word performs a function:

Word/PhraseSocial Function
Je vous prie"I request of you" — positions you as the one making a respectful request
d'agréer"to accept" — positions the recipient as the one who graciously receives
Monsieur le DirecteurAcknowledges the recipient's title and status — you see them and respect their position
l'expression de"the expression of" — you are offering something intangible
mes sentiments respectueux"my respectful feelings" — you define the nature of your regard for the recipient

Every element is a social signal. The formula says: "I know who you are, I know who I am in relation to you, and I am conducting myself accordingly."

Why This Matters for the TEF

The TEF evaluates sociolinguistic appropriateness (adaptation sociolinguistique) as a distinct criterion. This criterion asks: "Does the candidate demonstrate awareness of French social conventions in written communication?"

The closing formula is where this criterion is most visibly tested. A correct, well-matched closing formula tells the examiner: "This candidate understands French formal communication culture." An incorrect, absent, or inappropriately casual closing tells the examiner: "This candidate has linguistic ability but lacks cultural competence."

💡 The closing formula is not about politeness — it is about competence. The examiner does not care whether you are personally polite. They care whether you can perform the social conventions that Francophone professional and administrative life requires. The closing formula is proof of that performance.

2️⃣ THE ANATOMY OF A FRENCH CLOSING FORMULA

Every French formal closing formula follows a fixed grammatical architecture. Understanding this architecture means you can analyze any formula you encounter and verify whether one you construct is correct.

The Complete Architecture

A full closing formula contains five components arranged in a fixed order:

[COMPONENT 1] + [COMPONENT 2] + [COMPONENT 3] + [COMPONENT 4] + [COMPONENT 5]
   Verbal         Verb of         Salutation       Offering        Object of
   opening        acceptance      echo             word            offering

Let us examine each component with the model formula:

Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Component 1: The Verbal Opening

What it is: The phrase that begins the formula — always a first-person construction directed at the recipient.

Options:

FrenchLiteral MeaningFormalityNotes
Je vous prie de...I beg/request you to...⬛⬛⬛⬛ Very formalThe most common formal opening. "Prier" in this context means "to request" — not "to pray."
Veuillez...Please (be willing to)...⬛⬛⬛⬛ Very formalImperative of "vouloir." Equally formal as "Je vous prie de." Some consider it slightly more direct.
Je vous prie de bien vouloir...I request you to kindly...⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛ Maximum formalityCombines "prier" and "vouloir" — double politeness. The most deferential option.
Je vous saurai gré de bien vouloir...I will be grateful to you for kindly...⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛ Maximum formalityEven more deferential — adds gratitude. Less common in closing formulas; more common in the body for requests (Ch. 27).

What determines your choice:

If your letter is...Use...Because...
A complaintJe vous prie d'agréer or Veuillez agréerYou are the aggrieved party — standard formality is sufficient
A request to a high-ranking officialJe vous prie de bien vouloir agréerYou are asking for something — maximum deference is appropriate
A cover letterJe vous prie d'agréer or Veuillez agréerStandard professional formality
A letter to a peer or semi-formal recipientVeuillez agréerSlightly more direct, appropriate for lateral relationships

⚠️ Critical distinction: "Je vous prie de" vs. "Veuillez"

These are interchangeable in formality level, but they differ grammatically:

  • Je vous prie de + infinitive → Je vous prie d'agréer...
  • Veuillez + infinitive → Veuillez agréer...

Both are correct. Both are formal. The choice is stylistic, not hierarchical. For TEF, either works. Pick one and use it consistently.

Component 2: The Verb of Acceptance

What it is: The infinitive verb that describes what you are asking the recipient to do with your regards/feelings.

Options:

FrenchLiteral MeaningFormalityNotes
agréerto accept (with pleasure, formally)⬛⬛⬛⬛ Very formalThe standard verb for formal closing formulas. This is your default.
recevoirto receive⬛⬛⬛ FormalSlightly less formal than agréer. Appropriate for semi-formal letters.
accepterto accept⬛⬛ Semi-formalLess formal than agréer. Acceptable for semi-formal, but avoid in very formal letters.
croire à/ento believe in⬛⬛⬛⬛ Very formalUsed specifically with "l'assurance de" — see Component 4. Different grammatical construction.

🔴 THE MOST IMPORTANT RULE IN THIS CHAPTER:

Agréer and croire do NOT take the same objects.

VerbTakes...Example
Agréerl'expression de + [sentiments/salutations]...agréer l'expression de mes salutations distinguées ✅
Agréerl'assurance de + [sentiments]...agréer l'assurance de mes sentiments respectueux ✅
Croireà l'expression de or à l'assurance de + [sentiments]...croire à l'expression de mes sentiments distingués ✅
Croirel'expression de (without à)❌ INCORRECT — croire requires the preposition à
Agréerà l'expression de (with à)❌ INCORRECT — agréer is a direct transitive verb, no preposition

This is the single most common grammatical error in French closing formulas. Mixing the preposition rules of agréer and croire produces a formula that is immediately recognizable as wrong to any French reader — and to any TEF examiner.

Component 3: The Salutation Echo

What it is: The repetition of the salutation you used at the beginning of the letter, inserted into the middle of the closing formula between commas.

The Rule:

Whatever you wrote as your opening salutation (Ch. 10) must reappear exactly inside the closing formula.

If you opened with...Your closing must contain...
Madame,...agréer, Madame, l'expression de...
Monsieur,...agréer, Monsieur, l'expression de...
Madame, Monsieur,...agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de...
Monsieur le Directeur,...agréer, Monsieur le Directeur, l'expression de...
Madame la Maire,...agréer, Madame la Maire, l'expression de...
Monsieur le Rédacteur en chef,...agréer, Monsieur le Rédacteur en chef, l'expression de...

Why this rule exists:

The echo creates a frame — the letter opens and closes with the same acknowledgment of the recipient. It signals structural awareness, social consistency, and attention to detail. Breaking the echo (opening with "Madame la Directrice" but closing with just "Madame") is a coherence error.

⚠️ Common mistake: Forgetting to include the salutation echo entirely.

❌ Veuillez agréer l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

This is incomplete. The salutation echo is missing. It should be:

✅ Veuillez agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

The echo is not optional. Its absence is one of the most visible errors in a closing formula.

Component 4: The Offering Word

What it is: The noun that describes what you are "offering" to the recipient — what they are being asked to agréer or croire à.

Options:

FrenchLiteral MeaningFormalityUsed With
l'expression dethe expression of⬛⬛⬛⬛ Very formalBoth agréer (direct) and croire (with à)
l'assurance dethe assurance of⬛⬛⬛⬛⬛ Maximum formalityBoth agréer (direct) and croire (with à)

The Difference Between "l'expression de" and "l'assurance de"

This distinction confuses many learners, but it follows a clear logic:

Offering WordWhat It SignalsWhen to Use
l'expression de"I am expressing my feelings/regards to you" — you are communicating themDefault choice. Appropriate in virtually all formal letters. Use this when in doubt.
l'assurance de"I am assuring you of my feelings/regards" — you are guaranteeing themElevated choice. Used when writing to someone of significantly higher status, or when you want to convey particular deference. Implies a stronger commitment to the sentiment.

Practical guidance for TEF:

Use "l'expression de" in every letter. It is universally correct. "L'assurance de" is a refinement — knowing it exists and when it applies is C1-level awareness. Using "l'expression de" consistently will never lose you points.

💡 The hierarchy:

  • L'assurance de > L'expression de (in formality)
  • L'assurance de mes sentiments respectueux = more deferential than L'expression de mes sentiments respectueux
  • Both are correct. The difference is degree, not correctness.

Component 5: The Object of Offering

What it is: The noun phrase that describes the nature of your regards — what you are expressing or assuring.

This is the final word cluster of the formula, and it carries the tonal weight of the entire closing.

The Two Families:

French closing formulas use one of two noun families: salutations or sentiments. These are NOT interchangeable.

FamilyFrenchLiteral MeaningWhat It Signals
Salutationsmes salutations distinguéesmy distinguished greetingsNeutral respect. You acknowledge the recipient with appropriate formality. No personal warmth. Professional distance.
Salutationsmes salutations les plus distinguéesmy most distinguished greetingsHeightened respect. Same as above but intensified with "les plus."
Sentimentsmes sentiments distinguésmy distinguished feelingsWarmer respect. Sentiments implies a degree of personal regard — you are not just greeting, you are feeling.
Sentimentsmes sentiments respectueuxmy respectful feelingsDeference. Used when writing to someone of higher status. The respect is explicitly stated.
Sentimentsmes sentiments les plus respectueuxmy most respectful feelingsMaximum deference. The strongest form of respect short of "ma haute considération."
Sentimentsmes sentiments les meilleursmy best feelingsWarm but formal. Used in semi-formal contexts where personal warmth is appropriate.
Sentimentsmes sentiments dévouésmy devoted feelingsLoyal deference. Implies allegiance or service — used toward employers or patrons.
Considérationma considération distinguéemy distinguished considerationHigh formality. Used in diplomatic or highly official correspondence.
Considérationma haute considérationmy high considerationMaximum official formality. Reserved for heads of state, ambassadors, very senior officials. Rarely needed at TEF level.

The Salutations vs. Sentiments Decision

This is the most nuanced choice in the closing formula. Here is the decision framework:

Use salutations when...Use sentiments when...
The recipient is unknown to youThe recipient is known to you (even by name only)
The relationship is purely transactionalThe relationship has a personal dimension
You are writing to a company/institution (not a person)You are writing to a specific person
You want maximum professional distanceYou want to convey personal regard
The letter is a complaint (maintaining distance is strategic)The letter is a cover letter (personal warmth is strategic)

🔴 Gender rule for sentiments:

Traditional French convention holds that a man writing to a woman should NOT use "sentiments" in the closing formula — the word was historically considered to carry romantic connotations. In this case, "salutations" or "considération" should be used instead.

Modern practice: This rule has softened considerably, and many contemporary style guides no longer enforce it. However, because the TEF examiner may follow either traditional or modern conventions, the safest approach is:

Writer → RecipientRecommended Object
Man → Womansalutations distinguées or considération distinguée
Man → Mansentiments distingués or salutations distinguées
Woman → Mansentiments distingués or salutations distinguées
Woman → Womansalutations distinguées or sentiments distingués
Unknown gender → Unknown gendersalutations distinguées (always safe)

For TEF, "salutations distinguées" is the universal safe choice. It works for every gender combination, every letter type, and every level of formality. If you use nothing else, use this.

4️⃣ THE FORMALITY HIERARCHY — FIVE LEVELS

Now that you understand the components, here is the complete hierarchy from most formal to least formal. Each level is built by adjusting the components.

Level 5: Maximum Formality (Diplomatic / Official)

Je vous prie de bien vouloir agréer, [Salutation Echo], l'assurance de ma haute considération.

When: Letters to ministers, ambassadors, heads of institutions. Almost never needed at TEF level, but knowing it exists demonstrates mastery.

Components: Double-politeness opening + agréer + l'assurance de + haute considération

Level 4: High Formality (Standard Formal Letter)

Je vous prie d'agréer, [Salutation Echo], l'expression de mes sentiments [respectueux / distingués].

When: Complaints to directors, requests to officials, cover letters for important positions.

Components: Standard opening + agréer + l'expression de + sentiments with adjective

Level 3: Standard Formality (Default TEF Level)

Veuillez agréer, [Salutation Echo], l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

When: Most TEF letters. This is your default.

Components: Imperative opening + agréer + l'expression de + salutations distinguées

Level 2: Moderate Formality (Semi-Formal)

Veuillez recevoir, [Salutation Echo], mes salutations distinguées.

When: Semi-formal letters (to a teacher you know, a colleague in another department, a community association).

Components: Imperative opening + recevoir (less formal verb) + direct object (no l'expression de)

Level 1: Low Formality (Semi-Formal Minimum)

Veuillez agréer, [Salutation Echo], mes sincères salutations.

or

Recevez, [Salutation Echo], mes cordiales salutations.

When: Semi-formal letters where the relationship is relatively close but still professional.

Components: Simplified opening + simplified object

5️⃣ WHY YOU CANNOT INVENT YOUR OWN CLOSING FORMULA

The Temptation

Under exam pressure, candidates who cannot remember a complete formula sometimes try to construct one from general French:

❌ Je vous remercie et vous envoie mes meilleurs vœux de bonne continuation.

❌ Avec tout mon respect, je reste en attente de votre réponse.

❌ Merci beaucoup pour votre attention. Respectueusement.

These are not closing formulas. They are sentences that attempt to function as closing formulas — and they fail.

Why They Fail

French closing formulas are fixed expressions (expressions figées). Like idioms, they exist as complete units. You cannot substitute individual words or rearrange the structure without breaking the expression.

Consider the English parallel: "How do you do?" is a fixed greeting. You cannot say "How do you perform?" or "In what manner do you do?" and expect it to function the same way. The words are fixed. The structure is fixed. Deviation signals that you do not know the convention.

French closing formulas work the same way. The verbs (agréer, recevoir, croire), the offering words (l'expression de, l'assurance de), the objects (sentiments, salutations, considération), and the adjectives (distingué, respectueux, dévoué) are a closed set. You select from the set. You do not create new entries.

What Happens If You Invent

Invented FormulaWhat's WrongExaminer's Reaction
Veuillez accepter mes remerciements les plus sincères."Accepter" is not a standard verb of acceptance in closing formulas; "remerciements" is not a standard object. This reads as a thank-you sentence, not a closing formula."The candidate doesn't know closing formulas."
Agréez mes sentiments les plus hauts."Agréez" (imperative) is grammatically possible but never used — the imperative of agréer is always preceded by "Veuillez." "Les plus hauts" is not a standard adjective for sentiments."The candidate is guessing."
Avec mes salutations respectueuses.This is a phrase fragment, not a sentence. No verb, no subject. It reads like a gift tag, not a letter closing."The candidate has seen closing formulas but doesn't understand the structure."
Bien à vous.This is an email sign-off, not a formal letter closing formula. Appropriate for casual professional emails. Completely insufficient for a TEF formal letter."Register failure."
Cordialement.Same as above. One word. Appropriate for emails between colleagues. Catastrophically insufficient for a formal letter."The candidate does not know the difference between email and letter conventions."

🔴 Absolute rule: A TEF formal letter MUST end with a complete closing formula containing (at minimum) a verbal opening, a verb of acceptance, a salutation echo, and an object. Anything less is incomplete.

8️⃣ THE COMPLETE FORMULA FLOW — FROM OPENING TO CLOSING

To fully understand the closing formula, you must see it in the context of the entire letter's "politeness frame." The opening salutation and the closing formula form a matched pair — they open and close the social interaction.

The Matching Principle

Opening SalutationMinimum Acceptable ClosingRecommended Closing
Madame,Veuillez agréer, Madame, mes salutations distinguées.Veuillez agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.
Monsieur,Veuillez agréer, Monsieur, mes salutations distinguées.Je vous prie d'agréer, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.
Madame, Monsieur,Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, mes salutations distinguées.Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.
Monsieur le Directeur,Veuillez agréer, Monsieur le Directeur, mes salutations distinguées.Je vous prie d'agréer, Monsieur le Directeur, l'expression de mes sentiments distingués.
Madame la Directrice,Veuillez agréer, Madame la Directrice, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.Je vous prie d'agréer, Madame la Directrice, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.
Madame la Maire,Veuillez agréer, Madame la Maire, l'expression de mes sentiments respectueux.Je vous prie de croire, Madame la Maire, à l'expression de mes sentiments respectueux.

💡 The golden rule of matching: The salutation echo inside the closing formula must be a character-for-character copy of the opening salutation. If you opened with "Monsieur le Directeur," your closing must contain "Monsieur le Directeur" — not "Monsieur," not "Monsieur le directeur" (lowercase d), not "M. le Directeur."

This matching system will be presented as a complete reference table in Ch. 34. For now, understand the principle: opening and closing are paired, and the echo is mandatory.

9️⃣ WHAT COULD GO WRONG — FORMULA CONSTRUCTION ERRORS

Error 1: The Preposition Trap (agréer vs. croire)

❌ Veuillez agréer, Madame, à l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Why it's wrong: Agréer is a direct transitive verb — it takes a direct object without a preposition. The à does not belong.

✅ Veuillez agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

❌ Je vous prie de croire, Monsieur, l'expression de mes sentiments distingués.

Why it's wrong: Croire requires the preposition à before its object.

✅ Je vous prie de croire, Monsieur, à l'expression de mes sentiments distingués.

Error 2: The Missing Echo

❌ Veuillez agréer l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Why it's wrong: No salutation echo. The recipient has disappeared from the farewell.

✅ Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Error 3: The Mismatched Echo

Opening: Madame la Directrice,
Closing: ❌ Veuillez agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Why it's wrong: The opening used the full title "Madame la Directrice." The closing truncated it to "Madame." The echo is broken.

✅ Veuillez agréer, Madame la Directrice, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Error 4: The Hybrid Monster

❌ Veuillez croire agréer, Monsieur, à l'expression de mes sentiments distingués.

Why it's wrong: Two verbs of acceptance (croire and agréer) jammed together. The candidate is confusing two different formula patterns and merging them.

Fix: Choose ONE verb. Either:

✅ Veuillez agréer, Monsieur, l'expression de mes sentiments distingués.
✅ Je vous prie de croire, Monsieur, à l'expression de mes sentiments distingués.

Error 5: The Adjective Agreement Error

❌ ...l'expression de mes salutations distingués.

Why it's wrong: Salutations is feminine plural. The adjective must be distinguées (feminine plural), not distingués (masculine plural).

✅ ...l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Error 6: The Incomplete Formula

❌ Veuillez agréer mes salutations.

Why it's wrong: Missing the salutation echo. Missing the adjective. Missing "l'expression de." This is a skeleton formula — structurally present but stripped of its essential components.

✅ Veuillez agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Error 7: The Register Collapse

❌ Cordialement, Marie Dupont

Why it's wrong: "Cordialement" is an email sign-off. In a formal letter — which is what TEF Section B requires — it is catastrophically insufficient. It signals either ignorance of letter conventions or indifference to them. Either way, it costs significant points under adaptation sociolinguistique.

✅ Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Marie Dupont

🔟 THE CLOSING FORMULA AS PUNCTUATION — COMMA RULES

The closing formula contains mandatory commas that are part of its fixed structure. Getting them wrong alters the meaning or creates a grammatical error.

Comma Map

*Je vous prie d'agréer , Madame , Monsieur , l'expression de mes salutations distinguées .

Comma PositionWhy It's There
After agréerSeparates the verb from the vocative (salutation echo) — the echo is an apposition
After MadameSeparates the two elements of a double salutation
After MonsieurCloses the vocative apposition before the direct object begins
Period at endCloses the sentence — the entire formula is one sentence

For Single Salutation

*Veuillez agréer , Monsieur le Directeur , l'expression de mes sentiments distingués .

Two commas surround the salutation echo, functioning like parenthetical commas around an apposition.

Common Comma Errors

ErrorProblem
❌ Veuillez agréer Madame l'expression de...Missing commas around salutation echo — reads as if "Madame" is the direct object of "agréer"
❌ Veuillez agréer, Madame Monsieur, l'expression de...Missing comma between "Madame" and "Monsieur" in double salutation
❌ Veuillez agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées,Trailing comma instead of period — the formula must end with a period

  • Precise matching of formality level to recipient status
  • Examiner assessment: sociolinguistic mastery; candidate understands the hierarchy

💡 Your target is B2. A complete, correct formula with "Veuillez agréer" or "Je vous prie d'agréer," a matching salutation echo, "l'expression de," and "mes salutations distinguées" will earn you full marks on this element. The C1 refinements are valuable but not required.

1️⃣2️⃣ THE EMERGENCY FORMULA — WHAT TO WRITE IF YOUR MIND GOES BLANK

Despite all preparation, exam stress can cause memory blanks. If you arrive at the closing and cannot remember which formula you planned, here is your emergency protocol:

The One Formula That Always Works

Veuillez agréer, [exact copy of your opening salutation], l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

This formula is:

  • ✅ Grammatically correct
  • ✅ Appropriate for every formal letter type
  • ✅ Appropriate for every recipient type
  • ✅ Appropriate for every gender combination
  • ✅ Level 3 formality — the TEF default
  • ✅ Contains all five components
  • ✅ Has the salutation echo
  • ✅ Uses the universally safe "salutations distinguées"

Memorize this formula as your absolute baseline. Even if you learn nothing else from this chapter, this one formula, correctly deployed, will earn you the closing formula points on any TEF Section B letter.

How to Memorize It

Break it into rhythm:

Veuillez agréer          [pause — insert salutation echo]
l'expression de          [pause — this is always the same]
mes salutations          [pause — almost there]
distinguées.             [done]

Practice writing it 10 times by hand. Then 10 more times from memory. Then practice inserting different salutation echoes:

  • Veuillez agréer, Madame, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.
  • Veuillez agréer, Monsieur le Directeur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.
  • Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.
  • Veuillez agréer, Madame la Maire, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

The frame never changes. Only the echo changes. This is the power of the system.

1️⃣4️⃣ THE CLOSING FORMULA AND THE PRE-CLOSING — INTEGRATION

Recall from Ch. 32 that the pre-closing can either be separate from or fused with the closing formula. Here is how the two integration options look in practice:

Option A: Fused (Pre-closing flows into closing formula)

Dans l'attente de votre réponse, je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Anatomy:

  • "Dans l'attente de votre réponse" → pre-closing Element A (Ch. 32)
  • "je vous prie d'agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées" → closing formula

Advantage: Elegant, concise, demonstrates syntactic mastery
Risk: Long sentence; if any part is wrong, the entire combined structure fails

Option B: Separated (Pre-closing is its own paragraph)

Je reste à votre disposition pour tout renseignement complémentaire. Dans l'attente d'une réponse favorable de votre part, je vous remercie de l'attention que vous porterez à ma demande.

Veuillez agréer, Madame, Monsieur, l'expression de mes salutations distinguées.

Anatomy:

  • First paragraph: pre-closing (Elements B + A from Ch. 32)
  • Second paragraph: closing formula (standalone)

Advantage: Clear, safe, each element is independent
Risk: Slightly less fluid — but this is a minor aesthetic issue, not a scoring issue

💡 Recommendation for TEF: Use Option B (separated). It is safer, clearer, and easier to execute under pressure. The closing formula stands alone as the final sentence, which means if you make an error in the pre-closing, it does not contaminate the closing formula, and vice versa.
