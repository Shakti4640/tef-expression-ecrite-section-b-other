📖 THE EXAM MOMENT

You open the exam booklet. You turn to Section B. Inside a bordered box, you read the following prompt:

1️⃣ THE PROMPT

Situation :
Vous avez réservé une chambre d'hôtel pour quatre nuits (du 10 au 14 février) dans l'hôtel Le Grand Vallon, à Annecy, pour célébrer votre anniversaire de mariage. À votre arrivée, on vous a attribué une chambre donnant sur le parking au lieu de la chambre avec vue sur le lac que vous aviez réservée. De plus, la climatisation ne fonctionnait pas et le petit-déjeuner inclus dans votre réservation n'a pas été servi le premier matin. Malgré vos réclamations à la réception, aucune solution satisfaisante ne vous a été proposée durant votre séjour.

Consigne :
Vous écrivez une lettre au directeur de l'hôtel Le Grand Vallon pour vous plaindre de cette situation. Dans votre lettre, vous devez :

  • rappeler les circonstances de votre réservation et les problèmes rencontrés ;
  • exprimer votre mécontentement face à la gestion de la situation par le personnel ;
  • demander une compensation précise.

Rédigez une lettre formelle d'au moins 200 mots.

🔟 THE LETTER'S EMOTIONAL ARCHITECTURE

Beyond grammar and vocabulary, the letter has an emotional trajectory — a deliberate movement from calm to firm to diplomatic:

SENTENCE 1:  Calm, formal, contextual     → "I am writing to inform you..."
SENTENCE 2:  Nostalgic, personal           → "We had reserved for our anniversary..."
SENTENCE 3:  Shocked, factual              → "However, upon arrival..."
SENTENCE 4:  Escalating, accumulating      → "Furthermore, the AC was broken..."
SENTENCE 5:  Continued escalation          → "Moreover, breakfast was not served..."
SENTENCE 6:  Frustrated, controlled        → "Despite my complaints, nothing was done..."
SENTENCE 7:  Peak dissatisfaction          → "This indifference is all the more disappointing..."
SENTENCE 8:  Firm, precise, diplomatic     → "Consequently, I would be grateful for a refund..."
SENTENCE 9:  Clear, final demand           → "I would also like a written apology..."
SENTENCE 10: Professional, open            → "I remain at your disposal..."
SENTENCE 11: Forward-looking, hopeful      → "I hope we can find a favorable outcome..."

The trajectory is: context → shock → escalation → peak frustration → firm demands → openness → hope

This emotional architecture is not accidental — it mirrors the structure taught across Blocks 3, 4, and 5. The context paragraph (Block 3) is calm. The argument paragraph (Block 4) escalates. The solution paragraph (Block 5) is firm but diplomatic. The pre-closing (Block 6) is professional and forward-looking.

The examiner does not consciously analyze this trajectory, but they feel it. A well-architected emotional flow makes the letter persuasive, coherent, and professional. A letter that starts angry and stays angry, or starts calm and never builds, feels flat or uncontrolled.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY — WHAT THIS CHAPTER TEACHES THAT NO OTHER CHAPTER COVERS

Chapter 37 is the first chapter where everything comes together.

Every previous chapter taught one element in isolation — a salutation, a connector, a grammar structure, a paragraph strategy. This chapter shows how all elements interact simultaneously in a real letter responding to a real prompt.

The key lessons unique to this chapter:

  1. Planning determines quality. The 5-minute planning phase (Section 2) made every subsequent writing decision faster and more confident. Without planning, the letter would be disorganized regardless of the candidate's French ability.

  2. Every word earns its place. The annotation reveals that no word in the letter is random — every vocabulary choice, every connector, every tense >choice signals something specific to the examiner. The difference between "établissement" and "hôtel," between "époux" and "mari," between "désagréments" and "problèmes" — each substitution moves the letter from B1 toward B2.

    1. Connectors are architectural, not decorative. The nine connectors in this letter are not sprinkled randomly — each one performs a specific structural function (opposition, addition, consequence, comparison). Removing any one of them would break a logical link between ideas.

    2. The emotional trajectory matters. The letter moves from calm context through escalating frustration to firm-but-diplomatic demands to professional openness. This trajectory is not instinctive — it is engineered through paragraph structure and vocabulary choice.

    3. The closing section is not an afterthought. The pre-closing, closing formula, signature, and P.J. line together comprise approximately 60 words — nearly 25% of the letter. They were planned before the first content word was written, ensuring they would be present and correct regardless of time pressure.

    4. Grammar structures cluster by paragraph. Plus-que-parfait and imparfait dominate P1 (past context). Passé composé and passive voice dominate P2 (completed events). Conditional dominates P3 (diplomatic demands). This clustering is natural — each paragraph's communicative function calls for specific tenses.

    5. The prompt is a checklist, and every box must be checked. The three required points from the prompt are mapped to specific paragraphs in the planning phase and verified after writing. Missing a required point is the single most preventable error on the TEF — and the planning method makes it nearly impossible.
